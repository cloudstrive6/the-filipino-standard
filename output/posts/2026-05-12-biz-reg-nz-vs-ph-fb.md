## POST BODY

In New Zealand, registering a company takes about an hour.

In the Philippines, it takes weeks.

I've done both.

When I registered my NZ company, I sat at my kitchen table with a coffee. Opened the Companies Office portal. Typed in the company name, the share structure, the registered office, the director details. Paid roughly NZD $130 with a debit card. Hit submit.

The certificate of incorporation landed in my inbox before the coffee was cold.

One form. One portal. One hour.

Now here's the same process, in the Philippines, the way every Filipino entrepreneur I know has had to do it.

First, you choose between DTI (sole proprietorship) or SEC (corporation). That's already a different building, different forms, different waiting times. Then BIR, for your TIN and your authority to print receipts. Then your barangay clearance. Then your mayor's permit, which requires a sanitary permit, a fire safety inspection, sometimes a locational clearance, and depending on the LGU, an electrical or structural inspection on top.

Then back to BIR, because your books of account need to be stamped and your official receipts have to be registered before you can legally issue one.

Thirteen-plus steps, depending on locality and business type. Three to six weeks if you are lucky. Longer if you are not. PHP 6,000 on the low end, PHP 25,000+ on the high end in real costs, before you've earned a single peso of revenue.

And that is just the registration. That isn't the running of the business yet.

This is the part nobody likes to say out loud.

The Philippines is not a poor country in the way Filipinos have been taught to believe. It is a country where the cost of being legitimate has been deliberately kept high. Every step is a fee. Every fee is a small kingdom. Every kingdom has a gatekeeper. Take away the friction, and a lot of people lose their cut.

The friction is the point.

You'll hear the standard rebuttals. "NZ is small." "NZ is different." "We can't compare."

Singapore is 5.9 million people. They register a company in one day. Switzerland is 9 million people. They do it in days. The Netherlands is 17 million people, and one of the easiest jurisdictions in Europe to incorporate in.

Smaller. Bigger. It doesn't matter. The variable is not population. The variable is whether the state has decided that helping people build something is worth doing well.

The 1987 Constitution, Article II, Section 4:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime duty. Not optional. Not when convenient. The PRIME duty.

So when a Filipino entrepreneur, the kind of person who employs other Filipinos, who pays taxes, who builds something, spends three weeks of their life navigating a government obstacle course to legally exist, that is not a colorful local quirk. That is the state failing its prime duty.

To be clear, NZ is not perfect. No country is. Housing is expensive here. The healthcare system has wait times that frustrate people. Brain drain to Australia is a real and ongoing conversation. I'm not selling a postcard.

But the FLOOR is different. The expectation of how a state should treat someone who is trying to start something legitimate is on a completely different planet.

And the maddening part is, the Philippines knows how to do this. The technology exists. The PhilGEPS portal, the BIR online filing system, the various LGU "single window" pilots all prove the capability is there. It just never quite gets unified. Never quite stays funded. Never quite reaches the small business owner in a way that actually shortens the timeline.

You can streamline a registration system in 18 months if the political will exists. It hasn't existed in 38 years.

That is not a coincidence.

The Filipino is not the problem. The Filipino entrepreneur in NZ, in Singapore, in Canada, in the Gulf, the moment they're given a system that respects their time, they thrive. The diaspora's success is the country's indictment.

So the next time someone tells you "ganyan talaga, kasi Pilipinas," remember this.

It does not have to be this way. Other countries proved it. Smaller countries. Poorer countries. Countries with worse weather.

The only thing missing is the political will to make it different.

And that will, ultimately, comes from one place.

Us.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
