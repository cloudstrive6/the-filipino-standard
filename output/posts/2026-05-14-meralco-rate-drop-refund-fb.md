## POST BODY

Your Meralco bill went down this month.

Before celebrating, look at why.

The rate did not drop because Meralco decided to give anything back.

For the first time after three straight months of increases, Meralco's May rate fell. The overall rate slipped to P14.3345 per kilowatt-hour, down by about a centavo and a half from April.

A centavo and a half. After three months of climbing.

And even that small relief comes with a warning most headlines skipped. Because summer consumption is higher, plenty of households will still pay more in total pesos this month than last.

But the number is not the real story. The reason behind the number is.

Here is what actually brought the rate down. The Energy Regulatory Commission ordered Meralco to return the remaining P14.2 billion it owes customers over 12 months instead of two years. That faster schedule raised the refund credit on residential bills to P0.4278 per kilowatt-hour, more than double the previous P0.2024.

The regulator also suspended a renewable energy charge for May and June, and capped a grid fee so that suppliers, not households, absorb part of it.

Notice what every one of those moves has in common. Not one of them is Meralco lowering its price.

The generation charge, the actual cost of the electricity itself, went up this month. It climbed to P8.7942 per kilowatt-hour from P8.3864.

The bill went down because a refund went faster. Not because power got cheaper.

And that word, refund, is the whole point.

A refund is not a gift. A refund is money that was already yours, collected by someone else, and handed back later, usually without the interest it would have earned sitting in your own account. When a company refunds you, it is not being generous. It is being made to give back what it should not have been holding.

Now hold that next to a place where the system was built differently.

In New Zealand, Entrust, the community trust that owns 75.1 percent of Vector, Auckland's power company, paid roughly PHP 13,000 to every connected household last year. Since 1994 it has returned over NZD 2.5 billion to Auckland consumers. New Zealand has 26 of these community energy trusts.

That money is not a refund. It is a dividend. It is profit, shared, because the people who pay the bills also own the company.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has its own problems. Housing there is expensive, healthcare wait times in some regions are long, and brain drain to Australia is a real and ongoing conversation. But who owns the grid is not one of those problems.

That is the difference Filipinos rarely get to see laid side by side. One system returns profit because you are an owner. The other returns an overcharge because a regulator forced the schedule. One is a dividend. The other is a refund. They are not the same thing, and a country deserves to know which one it is actually getting.

The one part of this month's news genuinely worth noting is that a regulator acted at all. The 1987 Constitution, Article II, Section 4, says:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Forcing an overcharge back to the people who were overcharged is what that prime duty looks like in practice. The problem is not that the ERC did it. The problem is how rare and how small it feels when it happens.

So yes, the bill went down this month. By a centavo and a half. Funded by your own money, returned to you on a faster timetable.

That is not relief. That is the bare minimum, dressed up as good news.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa ganito.
