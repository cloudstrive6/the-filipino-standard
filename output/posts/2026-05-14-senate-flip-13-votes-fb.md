## POST BODY

This week, 13 votes flipped a Senate.

118 million Filipinos had no part in it.

That sentence is not a complaint. It is a description of how the country actually works.

On Monday, the House of Representatives passed articles of impeachment against the Vice President with 257 affirmative votes. On the same day, inside the same building, the Senate elected a new Senate President with 13 votes in favor, 9 against, 2 abstentions. By Wednesday, gunfire was reported inside the Senate as authorities tried to enforce an arrest warrant against one of the senators.

In 72 hours, the country watched a constitutional institution change direction, take in 257 articles of impeachment, and then host a security incident inside its own walls. None of it required a single Filipino voter to lift a finger.

This is the part most posts about the week are missing.

The story is not who won the leadership change. The story is not whether the impeachment is good or bad politics. The story is not even the gunfire, dramatic as it is.

The story is that everything that happened was, technically, allowed. The leadership change was constitutional. The impeachment vote was constitutional. The senators who voted, voted within their rights. None of the rules were broken.

And that is the actual problem.

When a constitutional institution can be flipped overnight by 13 votes inside a single chamber, while the public mandate from the last election has not changed at all, the institution is not malfunctioning. It is doing exactly what it was designed to do.

It is serving whoever holds the most votes inside the room.

Not whoever holds the actual mandate from the country.

That is a structural difference, and it is the difference between a republic and a chess board.

The 1987 Constitution opens with a sentence that nearly every Filipino has heard, and very few have ever been invited to take seriously.

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

Article II, Section 1.

Read it again. Sovereignty resides in the people. Government authority emanates from THEM. Not from a faction. Not from 13 senators on a Monday. Not from whichever bloc happens to hold the chamber on a given week.

So how does a country whose foundational clause names the people as the sovereign, end up with weeks where the institutions that supposedly serve that sovereign can change direction without the sovereign being asked, consulted, or even informed in advance?

The honest answer is that the institutions have been allowed to drift. Constitutional, on paper. Factional, in practice. The clause is still there. The text has not changed since 1987. What has changed, slowly and almost without anybody naming it, is the assumption inside the building that the public mandate is the floor.

The public mandate has stopped being the floor. It has become the curtain. Something to gesture at on opening day, then forget once the chamber doors close.

This is not a one-administration story. This is not a one-party story. Both tribes have done this. Both tribes will do this again. The structural pattern outlasts every news cycle.

Filipinos who have watched this play out for 38 years already know the rhythm. A new majority assembles. The leadership changes. The committee composition changes. The schedule of hearings changes. The order of bills changes. The same lawmakers who refused to move on a question last month are suddenly the ones moving on it now. The same lawmakers who were eager to move are suddenly eager to delay.

And the public watches. The public pays for all of it. The public has no seat in the room.

Article II, Section 1 was not written as a slogan. It was written as a standard. The people are the source of all authority. Every flip, every coup, every leadership change inside the building is supposed to ultimately answer to the people who voted those officials in.

When the institution starts answering to itself instead, the standard has been quietly abandoned. Nobody announced it. There was no press release. The Constitution still says what it says. It just stops being how the country actually runs.

The question is not who won the week.

The question is who the institution is supposed to serve in the first place.

Sino ba talaga ang sinisilbihan?

If you watched this week unfold and felt something you couldn't quite name, this is what it was. The institution moved. The mandate did not. That gap is the whole story.
