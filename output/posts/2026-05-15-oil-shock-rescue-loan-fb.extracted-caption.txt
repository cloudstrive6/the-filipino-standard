A war broke out thousands of kilometres away, in a strait most Filipinos will never sail through.

Within weeks, it was inside the grocery cart, the jeepney fare, and the electricity bill.

This week, the official answer arrived. It was a loan.

On Thursday, the peso closed at a record low of 61.64 to the US dollar. April inflation came in at 7.2%, the highest in over three years, with transport costs alone up 21.4% after fuel prices spiked. First-quarter growth slowed to 2.8%, the weakest since the pandemic.

Then on Friday, the Asian Development Bank offered the national government up to 1.75 billion US dollars in additional crisis lending to help manage the economic impact of the Middle East conflict. That is on top of roughly 2 billion US dollars in loans already being prepared for the country this year.

The headline frames it as help arriving. In the short term, it is. But it is worth sitting with the deeper question the headline skips.

Why does a single chokepoint on the other side of the planet land this hard, this fast, on a Filipino household?

Because the Philippines imports almost all of its oil. Around 98% of it, with nearly every barrel sourced from or shipped through the Middle East. When the Strait of Hormuz gets tense, there is no domestic cushion to soften the blow. The price shock travels straight from the tanker to the pump to the palengke.

And here is the part most people are never told. The Philippines is one of the few major Asian economies with no government-held strategic petroleum reserve. Other countries in the region built national stockpiles precisely because oil shocks are not freak events. They are a recurring feature of the last fifty years. The Philippines instead leans on a minimum inventory rule imposed on private oil companies, and is only now, in the middle of the crisis, seeing a bill filed in the Senate to finally create a real reserve.

A weaker peso makes all of this worse. Oil is priced in dollars. At 61.64 to the dollar, every imported barrel costs more pesos than it did the day before. The fragility compounds itself.

So the shock is global. The fragility is a choice.

No government controls when a war breaks out in the Middle East. Every government controls whether its country built anything to absorb one. The Philippines has watched the same chokepoint cause the same pain in the 1970s, in 1990, in 2008, in 2022. Four decades of the same lesson. Four decades of choosing not to build the buffer.

A rescue loan is not resilience. A rescue loan is the invoice for the buffer that was never built, and it is the public that repays it, with interest, long after the news cycle has moved on.

The 1987 Constitution, Article II, Section 4, states it plainly:

"The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Protecting citizens from a foreseeable, repeating external shock is not an optional extra. It is the prime duty. Energy security is not a luxury policy. It is the floor.

A country cannot control when the next oil shock comes. It can only control whether it built anything to survive one.

For half a century, the answer has been the same. Borrow, absorb, move on, forget.

Kailan pa ba?
