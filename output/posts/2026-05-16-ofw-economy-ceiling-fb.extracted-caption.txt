The Philippine economy has a Plan A: send the workers abroad and live off the remittances.

This week, Plan A started running out of road.

The BSP confirmed last Thursday that cash remittances in March came in at USD 2.87 billion, up just 2.3 percent from a year ago. That is the slowest growth in nearly three years.

The Department of Migrant Workers tells the same story from a different angle. In the first quarter of 2026, OFW deployments dropped to 526,464, down from 767,057 in the same quarter last year. A 45.15 percent collapse in a single quarter.

About 40,000 Filipinos who were already cleared to leave are sitting at home with their bags packed. The Middle East corridor froze. Saudi Arabia deployment fell about 39 percent. UAE fell 51 percent. Singapore, Hong Kong, Taiwan, Qatar, Japan, Kuwait, Australia, South Korea, all slowing at the same time.

Now look at the country those workers were supposed to be supporting from abroad.

The Social Weather Stations survey for March 2026 found that 52 percent of Filipino families, about 14.5 million households, consider themselves poor. 11.7 million say they are food-poor. The national self-rated poverty threshold sits at PHP 15,000 a month. Fifteen thousand pesos is the line Filipinos themselves drew, and roughly half the country is on the wrong side of it.

In 2025, Filipinos abroad sent home a record USD 35.63 billion. That is not a development success story. That is a coping story. A 118-million-person republic organised its single largest "industry" around the export of its own workers, and called the dependency a feature.

Here is the part no SONA will say out loud.

A country that needs its citizens to leave so that the citizens who stayed can eat is not running an economy. It is running a salvage operation. The OFW became the country's most successful product because nothing else was ever built to be successful at home. The diaspora is what the data calls "remittance inflows." Talk to any Filipino family that has put a parent on a plane for the Gulf or a sister on a plane for Hong Kong and they will tell you the more honest word: hostage of necessity.

When the world is calm, the system looks like resilience. When the world tilts, a war in the Gulf, a trade shock, a slowdown in any one of the host economies, the whole arrangement quietly admits what it always was. Outsourced citizenship.

The 1987 Constitution, Article II, Section 4:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

A country whose people are forced to serve and protect themselves by working in someone else's country has quietly redirected the prime duty. The Filipino abroad pays the school fees the state did not pay. The Filipino abroad covers the hospital bill PhilHealth did not cover. The Filipino abroad finances the small house the housing programs never built.

For 40 years the dollar inflows masked the failure. For 40 years the line "OFWs are our modern heroes" did double duty as a thank-you and as a permission slip for the state to keep underdelivering at home.

Filipinos thrive abroad. Filipino nurses run wards in the UK, Canada, NZ, the Gulf. Filipino engineers build infrastructure in Singapore. Filipino seafarers move global trade. Filipino entrepreneurs build successful companies in dozens of countries the moment they are given a working environment to build in. The problem has never been the Filipino. The problem has always been the country that needs the Filipino to leave.

Plan B was never built. The day Plan A wobbles is the day everyone is forced to notice.

This week was that day.

Ganun kalala.

If you have a relative who is one of the 40,000 sitting at home with cleared papers and nowhere to fly, what is their next move?
