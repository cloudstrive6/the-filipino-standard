# The Chamber That Writes The Law Became The Place Where The Law Was Dodged

The Senate is where Philippine laws are written.

This week, the Senate was where Philippine laws were broken so that a senator wanted by an international court could quietly walk out the door.

On Wednesday night, May 13, gunfire rang out inside the Senate premises in Pasay City. The Senate Sergeant-at-Arms, by the Palace's own account, fired the first warning shot at an agent from the National Bureau of Investigation. The NBI agent fired a warning shot back. An NBI driver was arrested at the scene. The chaos lasted long enough that by the early hours of Thursday morning, Sen. Ronald "Bato" Dela Rosa, the subject of an International Criminal Court arrest warrant for crimes against humanity, had quietly left the building.

The warrant against him was unsealed by the ICC on May 11. It covers the alleged murder of no less than 32 persons between July 2016 and the end of April 2018, during his term as the country's national police chief enforcing the Duterte drug war. He has not been tried. He has not been convicted. He has, however, been formally charged by the international court whose jurisdiction the Philippines once accepted.

On the same day the warrant was unsealed, the senator cast the deciding vote in a Senate leadership reshuffle. After the vote, the chamber placed him under "protective custody."

Two days later, the gunfire happened. By Thursday morning, he was gone.

On May 15, the Office of the Ombudsman ordered the six-month preventive suspension, without pay, of Senate Sergeant-at-Arms Mao Aplasca. The order can be appealed. The Ombudsman explained the suspension was meant to prevent "undue influence" on the parallel investigation, and the NBI Director publicly confirmed that authorities are also probing whether the Wednesday night shooting was staged.

This is where most of the coverage stopped.

A security officer fired a shot inside the Senate. The Ombudsman suspended him. Headline filed. Next story.

But this story is not about a security officer.

It is about an institution.

Because here is the question nobody in the corridor has clearly asked. What is the chamber for, if not for the law?

The 1987 Constitution gave a clean answer in Article XI, Section 1.

> "Public office is a public trust. Public officers and employees must at all times be accountable to the people, serve them with utmost responsibility, integrity, loyalty, and efficiency, act with patriotism and justice, and lead modest lives."

Public trust is not a slogan. It is a fiduciary standard. The chamber holds power on behalf of someone else, and the someone else is the Filipino people. The chamber does not own the building. The chamber does not own the seat. The chamber does not own the gavel. All of it is on loan, every day, from the public.

A chamber that uses its own premises to shelter one of its own members from a pending arrest is not exercising privilege. It is consuming the trust.

And here is the part that ages worst.

The same chamber will convene on Monday as an impeachment court. The same chamber writes the laws that govern an ordinary Filipino's permits, taxes, prosecution, and arrest. The same chamber decides, every fiscal year, how much of the public's money the public will be allowed to know about. The same chamber demands of every citizen what it currently struggles to demand of itself.

Filipinos who have lived under any functional rule-of-law system abroad will tell you what stood out. Not the buildings. Not the budgets. Not even the politicians. It was the assumption that elected office did not put a person above the warrant served on the citizen. That assumption is the floor. Without it, there is no rule of law. There is only rule of seat.

This is not a story about one senator and one ICC warrant. It is a story about whether the Filipino people still own the chamber the Constitution says they own.

The Sergeant-at-Arms is being made to answer for a warning shot.

The institution that produced the conditions for that shot is being asked to answer nothing.

Sino ba talaga ang dapat managot?

If the Senate cannot say out loud that it will not be a sanctuary against a lawful warrant, then the chamber has already answered the question.

It just did not say it in words. It said it in gunfire.
