## POST BODY

Tomorrow at three in the afternoon, the Senate of the Philippines convenes as an impeachment court.

One week ago, the same chamber held a coup against its own leadership.

Both sentences describe the same Senate.

The Articles of Impeachment against the Vice President were transmitted from the House to the Senate on the evening of May 13. The Senate President has scheduled the convening for May 18 at 3:00 PM. He will take his oath first as presiding officer. The 23 other senators will take their oath as senator-judges. Summons will be issued to the Vice President. The trial proper will follow on a date to be set.

That is the choreography of the next forty-eight hours. By Monday afternoon, the constitutional accountability mechanism for the country's highest officials will be sitting in session.

Here is what the same chamber did the week before.

On May 11, a single motion declared all Senate leadership posts vacant. The vote was 13 to 9. A new leadership bloc took over the chamber the same afternoon.

The chair of the Senate's blue ribbon committee, who had been running the inquiry into the flood control corruption scandal, lost the gavel in the reshuffle. That inquiry had already named six officials for possible plunder and bribery. Three of them are sitting senators. The committee's receipts, audit trails, and witness statements were forwarded to the Office of the Ombudsman. The Senate's own work on the file paused.

That is what the chamber chose to do, on its own initiative, in the week before it was scheduled to sit as a court.

The Philippine Senate's power to remove a Vice President is not an inherited prestige. It is a delegated authority. The 1987 Constitution is precise about where that authority comes from.

Article II, Section 1:

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

The senators do not sit as judges because they hold the office. They sit as judges because the people, in whom sovereignty resides, delegated that role to the chamber. Every senator who takes the senator-judge's oath on Monday afternoon is borrowing power. Borrowed power has terms.

The Constitution did not say the people delegated this power so the chamber could conduct accountability selectively. The legitimacy of an impeachment court is not downstream of the closeness of the math. It is downstream of one thing: whether the chamber that sits as a court has shown itself willing to hold accountability sacred when accountability inconveniences the chamber.

The flood control inquiry was the test. A Senate inquiry, conducted by a Senate committee, into the misuse of Philippine public funds on Philippine infrastructure work. The inquiry got close to the receipts. The chamber reorganized. The inquiry paused.

That is the chamber that convenes as a court tomorrow.

None of this prejudges the trial. None of this names a verdict. None of this answers the technical questions about evidence, procedure, or the Senate Rules.

What it does answer is the question that hangs over the chamber before the first gavel.

Whose court is this, really?

If the answer is "the people's court, on loan to twenty-four senators," then the chamber owes the public an explanation for what was done to the blue ribbon work the week before, and a visible commitment that the same instincts will not run the trial.

If the answer is "the senators' court, to be conducted on terms convenient to whoever holds the votes," then what convenes tomorrow afternoon is not the constitutional mechanism the 1987 framers designed. It is a house show with a verdict shaped before the witnesses are sworn.

Kailan pa ba?

Eight million Filipino households watched their electricity bill move again this month. The receipts on their flood control taxes were walked over to the Ombudsman by a Senate committee that had just been disempowered by its own chamber.

Tomorrow at three, the same chamber sits as a court.

The eyes of the country are on the bench.
