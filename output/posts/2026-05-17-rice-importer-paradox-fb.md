## POST BODY

Filipinos paid 13.7 percent more for rice in April than a year earlier.

The Philippines is the largest rice importer on the planet.

Both sentences describe the same country. The same week. The same month.

The April 2026 inflation print landed at 7.2 percent, the fastest in over three years. Rice alone climbed from 3.5 percent year-on-year inflation in March to 13.7 percent in April. Cereals, the category that contains rice, contributed more than half of all food inflation in a single month. Households felt it before the report came out, the way households always feel it before the report comes out.

By the time the Palace ordered the Agriculture Secretary to do "everything possible" to bring prices down, the country had already absorbed the hit at the dinner table.

Here is the part of the story that does not fit on a news headline.

The Philippines is not a rice-poor country. Rice grows here. Rice has grown here for centuries. Whole regions of the country are built around the crop. Whole festivals, whole songs, whole household economies are organized around rice.

And the Philippines is, year after year, the largest rice importer in the world.

In 2024, the country imported a record 4.8 million metric tons of rice. About 78 percent of that came from Vietnam. The rest came from Thailand, Pakistan, and Myanmar. The pattern was not a one-off. The Philippines has been at or near the top of the global rice import table for years now, and every shock in the global supply chain lands directly on the Filipino household budget.

That is not a weather problem. That is a policy problem.

In 2019, Republic Act 11203, the Rice Tariffication Law, replaced the country's quantitative limits on rice imports with a tariff regime and substantially stripped the National Food Authority of its regulatory teeth. The official theory was that opening up imports would push rice prices down for consumers. The official theory has a graph behind it. The household budget has a different graph behind it.

Under Executive Order 105, effective January 2026, the tariff regime shifted again. Rice import tariffs now move quarterly, indexed to the international price of Vietnam 5 percent broken rice. When global prices fall, the Philippine tariff rises. When global prices rise, the Philippine tariff falls. The math is supposed to keep retail prices stable.

It is not keeping retail prices stable.

It is doing something else. It is binding the Philippine rice table directly to whatever happens in a Vietnamese export market and a global shipping lane. The bowl on the Manila dinner table now moves at the speed of an international price index, set thousands of kilometres away.

A country with that arrangement does not actually have a rice policy. It has a subscription.

The deeper question is the one that almost never gets asked out loud.

Why is the country that gave the world the word "palay" relying on Vietnam for 78 percent of its imported rice?

It is not because Filipino farmers do not work. Filipino farmers are some of the hardest-working people in the country and the most exposed to price collapses, weather shocks, and middlemen who clip the value chain before the farmer ever sees it. The 2019 law made imported rice cheaper at the retail shelf and quietly made the local farmgate price worse. The same Filipinos who buy the rice at the market lose the income that would have made buying the rice easier in the first place.

It is also not because the country is too small. The Philippines has roughly 118 million people. Vietnam, the country selling most of the imported rice, has about 100 million. Both grow rice. One feeds itself and exports the surplus. The other imports.

The difference is not climate. The difference is not work ethic. The difference is whether the state treats food security as a foundational responsibility or as a market problem to be managed by tariff adjustment.

The current arrangement has made the answer clear. Food security in the Philippines is now treated as a market problem. And the market has just sent the receipt.

A 13.7 percent rice inflation print in a rice-eating country is not a freak event. It is the natural output of a system that imported its way out of a hard policy question and called the import volume a solution.

The hard policy question is still there.

Who actually feeds the Filipino?

If the answer is "Vietnam, mostly, depending on the tariff schedule," then the country has quietly outsourced a piece of sovereignty that no Constitution can give back later.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
