## POST BODY

At three this afternoon, twenty-four senators will sit as judges.

One hundred eighteen million Filipinos will sit as audience.

Only one of those groups has the option to scroll past.

The Senate of the Philippines convenes today as an impeachment court at 3:00 PM. The Senate President will take the presiding officer's oath first. The other 23 senators will take their oath as senator-judges. Summons will be issued. The trial proper will follow on a date the chamber has not yet set.

That is the chamber's choreography for the afternoon.

The harder question is what 118 million Filipinos do for the next several weeks.

In the modern era, the Senate has sat as an impeachment court only twice. In 2000 to 2001, the Estrada trial collapsed when senators voted 11 to 10 to keep the second envelope of alleged evidence sealed. The prosecutors walked out. The country walked into People Power II. The chamber did not finish what the Constitution asked it to finish.

In 2012, the Corona trial ran four months. The vote was 20 to 3 for conviction. The chamber did finish, and the verdict held.

The same institution, the same rules, two completely different exits. The variable was not the Constitution. The Constitution did not move. The variable was the audience.

Article XI, Section 3, paragraph 6 of the 1987 Constitution is precise about where the power to remove a sitting Vice President resides:

> "The Senate shall have the sole power to try and decide all cases of impeachment. When sitting for that purpose, the Senators shall be on oath or affirmation. ... No person shall be convicted without the concurrence of two-thirds of all the Members of the Senate."

Two-thirds of all the members. Sixteen of twenty-four. That is the math the chamber will run on.

But notice what the clause does not say. It does not say the Senate exercises this power as the chamber's own property. It does not say the senators sit as a court because the seat is theirs. The sole power is the chamber's. The power itself is the public's, on loan.

The two impeachment trials in modern Philippine history both turned on whether the public stayed informed enough to make ducking the verdict politically expensive. In 2001, the chamber's attempt to bury the evidence was met with a population that refused to look away. The institution moved because the audience moved.

In 2012, the trial proceeded on schedule because the audience watched the receipts as carefully as it watched the personalities. The verdict was earned by months of attention, not delivered by drama.

Filipinos who have watched the institution before know the pattern. The cameras come on. The hearings get clipped for primetime. The trending hashtags rotate. The personalities become characters. The substance becomes background music to the spectacle. By the time the verdict lands, the audience has consumed so much theater that the verdict feels like the season finale of a show rather than the resolution of a constitutional question.

That outcome is not an accident of bad television. It is what happens when the audience accepts a role of consumer instead of principal.

Watching is not consent. Spectating is not sovereignty. Consuming clips is not citizenship.

Here is what an informed audience actually does over the next several weeks.

Reads the Articles of Impeachment. Not the press release about them. The articles themselves, line by line. The reasons cited for removal are the only legal question on the table. Anything outside those reasons is theater.

Notes the votes. Not the speeches. Votes are receipts the chamber cannot edit later. Speeches are airtime, written for clips.

Tracks the procedural rulings. Motions to dismiss. Motions to defer. Motions to bury evidence. Each one is a tell. The Estrada trial ended on a procedural motion that buried evidence. Procedure is not boring; procedure is where institutions choose what kind of institution they want to be.

Compares the receipts to the public statements. When a senator says one thing on camera and votes another way on the floor, both go on the record. The public is the record-keeper.

Holds the verdict to the standard the Constitution set, not the standard the news cycle settles for. Two-thirds for conviction. Whatever the math says, the math says. The audience's job is not to celebrate or to mourn; the audience's job is to remember.

This is the work.

It is not glamorous work. It is not screenshot-friendly work. It will not trend. It is the work that a republic asks of its citizens in the weeks when the chamber sits as a court on the public's behalf.

The chamber's job is to try and decide. That is in the text.

The audience's job is to make sure the deciding is done in front of an audience that knows what it is watching. That is in the design.

At three this afternoon, the senators take their oath. Their oath is to the law and to the Constitution. The audience takes no oath, because the audience already holds the original authority that the senators are borrowing for the duration of the trial.

The country is the principal. The chamber is the agent.

For the next several weeks, the principal is going to be asked to pay attention.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa panonood na lang.
