## POST BODY

The Senate held a hearing today on whether the government should stop charging 12% VAT on the electricity bills of the poorest Filipino households.

Read that line twice.

That is not a budget hearing. That is the state asking itself, in public, on a livestream, whether to keep taxing what citizens cannot live without.

The bill is Senator Risa Hontiveros's SB 2093, the "Kuryente Relief Act of 2026." It would exempt residential households consuming 150 kilowatt-hours or less per month from the 12% VAT on their monthly bill.

A family of five in Metro Manila on minimum wage typically falls inside that band, 100 to 150 kWh, according to the senator's own filing and corroborating coverage by GMA News and the Inquirer. The estimated monthly saving for an exempt household, if the bill passes: roughly 250 to 300 pesos.

Two hundred fifty pesos. The state has been collecting that, on top of one of the most expensive electricity bills in Asia, from households the same legislature classifies as "energy-poor."

Every month. For years.

The current Meralco residential rate for May is P14.3345 per kWh, after a 1.5-centavo token decrease and an ERC-ordered acceleration of an existing P14.2-billion refund. A refund of past overcharges. Not a discount. Not a gift.

The regulator and the utility are returning, in monthly instalments, money that should not have been collected at that level in the first place.

On top of that residential rate, the government takes 12 percent.

The Department of Energy has said publicly that it supports lifting the VAT but that the economic managers must evaluate the revenue impact first. That sentence deserves attention. The state is treating the VAT collected from energy-poor households as a revenue stream worth defending in a meeting before it can be returned to the people paying it.

Article II, Section 4 of the 1987 Constitution is precise:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime duty. Not "after the revenue managers review impact." Prime.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has its own problems, expensive housing, brain drain to Australia, healthcare wait times in some regions. But the point of bringing it up here is not to romanticize.

The point is that in Auckland in 2025, the community trust Entrust paid roughly PHP 13,000 per household as an ownership dividend. Not a refund. Not a tax break. A return on the fact that the people are on the cap table of the utility that serves them.

In the Philippines, over 8.2 million households are on Meralco's customer list. Zero are on its cap table. And on top of paying that monthly bill, every one of them pays the state another 12 percent for the privilege of keeping the lights on.

Filipinos who have paid an electricity bill at home and a power bill in a system that does not tax basic survival recognize the difference instantly. It is not the rate alone. It is what the bill is being treated as.

A bill in one country is a payment for a service. A bill in this country is a service charge, a refund, and a tax base, stacked on a household that may already be choosing between food and the meter.

Here is the part that does not fit on a banner.

A refund is not generosity. A refund is the return of money that was already overcharged.

A VAT exemption for the energy-poor is not a gift. It is the state finally admitting it should not have been taxing a survival service at the same rate it taxes a luxury car.

A dividend is something different again. A dividend is the system sharing value with the people who own it.

The Philippines has not built that system. It has built one where the customer is not the owner, the regulator is the referee, and the state is a third hand reaching into the bill.

The Senate hearing today is a small, polite, civil-society step toward fixing one of those layers. The bill is good. The senator's framing is correct. Supporting it is not the question.

The question is what it says about the design that the bill is needed at all.

The question is why holding a hearing on whether to stop taxing the poorest is treated as bold legislation instead of a basic correction of an old mistake.

The question is why "economic managers must evaluate the impact" is the polite version of "give us a moment to decide whether the state can afford to stop overcharging the poor."

The bill is a small step. The hearing is necessary. The fact that the step requires a Senate hearing, an economic-manager review, and a presidential signature, all to lift a 12% tax on the bills of households that consume less than 150 kWh per month, is the indictment.

Kailan pa ba?
