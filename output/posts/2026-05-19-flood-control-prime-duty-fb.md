The country pays for a flood wall.

The country gets thirty-five percent of a flood wall.

The other sixty-five percent never reaches the riverbank. It gets eaten before the cement is poured.

On May 18, flash floods hit multiple parts of the Davao Region. On May 19, a lawmaker walked into the Office of the Ombudsman with a complaint covering one hundred twenty-one DPWH flood control contracts in Davao City's First District, worth approximately P4.4 billion, awarded between 2019 and 2022.

The pattern in the filing is the part to read slowly.

Two contracts allegedly funded construction on identical river sections, about P135 million between them. The same P115 million project appeared twice in the 2020 General Appropriations Act and was awarded to two different contractors. Eight projects worth P425 million were reportedly implemented in locations different from those they were authorized for.

A river does not get two flood walls because the river had two budgets. The reader can finish that sentence.

Zoom out from one district. The national picture is worse, and it is older.

Since July 2022, public funds for flood control nationwide have reached approximately P545.6 billion. Out of 2,409 contractors DPWH identified for local and national flood projects, twenty percent of that total budget, around P100 billion, went to just fifteen of them. Fifteen.

The Independent Commission for Infrastructure prioritized 416 projects as ghosts before it formally closed in March 2026 and handed the work over to other agencies. The Senate Blue Ribbon Committee's partial report on flood control has drawn 7 of the 9 senator signatures it needs to be released.

On the Senate record, Sen. Erwin Tulfo, the panel's vice chair, has testified that the math works roughly like this. Contractors are pressured for 20 to 25 percent commissions at every level of approval, from the district engineer up to the undersecretary. By the time a project actually starts, only 30 to 40 percent of its original value is left for the concrete, the steel, the labor, the wall that is supposed to keep the water out of someone's house.

That number is a senator's testimony, not a closed forensic audit. But the math is consistent with what every Filipino who has tried to run a legitimate business in the Philippines already suspects. Compliance costs you. Connection rewards you. The bidding podium is shaped to land on the same fifteen names.

Now read what the 1987 Constitution actually says about this.

Article II, Section 4:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Read that as a contract.

When the rainy season opens with flash floods in Davao on May 18, and a P4.4 billion red-flag pile lands at the Ombudsman on May 19, the contract is not being honored. It is being looted in advance of the storm.

Here is the part nobody likes to say out loud.

This is not a series of one-off scandals. This is the architecture. The category called "flood control" has functioned, for years, less as a public works program and more as a quarterly distribution mechanism. The walls get built tall enough to photograph. The walls get built short enough to overtop. The next storm arrives. The next budget arrives. The same fifteen names arrive.

The Filipino is not bad at building walls. The Filipino built the Banaue Rice Terraces by hand. Filipino engineers design storm surge defenses in Singapore. Filipino foremen pour concrete across the Gulf. The skill is not missing. The walls would stand if they were paid for honestly.

What is missing is the political will to make the prime duty an actual duty.

Until then, the typhoon is not the disaster. The budget is the disaster. The typhoon just confirms it.

Saan napupunta ang pera ng baha?

Sa ilalim na ng tubig.
