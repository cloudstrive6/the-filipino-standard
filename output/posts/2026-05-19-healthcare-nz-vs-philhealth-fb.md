# Healthcare: what the floor actually looks like in two countries

In New Zealand, a family walks out of a public hospital without a bill.

In the Philippines, a family walks out of a public hospital with a list of things they still owe.

That is not a difference in compassion. That is a difference in design.

In NZ, public hospital care is funded through general taxation. Citizens and residents are not handed an invoice at discharge. GP visits cost a small subsidised fee, and many children under 14 see a GP for free. The floor is not "great care." The floor is "you will not be financially ruined by getting sick."

In the Philippines, every working Filipino pays into PhilHealth every payday. The program is real. The contributions are real. The promise on paper is real.

The receipts at the cashier window tell a different story.

International datasets consistently show Filipino households paying for roughly four out of every ten pesos spent on health in the country directly out of pocket. The NZ share is closer to one peso in eight. Same act, getting sick, with the bill landing on completely different shoulders.

This is why a single hospitalization can wipe out a Filipino family's savings, while in NZ the same hospitalization does not. Not because Kiwis love their families more. Because their system was structured to stop a medical event from becoming a financial event.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own healthcare problems. Wait times in some regions are long. Rural access is uneven. Specialist queues can stretch for months. None of that is being romanticised here.

But the FLOOR is different. In one country, the floor is "you will be treated and you will not be billed for the basics." In the other, the floor is "show your receipt of contributions, then prepare to pay anyway."

The Constitution is direct about whose job this is. Article II, Section 4:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime duty. Not optional. Not when the budget allows. The PRIME duty.

So why does the country with a constitutional command to serve and protect leave its citizens paying four times the share Kiwis pay? Why does PhilHealth, audited by COA cycle after cycle, still leave the average family covering most of the bill?

Because nobody at the top of the system has been forced to fix it. The contributions keep coming in. The receipts at the cashier window keep getting longer. The cycle keeps turning. Filipino families keep absorbing the difference between what was promised and what was delivered.

Filipinos deserve a healthcare floor where getting sick does not mean getting financially destroyed. Not because they migrated. Because they are citizens of a republic that already wrote down its prime duty.

Saan napupunta ang kontribusyon?
