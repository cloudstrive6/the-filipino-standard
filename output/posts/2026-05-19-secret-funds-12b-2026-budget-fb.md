Article II, Section 28 of the 1987 Constitution promises "full public disclosure of all its transactions involving public interest."

The 2026 national budget then quietly allocates P11.8 billion to a category of spending built to escape that exact rule.

That category has a name. Confidential and intelligence funds.

Here is how they work. Once an agency's budget includes confidential or intelligence funds, the money is audited under separate, restricted procedures. The reports do not go through the regular public disclosure process. The public sees the total. The public does not see what it bought.

The 2026 General Appropriations Act, signed into law on January 5, 2026, carries approximately P11.8 billion in confidential and intelligence funds across executive agencies. The Office of the President alone holds P4.56 billion of that total. Roughly 38 percent of every peso classified as secret spending in 2026 sits in the civilian executive office.

Then there is what happened on the way to the GAA.

The Senate version of the 2026 budget did not trim those funds. It added to them. P876.6 million more in confidential funds, lifting that line to P5.24 billion. P231.15 million more in intelligence funds, lifting that line to P6.63 billion. Total: nearly P12 billion before bicam.

While that was happening, the Senate cut its OWN 2026 budget to P7.52 billion, almost half of its previous P13.9 billion. The Senate allocated itself zero confidential funds. Zero intelligence funds.

So the institution that is supposed to scrutinize public spending lives on a halved, fully auditable budget. The institutions it is supposed to scrutinize keep growing their secret one.

Now read the constitutional clause again.

> "Subject to reasonable conditions prescribed by law, the State adopts and implements a policy of full public disclosure of all its transactions involving public interest."

That opening qualifier, "subject to reasonable conditions prescribed by law," is supposed to be a narrow carve-out for genuinely sensitive operations. It is not supposed to be the door through which a P12 billion category walks every year.

Here is the part nobody likes to say out loud.

This is not a leak in the system. This is a feature of the system. The category exists precisely so the answer to "where did the money go?" can be "you are not allowed to ask." Every administration since 1987 has used it. The numbers move up and down. The category never closes.

The Constitution did not promise full public disclosure as an aspiration. It promised it as a policy.

P12 billion in 2026. None of it traceable by the public who paid for it.
