# A republic of around 178 families

Most countries pass political power between strangers. The Philippines passes it between cousins.

The math is not subtle. Research compiled by PCIJ and academic teams at the Asian Institute of Management has tracked it across multiple election cycles. A few hundred political families, often cited as around 178 at the most concentrated count, hold a disproportionate share of elective offices from the barangay up to the Senate. In many provinces, the math is harsher. A single family controls the governor, the vice governor, the congressional seat, the mayor's office, and a board member or three. Same surname. Different position. Same household budget meeting.

This is not "a few bad apples." This is the orchard.

A dynasty does not need to be cartoonish to be a dynasty. It just needs to be the reason a 24-year-old with a famous surname becomes a vice governor before they have ever held a job that did not come with their family's last name attached.

The 1987 Constitution saw this coming. Article II, Section 26 says it plainly:

> "The State shall guarantee equal access to opportunities for public service, and prohibit political dynasties as may be defined by law."

Prohibit. The word is prohibit.

The trap is in the last six words. "As may be defined by law." Defined by whom? By Congress. The same Congress that, if it defined the term honestly, would have to disqualify a large share of its own members and their relatives from the next election.

For 38 years, Congress has refused to define the term. Bills have been filed. Bills have been buried in committee. Bills have died at second reading. Bills have been rewritten so narrowly they would exempt almost everyone. Different decade, same outcome.

That is not a legislative coincidence. That is a class protecting itself.

The cost is not just symbolic. Studies from the AIM Policy Center have repeatedly found that provinces with the deepest dynasty concentration also tend to score worse on poverty incidence, on human development indicators, on local revenue performance. Correlation is not causation. But after enough cycles, "the families who rule us also preside over the worst outcomes" stops looking like a coincidence and starts looking like a pattern.

Here is the part most Filipinos already feel and rarely say out loud.

The electoral system is not really a competition. It is a renewal. A renewal of permission for the same families to keep doing what they have been doing for two or three generations. The "election" is a ritual. The outcome was set in 1972, or 1986, or sometime in the late 1990s when the same surnames decided which seat would belong to which child next.

This is why platforms do not matter. Why parties dissolve and re-form between cycles. Why the senator who switched parties last year is suddenly your local congressman's running mate next year. It is not ideology. It is family logistics.

The Constitution already named the disease. Article II, Section 26 is not a suggestion. It is a command the State was supposed to obey by passing the enabling law. The State, made up largely of people from those same families, has spent 38 years politely declining.

There is no version of Philippine reform that survives without naming this. Public services, healthcare, education, infrastructure, utilities, every one of them keeps under-performing because the same small pool of families benefits from the inefficiency, the contract, the placement, the appointment. They are not going to vote against their own household.

The Filipino people are not the problem. The Filipino people keep showing up to vote. The Filipino people keep paying taxes. The Filipino people keep building, leaving, returning, trying again.

What has not shown up, for 38 years, is the law the Constitution ordered.

Kailan pa ba?
