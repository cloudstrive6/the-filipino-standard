# "Ganyan talaga" is not an analysis. It is a closing of the conversation.

Every conversation about reform in the Philippines tends to end the same way.

Someone raises the problem. The traffic, the bill, the queue, the dynasty, the bidding, the school, the typhoon response. Someone agrees. Someone gets briefly angry. Someone explains it. Someone proposes the fix.

And then somebody, often the kindest person in the room, sighs and says: "Eh, ganyan talaga."

The room goes quiet. The conversation ends.

The phrase has done its job.

"Ganyan talaga" is not Filipino wisdom. It is not deep cultural acceptance. It is not a sign of maturity. It is the verbal equivalent of putting the receipt back in the drawer and pretending the number was not that high. It is the polite version of giving up.

The phrase sounds neutral. It even sounds patient. But underneath, it carries a very specific argument: "things have always been this way, things will always be this way, and nothing the person in this room can do will change it." That is a complete argument with three premises. None of the three is actually true.

Things have not always been this way. Forty years ago, EDSA was not a guarantee. Filipinos delivered it anyway. The very Constitution that today gets ignored was written because Filipinos decided that "ganyan talaga" was no longer the answer. The country has rewritten its rules before. It can rewrite them again.

Things will not always be this way. Countries change. Not by miracle, by mechanism. New laws. New enforcement. New leadership pools. New media. New economic gravity. The places Filipinos work in, Singapore, NZ, Canada, the Gulf, Australia, all had to actively choose to become functional. None of them were born that way. Korea was once poorer than the Philippines. Vietnam, Indonesia, Estonia, Rwanda, every one of them looked at "ganyan talaga" and pushed back.

And the person in the room can change things. Not single-handedly. But the change has never come from one heroic individual. It has come from enough citizens deciding at the same time that the phrase no longer ends the conversation. Public pressure changes votes. Votes change Congress. Congress changes laws. Laws change incentives. Incentives change behavior.

Here is the part the phrase hides.

"Ganyan talaga" benefits exactly one group. The people who benefit from the system staying the way it is. They are not the ones who say the phrase out loud. They are the ones who quietly count on the rest of the country saying it.

Every time a Filipino says "ganyan talaga" to a neighbor, the next election cycle gets easier for the incumbent. Every time a worker says "ganyan talaga" to a coworker, the next labor abuse goes unreported. Every time a small business owner says "ganyan talaga" to a friend, the next bribe is paid without protest. The phrase is not free. Somebody is collecting on it.

The Constitution opens with this:

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

Read that one more time. "Sovereignty resides in the people." Not in the dynasty. Not in the COA-flagged office. Not in the family that owns the franchise. In the people. That includes the kind tito who just sighed "ganyan talaga" five minutes ago.

The Constitution did not say sovereignty resides in the people unless they are tired.

Filipinos are not lazy. Filipinos are not stupid. Filipinos are not cursed. Filipinos have been worn down by a system that profits from their resignation and politely repackages that resignation as "Filipino values." Resilience as a value is beautiful. Resilience as a coping mechanism for a system that refuses to function is exploitation.

So the next time the phrase shows up in the conversation, refuse it gently. Say: "Ganyan nga, pero hindi dapat."

That is not a slogan. That is the start of every reform that has ever happened anywhere.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
