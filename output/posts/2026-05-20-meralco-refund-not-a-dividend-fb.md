**A refund is not a dividend.**

Read your May Meralco bill carefully and there it is: a small "refund" line, quietly giving a little back.

Here is what nobody says out loud about that line.

That refund is not Meralco sharing its profit with you. It is Meralco returning money it should never have collected from you in the first place.

In April, the Energy Regulatory Commission ordered Meralco to hand back the remaining P14.17 billion of a larger P19.96-billion regulatory adjustment. The order compressed the payback from 36 months down to 12, so the relief shows up faster on bills starting this month.

Sounds generous. Until you read what the refund actually is.

It comes from Meralco's distribution-rate "true-up" for the period July 2022 to December 2024. In plain language: the regulator compared what Meralco was approved to charge, a distribution rate of P1.35 per kilowatt-hour, against what it actually collected. The collection ran ahead of the approved rate. The gap is the refund. Households were billed for more than the approved distribution rate allowed, and now a fraction of that is being returned, slowly, line by line, over a year.

A company giving back money it over-collected is not charity. It is correction. The fact that it took a regulator's 24-page order to make it happen tells you who the system actually answers to.

Now look at what a refund looks like when the people actually own the wires.

In Auckland, the power network is 75.1% owned by Entrust, a community trust. The owners are the households connected to the grid. In 2025, Entrust paid NZD $364, around PHP 13,000, to every connected household. Not a refund of an overcharge. A dividend. A share of the profit, paid out because the people who use the network are the people who own it. Since 1994, Entrust has returned over NZD $2.5 billion to Auckland consumers. New Zealand has 26 of these community energy trusts across the country.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has rough housing costs, real brain drain to Australia, and uneven healthcare waits in some regions. The point is not that NZ is paradise. The point is the structure underneath the bill.

Here is the contrast, stripped to the bone.

In Auckland, the household opens an envelope and finds a cheque, because it is an owner.

In Manila, the household opens a bill and finds a refund line, because it was overcharged and a regulator noticed.

One is ownership. The other is a correction you had to wait years for.

And the deeper pattern is this. Meralco has never been community-owned. Not in 1903 when it was American-owned. Not in 1962 under the Lopez family. Not in 1972 under Marcos cronies. Not in 1986 when it returned to the Lopez family. Not since 2008 under MPIC and JG Summit. In 122 years, the more than 8 million households who pay every single month have never once been on the cap table.

So while this year's refund trickles back, the same company is asking regulators to nearly double its distribution charge to P2.34 per kilowatt-hour to fund a P532-billion expansion plan, on its way to roughly P50 billion in earnings. The refund is the headline. The tariff hike is the fine print.

The 1987 Constitution, Article II, Section 4:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime duty. Not optional. The state's job is to make sure the country's most essential infrastructure works for the people who pay for it, not to wait three years and then order a slow refund of an overcharge as if that were a favor.

Filipinos who have lived in countries where the grid belongs to the public know the difference in their bones. A dividend arrives because you own something. A refund arrives because you were overcharged and someone finally made them give part of it back.

Eight million households. A small refund line this month. Still not one of them an owner.

Ganun kalala.

If your power company sent you money this year, was it a share of the profit, or just a slice of what they took?
