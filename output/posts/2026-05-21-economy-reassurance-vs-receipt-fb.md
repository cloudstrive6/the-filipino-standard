The economy slowed to its weakest in five years. Prices ran to their fastest in three.

And the official line stayed calm: don't worry, this isn't stagflation, the next quarter will be better.

Here is the gap nobody in a press briefing wants to name.

The numbers came in this month and they were not kind. Q1 2026 growth limped in at 2.8 percent, the slowest pace since the pandemic quarter of early 2021. Inflation hit 7.2 percent in April, the fastest in three years, after climbing month after month from 2 percent in January. The central bank, which had spent the prior stretch cutting, reversed course and started raising rates again, its first hike since October 2023.

Then came the reassurance. The country's chief economist said he does not see stagflation. The President said the economy will perform better in the second quarter. The official inflation forecast for the year was nudged up to 6.3 percent, well above the 2 to 4 percent the central bank is supposed to hold it inside.

Two stories. One on the podium. One at the palengke.

Strictly speaking, the chief economist has a point about the textbook. Stagflation, in the formal definition, needs low growth, high inflation, and high unemployment all at once. Unemployment is still hovering around 5 percent, not spiking. So the academic label is genuinely debatable, and serious economists are debating it.

But notice what the debate quietly does. It moves the whole conversation onto a word, and away from the kitchen table.

Because a household does not experience a definition. It experiences a receipt. The same basket of rice, cooking oil, onions, and jeepney fare that fit the budget last year does not fit it now. The salary did not move 7 percent. The grocery bill did. That difference is not a forecast. It is the missing change at the counter.

And the World Bank already flagged where this lands hardest: weaker household incomes and higher food prices, with the heaviest weight falling on agriculture and construction, the sectors that employ the poorest Filipinos. The slowdown is not shared evenly. It never is.

Here is the deeper pattern, and it is older than this one quarter.

When the data is good, the people in charge take the credit. When the data turns, the same people reach for the calmer word, the better-next-quarter promise, the reminder that it could be worse. The reassurance is not exactly a lie. It is a mood-management technique. Keep the public calm. Buy time. Wait for the cycle to turn so the chart can point up again before anyone has to answer for the dip.

The trouble is that a family cannot eat a better-next-quarter. A sari-sari store owner cannot pay suppliers with a debate over whether the correct word is "slowdown" or "stagflation." The label is a luxury enjoyed by people whose own grocery budget is not the variable under stress.

Filipinos do not need a press conference to confirm what their own receipt already told them. They are not panicking. They are not imagining it. They are reading the same arithmetic they run every single week.

So the next time the message is "relax, the numbers are not as bad as they feel," there is a fair answer.

The numbers feel that way because the receipt says so.
