The budget was not cut.

The money is sitting right there. The government just could not spend it.

In the first three months of 2026, national government infrastructure and capital spending fell 43.5%, down to P147.8 billion from P261.8 billion in the same months a year before. That is barely 11.6% of what was programmed for the entire year. In March alone, capital spending dropped 48%, the third straight month of decline.

Roads, bridges, classrooms, flood control. The line items every politician promises at every election. The funding was approved. About P1.27 trillion is set aside for infrastructure this year. And for three straight months, the spending just kept falling.

So what changed?

Nothing in the budget. What changed was the scrutiny.

The slide started around the middle of 2025, when the corruption scandal over flood-control projects blew open. Since then, the DBM points to lower disbursement at the DPWH and, in its own words, "stricter validation" of billing claims "to ensure project quality and value for money." Translation: somebody finally started checking the receipts before releasing the money.

And the moment somebody started checking, the machine seized up.

Read that again, because it is the whole story. The system was not running smoothly because it was efficient. It was running smoothly because no one was looking too hard. The instant the validation got tighter, the disbursements collapsed by nearly half.

That is the quiet confession buried inside these numbers. For years, the speed of government spending was not a sign of competence. It was a sign of how little friction the money met on the way out the door. Take away the loose validation, and a huge share of the spending simply stops, because it was never built to survive a real check.

Filipinos who have run a legitimate business know exactly what a clean billing process feels like. You submit a claim, it gets verified against the work actually delivered, and you get paid. That should not freeze a country. In functioning systems abroad, honest procurement and steady spending coexist every single day. No country is perfect, and even well-run governments have delays and audits and slow quarters. But the floor is different. Their machine does not grind to a halt the moment you ask it to prove the work was real.

Here, asking for proof was enough to cut the spending almost in half.

There is a version of this story being sold as good news: see, we are cracking down, the slowdown means the cleanup is working. Maybe. But sit with the other half of it. A government that can only move money quickly when nobody is validating it is not a government with a spending problem. It is a government with an honesty problem that it has been calling efficiency for decades.

The roads still are not built. The bridges still stop halfway. The classrooms are still short. And now the country gets to watch how much of its own infrastructure budget was apparently moving on the strength of claims that could not pass a closer look.

The real question is not why spending fell this quarter.

The real question is what was holding it up all the years before.

Ganun kalala.
