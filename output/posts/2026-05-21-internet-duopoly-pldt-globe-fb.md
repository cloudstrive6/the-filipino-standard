# Two telcos pretending to compete

Filipinos do not have a competitive internet market. Filipinos have a duopoly with a polite mascot of competition.

PLDT and Globe together hold the bulk of fixed broadband and mobile subscribers in the country. A third player exists. A few smaller WISPs and fiber providers exist. None of them has changed the price floor in a structural way. The two big players post similar plan prices. They offer similar speeds. They roll out at similar paces. They raise prices on similar cycles.

That is not a coincidence. That is what a duopoly looks like.

Now compare a country that solved this.

New Zealand made a structural choice. The retail telco business was separated from the wholesale fibre network. The fibre layer is operated by Chorus and a few regional fibre companies under regulated terms. Above that layer, more than 30 retail ISPs compete on price, plan design, and service. The same fibre cable can be sold by a dozen different brands. The customer switches by sending an email.

What did that produce? A market where fibre plans for households cost a fraction of the per-Mbps rate that Filipinos pay, where switching is normal, and where any retail ISP that gets greedy loses customers in days, not years.

Nothing about that mechanism is a state secret. The NZ telecommunications market structure is documented on its Commerce Commission website. Every other country thinking about a fibre rollout reads it.

The Philippines did not do this for fixed broadband. The two dominant telcos own the network AND the retail. There is no large independent wholesale layer for them to compete on. Building a competing network from scratch is a billion-peso entry barrier. So the entrants stay small, the duopoly stays large, and the price-per-Mbps stays uncomfortably high.

Ookla's Speedtest Global Index regularly puts PH fixed and mobile speeds below where the GDP-per-capita peer group would predict. The country has been catching up. It is still not where the population's investment would suggest it should be.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own internet complaints. Rural coverage gaps. Slow upgrades in some regions. Occasional pricing controversies. None of that is being romanticised here.

But the FLOOR is different. A New Zealander pays a different brand's bill knowing the underlying infrastructure is regulated to serve them. A Filipino pays a duopolist's bill knowing the underlying infrastructure is owned by the same company sending the bill.

That is not bad luck. That is a regulatory choice.

The Constitution gave the State a tool here. Article XII, Section 19 says the State shall regulate or prohibit monopolies when public interest requires it. The State has chosen not to use the tool for fixed broadband structural separation. The NTC could push the conversation. Congress could legislate the separation. The DICT could publish a roadmap. None of it has happened in any way that has moved the price floor.

Here is the part most subscribers already feel.

Every Filipino household that has tried to switch from one telco to the other knows the experience. New SIM. New router. Same speeds. Same outages. Same call center. Different logo on the bill. The customer pays the price of switching and gets approximately zero of the benefit of competition.

That is the diagnostic. Real competition reduces prices. Cosmetic competition just gives the customer the option to be unhappy with a different brand.

Filipinos deserve a market where their internet bill is shaped by competition, not by the polite agreement between two dominant providers and the long shadow of the spectrum they hold.

Until the structure changes, the bill will not.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
