# The law says your business permit should take 3 days. Reality says otherwise.

On paper, the Philippines writes some of the best business rules in the world.

In practice, it makes the people who follow those rules live the opposite.

The World Bank just put a number on the gap, and it explains almost everything.

In January, the World Bank released its Business Ready (B-Ready) 2025 report. The Philippines landed 53rd out of 101 economies, with an overall score of 61.04, up slightly from 59.81 the year before. Middle of the pack. But the average is hiding the real story.

The report scores three different things: the rules a country writes, the public services it actually delivers, and how smoothly a business can operate day to day.

On the rules, the Philippines scored 73.86. That is 26th in the world. A top-30 rulebook.

On the public services meant to bring those rules to life, it scored 57.82 and slid to 51st. And on the ground-level experience of registering and running a business, the score went down from the year before, not up.

Read that again. The country writes rules like a top-30 economy and delivers them like a bottom-half one.

That gap between the rulebook and the counter window is not a statistical quirk. It is the daily life of every Filipino trying to build something legitimate.

Here is the most concrete proof.

Since 2018, Republic Act 11032, the Ease of Doing Business Act, has been the law of the land. It says a simple government transaction must be processed in 3 working days. Complex ones in 7. The most technical, 20. It even includes an automatic approval rule: if the agency blows the deadline, the application is considered approved.

That is a genuinely good law. On paper, a Filipino entrepreneur in 2026 should clear a simple permit in three days, with the law itself on their side if a clerk decides to stall.

Now talk to anyone who has actually opened a business.

SEC or DTI for the entity. BIR for the TIN and the authority to print receipts. Barangay clearance. Mayor's permit. Sanitary inspection. Fire safety check. Sometimes a locational clearance on top. Thirteen-plus separate steps, different windows, different forms, and the sentence every owner learns to dread: "balik ka na lang bukas."

Compare the floor somewhere else. In New Zealand, registering a company is one online form, about NZD $130, finished in under an hour, with the certificate landing in the inbox before the morning coffee goes cold.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has its own real problems: expensive housing, healthcare wait times in some regions, a steady brain drain to Australia. But a person trying to start a business there is not punished for the act of trying.

This is the part the "ganyan talaga" crowd has backwards.

The Philippines does not have a law problem. It already passed the law. The World Bank data confirms the rules are world-class. What the country does not have is delivery, and that missing delivery is not an accident of capacity.

Every extra step is a fee. Every fee is a small kingdom. Every "come back tomorrow" is leverage. A 3-day deadline that no one enforces is not a failure of ability. It is a decision to keep the friction, because somewhere along the line, the friction pays.

The law was written to remove the gauntlet. The gauntlet survived because the gauntlet earns.

A country that can write a top-30 rulebook can enforce a 3-day permit. It has simply chosen, year after year, not to.

Nasa batas na. Hindi pa rin sinusunod.

If you have ever opened a business in the Philippines, how far past 3 days did your "simple" permit actually take?
