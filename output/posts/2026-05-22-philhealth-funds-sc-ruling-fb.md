# PhilHealth: The Money Was There the Whole Time

Every payday, a slice of every working Filipino's salary disappears into PhilHealth. There is no opt-out.

So here is a fair question: where did a big chunk of that pool actually go?

Not to a hospital. To the National Treasury.

Start with what comes out of the paycheck. In 2026, the PhilHealth premium is 5% of monthly basic salary, split between employer and employee. The worker's half, 2.5%, is deducted automatically, every cut-off, whether or not anyone in the family ever sees the inside of a ward that year. It is mandatory by law for every employed person in the country.

That is the deal Filipinos are sold. Pay in now, be covered later.

Now look at what happened to the money once it was pooled.

In 2024, the Department of Finance issued a circular directing the transfer of 89.9 billion pesos in PhilHealth funds, labeled "excess reserves," to the National Treasury. PhilHealth complied, remitting 60 billion pesos in three tranches. Money collected for the health of Filipinos was moved out of the health fund and into the general pot to help plug the national budget.

This was not a quiet accounting footnote. The Commission on Audit had already flagged that PhilHealth's tens of billions in unused funds could have been spent expanding benefit programs, and that the Reserve Fund was not being managed in full compliance with the Universal Health Care Act. The receipts were on the table. The money existed. It simply was not reaching the people who paid for it.

Then, on December 5, 2025, the Supreme Court ruled. Unanimously.

The Court declared the fund transfer unconstitutional. It struck down the 2024 budget provision and the DOF circular that authorized the sweep, calling them a grave abuse of discretion and, in part, a "rider," a provision that did not even belong in the budget law to begin with. It ordered the 60 billion pesos returned to PhilHealth through the 2026 budget. It permanently barred the transfer of the roughly 29.9 billion pesos still sitting in the balance.

That 2026 budget is being finalized in Congress right now. The restoration is not history. It is happening in the current cycle, in the appropriations being reconciled this month.

Here is the part worth sitting with.

For years, the official story was that the funds were "idle," "excess," reserves too large to leave untouched. But idle is a choice, not a fact of nature. A health fund overflowing while citizens pay out of pocket at the hospital window is not proof of prudence. It is proof that the money was there and the will to spend it on Filipinos was not.

The 1987 Constitution is not vague about whose job this is. Article II, Section 4:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime duty. Not a discretionary line item. Not a reserve to be raided when the budget runs short elsewhere. The first obligation.

A government that treats a health fund as a piggy bank to balance the books has quietly inverted that duty. The people became the source of spare cash, instead of the reason the cash was collected in the first place.

It took the highest court in the land to say what should have been obvious: money paid by Filipinos, for the health of Filipinos, belongs to the health of Filipinos.

The money was never missing.

It was parked. Parked while a family counted out cash at a hospital cashier for a bill PhilHealth was supposed to soften.

Nandiyan lang pala ang pera.
