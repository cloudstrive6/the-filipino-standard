# A salaried employee gets the car loan. A 5-year-old business does not.

In the Philippines, three months of payslips can get a salaried employee a car loan in two weeks.

A five-year-old SME with consistent monthly receipts, audited financials, and a proven track record will sit through eight weeks of follow-ups and still walk out with a "we will get back to you."

That gap is not a story about Filipino entrepreneurs being uncreditworthy. It is a story about a banking system that learned to lend against payslips and never bothered to learn to lend against cash flow.

The dominant model of PH SME lending is collateral and relationships. Hard assets to pledge. A connection inside the branch. A long-time relationship manager who can vouch. A property title to drop on the desk. The actual business performance, the bank statements, the sales records, the steady volume of transactions, all of that ranks below the title deed and below the personal introduction.

This is why a Filipino small business owner often has to mortgage the house to fund the business. The business cannot get a loan on its own merits. So the family asset becomes the cushion. If the business fails, the family loses the house. The risk is not just commercial. It is residential.

Now look at what the law actually says.

The Magna Carta for MSMEs (Republic Act 6977, as amended) requires banks to allocate a portion of their total loan portfolio to MSMEs. Eight per cent for micro and small enterprises. Two per cent for medium. Ten per cent total.

The BSP reports the compliance numbers year after year. The pattern is consistent. A significant share of the banking industry would rather pay the penalty for non-compliance than actually book the loans. The math works for the bank. The penalty is smaller than the perceived hassle and risk of underwriting an SME. So the SMEs keep getting turned away. And the law keeps being honored more in the penalty than in the lending.

There is also a Credit Information Corporation. Set up by law to centralize credit data. It exists. It functions. In theory, a Filipino SME with a clean repayment history can be assessed on data instead of on a relationship. In practice, the adoption inside the banking sector has been slow, uneven, and concentrated among larger players.

Compare a country where SME lending actually works. NZ banks routinely underwrite small businesses on the basis of bank statements, accounting software exports, and clean tax records. The owner does not need to mortgage a house to grow a five-person company. The credit decision can be made on the company, not on the bloodline.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own issues. SME loan rejection rates spike during downturns. Some sectors are still under-served. The system is not utopia. But the FLOOR is "your business can be assessed as a business." That is a different floor.

The Filipino SME is not the problem. The Filipino SME has been the answer to almost every economic shock the country has absorbed for the last 40 years. Through downturns, through peso volatility, through pandemics, through typhoon seasons, it is the small business that kept hiring, kept paying, kept showing up.

The banking system the Filipino SME is forced to ask for credit is the bottleneck. A bottleneck written into the policy of every major bank that chooses the penalty over the loan.

Here is what is at stake. Every SME that cannot access fair credit is an SME that stays small. Every SME that stays small is jobs that never get created, tax revenue that never gets collected, and a generation of Filipino entrepreneurs who keep importing capital instead of mobilizing the country's own.

A country that cannot fund its own small businesses is a country that has decided its growth will be small.

That is a decision. Not destiny.

Dapat ma-access ng Filipino SME ang sariling banking system.
