# The most inconvenient line in the Constitution

The Philippine Constitution opens with eleven words that politicians prefer not to dwell on.

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

Article II, Section 1. The first substantive line in the Declaration of Principles. The line everything else in the document is built on top of.

In plain English, here is what it actually means.

The Filipino people are not the audience of the government. The Filipino people are the source of the government. The president, the senator, the congressman, the governor, the mayor, the barangay captain, all of them hold authority that the citizens loaned them. Not gave. Loaned. Conditional. Revocable at the next election. Constrained by law in between.

Every official, from the highest down, is exercising borrowed authority. The original holder of that authority is the household paying the taxes, the worker punching in at 8 AM, the small business owner queuing at BIR, the farmer in the province, the OFW in Riyadh, the nurse in Auckland. Every Filipino. Together.

That is not a poetic flourish. That is the legal architecture.

A government official can issue orders, but only because the people, through the Constitution, authorized the office to issue orders. A senator can vote on a budget, but only because the people, through the Constitution, authorized the Senate to do that. A president can sign a treaty, but only within the limits the Constitution, written by the people, allowed.

When an official forgets this, the Constitution does not change. The official is the one out of step. Not the citizen.

Here is why politicians find this line uncomfortable.

If sovereignty resides in the people, then "showing respect" to a politician is not a constitutional obligation. It is courtesy. Optional. Withdrawable. The constitutional obligation runs the OTHER way. The state has a duty to the citizen. Not the citizen to the state.

If sovereignty resides in the people, then "tradition" is not a defense for keeping a system that benefits the few at the cost of the many. A republic cannot be inherited like a family business, even if Congress refuses to define dynasties.

If sovereignty resides in the people, then the COA audit findings, the Senate hearing transcripts, the local government budgets, the procurement records, the SALN filings, all of those are not government secrets. They are the people's records. The state holds them in trust. The state has no legal authority to hide them from their actual owners.

The clause does not need amplifying. It simply needs reading.

Article II, Section 1 is the foundation. Everything in the Bill of Rights stands on it. Freedom of speech is meaningful because the speaker is the sovereign. The right to petition the government is meaningful because the petitioner is the original source of the government's authority. Even the anti-dynasty clause in Article II, Section 26 only makes sense as a guard rail to protect the actual sovereign from being quietly displaced by hereditary office-holding.

Most Filipinos have never had this read to them.

Most Filipinos have been raised to think of government as the boss, with citizenship as a kind of polite obedience. The Constitution says the opposite. The Constitution says the relationship is upside-down from how it currently feels.

So the next time an official, at any level, behaves as if the seat belongs to them, remember the line.

The seat does not belong to them.

The seat is on loan from the people who, eleven words into the Constitution, named themselves the sovereign.

Sino ba talaga ang amo dito?
