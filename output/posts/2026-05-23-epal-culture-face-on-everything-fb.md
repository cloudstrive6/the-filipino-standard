# The bench, the streetlamp, and the basketball court walk into a tarpaulin

A Filipino can travel from one barangay to the next and see the same official's face on a bench, a streetlight, a covered court, a tricycle terminal, and a freshly painted gutter.

The official did not personally pay for any of these.

The taxpayer did.

This is epal culture. The habit of stamping a politician's face, name, slogan, and party logo on every public project funded by public money, as if the project was a personal gift from the official rather than the basic delivery of services the office was elected to perform.

The mechanics of the practice are simple. Public funds are appropriated. The project is built or bought. Before the unveiling, the politician's image and name are added to the signage. The official then attends the ribbon-cutting in a barong, hands out plaques, smiles for the local paper. The official's social media team posts the photos. The local news cycle absorbs the appearance. Months later, the constituent who actually paid for the project sees the face every time they pass the bench.

The Constitution has a clear principle on public money. Article VI, Section 29:

> "No money shall be paid out of the Treasury except in pursuance of an appropriation made by law."

The line is about authorization. Public money goes where the law says it goes. The unwritten companion principle, which every developed jurisdiction enforces with discipline, is that public money goes for public purpose. A bench paid for by taxpayers is not a campaign asset. A streetlamp funded by the General Appropriations Act is not a personal brand placement.

In the Philippines, that distinction has been blurred so completely that most citizens no longer notice it. The face on the bench is "normal." The tarpaulin at the rebuilt classroom is "normal." The painted name on the side of the disaster relief truck is "normal." Each individual instance is small. The cumulative cost is enormous.

The cost is not just visual pollution. The cost is the message.

The message of epal culture is built into the design. The bench exists because of the official whose face is on it. Not because of the taxes the household paid. Not because of the law that appropriated the money. Not because of the engineer who designed the road or the worker who poured the concrete. The branding says: vote for this name, re-elect this name, be grateful to this name.

That message is the opposite of what a democratic state is supposed to teach its citizens.

A democratic state is supposed to teach its citizens that the road is theirs. The bench is theirs. The covered court is theirs. The funds came from them. The official who arranged the project was doing their job, not performing a favor.

Multiple Anti-Epal bills have been filed in the Senate and the House across several Congresses. Most have stalled. A few DBM and DPWH circulars have tried to limit personal political branding on infrastructure signage. The compliance has been wildly uneven. The local politician keeps the tarpaulin up because the local politician has not yet been punished for keeping it up.

Here is the reframe that makes the practice intolerable once you see it.

Epal culture is taxpayer-funded personal advertising. In a private business context, a manager who used company money to print their own face on company products would be fired by the end of the quarter. In a public-sector context, the equivalent behavior is rewarded with re-election.

That is not a glitch. That is the design of a system where the line between personal brand and public office has been allowed to dissolve. The dissolution benefits incumbents. It costs everyone else, twice. Once in tax money. Once in the slow erosion of the idea that public services are a right, not a gift.

Sovereignty resides in the people. The roads, the lamps, the benches, the courts, all of them belong to the people who paid for them. The official who arranged the project does not own them. The official is allowed to put up a small "constructed in YYYY under the YYYY-YYYY administration" plaque, modest, factual, undecorated. That is enough.

The face does not need to be there. The face has never needed to be there.

Sinong nagbabayad? Hindi sila.
