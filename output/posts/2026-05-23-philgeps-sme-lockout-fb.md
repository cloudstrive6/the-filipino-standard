# Public money, private pool

In the Philippines, public procurement looks competitive on the surface.

There is a portal. There is a law. There is a published bidding process. There are deadlines, criteria, and an evaluation committee that signs the award.

Underneath, public procurement is one of the most consistently captured systems in the country.

The Government Procurement Reform Act, Republic Act 9184, set up PhilGEPS as the central electronic portal. The intent was good. Visible bidding. Documented decisions. A trail any citizen could follow.

The execution has a small problem at the front gate.

Before any SME can submit a bid, the SME has to clear pre-qualification. The list of requirements is long and individually defensible. A track record of comparable contracts. Audited financial statements for the last several years. A performance bond. Business permits. PhilGEPS Platinum membership. Sector-specific accreditation. Sometimes a minimum paid-up capital. Sometimes a credit line letter from a bank.

Take each criterion alone and it sounds prudent.

Stack them together and the result is a wall. The five-person Filipino SME that could actually deliver a small batch of office furniture, a barangay health center renovation, or a localized IT service contract is filtered out before evaluation begins. Not on quality. Not on price. On the front-page checklist.

Who clears the wall? The same firms that have cleared it for years. The track record is already there because the contracts were already won. The bond is easy because the relationship with the surety company already exists. The audited financials are clean because the firm is large enough to afford a big-name auditor. The accreditation is renewed because the team that handles it is full-time staff. The cycle reinforces itself.

A new entrant cannot accumulate a track record of public contracts without first winning a public contract that they cannot bid for because they have no track record. It is a closed loop. It looks like meritocracy. It functions like a private pool.

Public procurement is a meaningful share of GDP in any country. The decisions about who gets to deliver to government shape the shape of an entire SME ecosystem. A country that consistently routes its procurement to the same handful of large players ends up with exactly the SME landscape one would expect: small businesses that cannot scale beyond private markets, large incumbents that grow on a steady diet of guaranteed public contracts, and a public sector that pays prices set by limited competition.

The Constitution gave the State a posture on this. Article II, Section 26:

> "The State shall guarantee equal access to opportunities for public service."

"Equal access" was not written about office buildings. It was written about the structural openness of public opportunity. A procurement system that effectively pre-qualifies only a small cluster of well-connected firms is not providing equal access. It is providing equal pageantry.

There are fixes. Reserved windows for MSMEs on contracts below certain thresholds, with relaxed bond requirements. Unbundled lot sizes that match what SMEs can actually deliver. A separate accreditation track that rewards demonstrated quality on private-sector contracts instead of only public ones. A graduated bond requirement that does not require a small firm to lock up half a year of working capital to bid on a single tender.

Other countries do versions of all of this. The EU has SME-targeted procurement frameworks. Australia and NZ run government panels designed to let small suppliers reach public buyers. None of this is exotic. None of it requires the Philippines to invent anything.

What it requires is willingness. And the willingness has not arrived because the current arrangement works very well for the firms that already cleared the wall.

Public procurement is public money. It is supposed to flow back into the economy through fair competition. When it flows back into the same private pool every cycle, the public is paying a hidden tax called concentration, and the SME sector is starved of the one thing it cannot manufacture on its own: a government willing to be its customer.

Filipino SMEs can deliver. The wall is the problem, not the supplier.

Dapat buksan ang pintuan.
