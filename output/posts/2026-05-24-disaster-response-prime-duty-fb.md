The Philippines is the most disaster-exposed country on earth. Not one of the most. The most.

Three years running, the WorldRiskIndex has ranked it number one.

And every typhoon season, the country still acts surprised.

About 20 tropical cyclones enter Philippine waters every year. Eight or nine of them make landfall. That is not a forecast. That is a calendar.

As the wet season opens, PAGASA has already issued an El Niño Alert for the months ahead, with fewer but stronger storms expected as it develops. The country knows what is coming, roughly when, and roughly how hard.

A government that knows the disaster arrives on schedule every single year has no honest excuse to treat it like an ambush.

Look at what happens after the storm passes.

Yolanda made landfall on November 8, 2013. It killed at least 6,300 people, the deadliest typhoon in modern Philippine history.

More than a decade later, the housing promised to its survivors was still not finished. As of December 2024, the National Housing Authority reported around 175,728 units completed, with roughly 14,072 still pending and pushed to the end of 2025. Independent reporting found that years after the storm, about 15% of the units were unfinished and thousands stood empty. The Commission on Audit flagged the agency for delays and unliquidated funds.

Eleven years. Survivors still waiting on a roof that was promised the same year the storm took the old one.

Now look at how rebuilding moves when the state treats it as the job.

When the Canterbury earthquakes flattened parts of Christchurch in New Zealand in 2010 and 2011, the central government and the city signed a cost-sharing agreement: NZD 2.9 billion from the national government, NZD 1.9 billion from the council, against a total rebuild estimated above NZD 40 billion. Within roughly a decade, about 900 community assets like pools, libraries, and community centres were repaired or rebuilt, alongside 75 restored heritage buildings and more than 1,000 council social-housing units.

No country is perfect. The Christchurch rebuild ran over budget, took longer than promised, and a few anchor projects are still being argued over in 2026. New Zealanders complained about every delay, loudly, in public, with receipts.

That complaint is the whole point. The rebuild was treated as an obligation the state owed, not a favor it performed when the cameras showed up.

Here is what nobody says out loud.

The difference is not money, and it is not the weather. The Philippines does not get fewer storms by calling itself unlucky.

The difference is whether disaster recovery is treated as a duty the state owes its people, or a kindness it grants on its own schedule.

The 1987 Constitution already settled which one it is supposed to be. Article II, Section 4:

"The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime duty. Not a relief photo op. Not a ribbon-cutting eleven years late. The first obligation of the state, written into the document it was built on.

And every year, the gap between that line and the reality gets called by a gentler name. Resilience. Bayanihan. Pride in smiling through the flood.

Those are real, and they are beautiful. But they were never meant to replace a government that does its job. A people should not have to be the most resilient on earth because their state is among the least prepared.

The storms are coming. They always do. The only open question is whether the country keeps treating the predictable as a surprise.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa ganito.

If you have lived through a storm the whole country saw coming, what did your barangay actually get afterward, and how long did it take?
