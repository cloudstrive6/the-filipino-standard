# Where does the PhilHealth contribution actually go?

Every working Filipino pays into PhilHealth every payday.

The deduction is automatic. The amount has been climbing over the years as contribution rates were raised under the Universal Health Care Act. Employers match. Employees contribute. Self-employed Filipinos pay quarterly. OFWs pay too.

The money goes in. Reliably. Every cycle. For decades now.

The question Filipino families keep asking, with very good reason, is what happens after that.

The Universal Health Care Act, Republic Act 11223, automatically enrols every Filipino in PhilHealth. The law promised expanded coverage, simpler claims, and a real safety net at the hospital cashier window. The law was a serious step. The execution has been more complicated.

Here is what the receipts at the cashier still say.

Filipino out-of-pocket health spending, the share of total health spending paid directly by households at the point of care, remains among the highest in ASEAN. WHO and World Bank country data have shown this for years. The PhilHealth coverage is real. It is also, for most major hospitalizations, partial. The patient is discharged, hands over the PhilHealth slip, gets a deduction, and still receives a bill the family then has to cover.

That is not the experience of citizens in countries where health insurance has been built well. In a properly functioning system, the patient should leave the hospital owing little to nothing for covered conditions. In PH, the patient leaves owing the difference between what was promised and what was delivered. The contribution covered the gap that was supposed to be closed by the contribution.

Now layer in what the Commission on Audit has been saying.

COA's annual audits of PhilHealth across multiple administrations have flagged the corporation on fund handling issues, payment delays to hospitals, reserve fund management questions, and gaps in internal controls. The reports are public. The findings are detailed. The corporate response has typically been polite. The structural pattern has continued.

Hospitals across the country, including charity hospitals, have at various points complained publicly about delayed reimbursements. A hospital that is owed reimbursement and cannot collect it on time becomes a hospital that is more cautious about admitting future PhilHealth patients. The cost of the delay ends up where it always ends up. With the patient.

The Constitution did not leave this ambiguous. Article II, Section 15:

> "The State shall protect and promote the right to health of the people and instill health consciousness among them."

Protect. Promote. The State, not the family savings account. The State, not the GoFundMe page that goes around when a relative is rushed to the hospital. The State, which is supposed to be the entity collecting the contributions for exactly this purpose.

Here is the part that most Filipinos already feel.

The contribution feels like a tax. It does not feel like an insurance premium. A real premium pays for a real benefit when the bad day arrives. The current PhilHealth experience for the average household is closer to "the contribution was collected, the benefit was partial, the bill was personal." That gap is the diagnosis.

The fix is not "abolish PhilHealth." The fix is to make PhilHealth do what the law said it would do. Cover more. Pay hospitals on time. Reduce the patient's residual share. Audit, report, correct, repeat. The mechanism is in the law. The political will to execute it has not consistently been there.

Other countries collect the same kind of contribution and the patient walks out of the hospital without an invoice. That is not a fantasy. It is the standard outcome in NZ public hospitals, in much of Europe, in Australia for citizens, in Japan for the insured. The Philippines, with a Universal Health Care Act on the books, can land closer to that outcome. It has chosen not to land there yet.

Filipino workers have paid in. The question now is whether the State will pay out.
