# Two classrooms, same age children, two completely different countries

In one classroom, eighteen ten-year-olds sit at their own desks. The teacher knows every name, every reading level, every household situation that might explain why a child is quiet today.

In another classroom, fifty-two ten-year-olds share double desks. Two children per chair. The teacher has been awake since 4 AM and still has the afternoon shift to teach. The bell will ring in 38 minutes and a different fifty children will take the same seats.

Both children are ten. One country chose to fund the difference. The other did not.

New Zealand has spent decades trying to make funding follow the child who needs it most. For years the mechanism was called the decile system. Since 2023, it has been replaced by the Equity Index, which uses more granular data on student backgrounds to direct extra funding to schools serving the highest-need communities. The mechanism is not perfect and Kiwis argue about it. The principle is intact: the harder a school's intake, the more public money the school gets to do the job.

The Philippines has a Constitution that is equally explicit. Article XIV, Section 1:

> "The State shall protect and promote the right of all citizens to quality education at all levels and shall take appropriate steps to make such education accessible to all."

Read the verb. Promote. Take appropriate steps. Make accessible. Not "if budget permits." Not "in the medium term." Now.

What is actually happening in PH public schools, especially in urban congested areas, is structurally different from what the Constitution promised.

The classroom backlog has been measured in the tens of thousands for years under multiple administrations. DepEd has acknowledged the gap. Multi-shift scheduling, where two batches of students use the same classrooms on the same day, is the workaround. The morning shift starts before sunrise. The afternoon shift ends after sunset. The teacher takes both. The classroom never gets a full clean. The students get half the contact hours of a child in any country that funded the build-out.

Pupil-teacher ratios in many PH urban public schools regularly exceed 40 to 1. Documented spikes above 50 to 1 are common in the densest districts. A teacher with 50 students cannot give individual feedback the way a teacher with 18 students can. That is not a teacher problem. That is an arithmetic problem the system has refused to solve.

No country is perfect. NZ education has its own issues. Rural school resourcing remains uneven. Teacher pay has been the subject of repeated strikes. Special needs support is not enough in many regions. The Equity Index itself is still being debated. None of that is being romanticised here.

But the FLOOR is different. The Kiwi parent dropping a child off at a low-decile school knows the school is getting more money per student than a wealthier school across town. The Filipino parent dropping a child off at a Manila or Quezon City public school knows the classroom is borrowed from another batch of students who will arrive in the afternoon.

The deeper consequence is intergenerational. A child who learns in a 50-pupil shift with stressed teachers does not get the same launch pad as a child in a 20-pupil classroom with regular one-on-one time. Over twelve years of basic education, the gap compounds. The Philippines is producing a generation of bright students who, through no fault of their own, have been short-changed of the very thing the Constitution promised them.

Filipino teachers are not the problem. Filipino teachers, on consistently below-regional salaries, with the largest classes in ASEAN, with the longest unpaid administrative load, are still producing students who win international competitions, run hospitals abroad, and code for companies in three continents. They are heroes in the same way a soldier holding a position with no ammunition is a hero. The country should stop romanticising the heroism long enough to send the ammunition.

The fix is not glamorous. Build the classrooms. Hire the teachers. Reduce the ratios. Direct more funding to the schools serving the hardest communities. Fund the meal programs that keep children alert. Stop treating education as a discretionary line item the budget can squeeze.

Filipino children deserve a classroom that is not borrowed. Not because they migrated. Because they are citizens of a republic that already promised them this.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
