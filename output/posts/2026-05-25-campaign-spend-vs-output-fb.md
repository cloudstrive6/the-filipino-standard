# Hundreds of millions to win the seat. Almost nothing to use it.

A Philippine senatorial campaign routinely costs hundreds of millions of pesos.

The Statement of Contributions and Expenditures filed with COMELEC after each election rarely reflects the real spend. The tarpaulins, the convoys, the sound trucks, the radio buys, the ad placements, the field operations, the meals, the venues, the gifts, the per-cycle "thank you" tokens to local mayors, the volunteers who somehow always have shirts and snacks and transport, none of it is free. Researchers who track political finance (PCIJ, Stratbase, IDEA Philippines, KAS Philippines) have consistently shown the real number runs far above the legal cap.

After spending hundreds of millions to win the seat, the average senator's measurable legislative output is staggeringly thin.

The bills filed are many. The bills passed are few. The bills passed that materially improve a Filipino's life are fewer still. Multiple Congresses have closed with single-digit counts of priority laws actually enacted, against hundreds filed for headlines. The hearings produce content. The content produces clips. The clips produce engagement. The country produces almost no new policy.

That gap is the diagnosis.

A normal incentive structure rewards people for the output. A senator paid hundreds of millions of pesos worth of "investment" by donors should, by any rational accounting, produce hundreds of millions of pesos worth of legislative work in return. Most do not. The work is the slowest part of the job. The visibility is the fastest. So the slowest part shrinks, and the fastest part grows.

What the senator IS paid for, in practice, is visibility. Photo ops. Senate hearing clips. Press releases. Position papers issued for the news cycle. The seat is a media asset before it is a legislative office. The donors know this. The voters are taught to accept this.

Here is the structural reason the country tolerates this trade.

The Filipino voter is being asked, every three years, to make a hiring decision with almost no information about job performance. There is no public scoreboard. Most newspapers do not run a senator's bill-passage stats next to their re-election bid. Most barangays do not have a forum where the incumbent has to answer for legislative output. The only data the voter typically sees is what the incumbent's own communications team puts in front of them.

That is not a meritocracy. That is a brand-building competition.

Imagine a private company that paid an executive hundreds of millions of pesos a year to produce one or two memos and a couple of press releases. The board would intervene by the second quarter. In the public sector, the "board" is the electorate, and the electorate has been deprived of the data it would need to fire underperformers. So they get re-elected. They re-fundraise. They re-spend. They re-photograph.

The Constitution gave COMELEC a tool here. Article IX-C, Section 2(7) authorizes COMELEC to recommend measures to minimize election spending. The tool exists. The application has been gentle. Spending caps remain low on paper. The real spend remains high in practice. Nothing in the gap is a mystery.

What is missing is enforcement, and the political will to enforce. Enforcement would mean disqualifying overspenders. Enforcement would mean unsealing SOCE filings to actual public scrutiny. Enforcement would mean prosecuting clear cases of campaign finance violation. None of these are exotic. All of them are uncomfortable for the people who would have to enact them, because those people are the people who currently benefit from the gap.

The Filipino voter is not at fault for being kept in the dark.

The Filipino voter is, however, the only entity in the system that can demand the lights be turned on. That demand starts in the household conversation. It travels through media that refuses to be polite about the math. It ends, if it ends well, in a voter who walks into the booth with a scoreboard in their hand and a question that no campaign budget can outspend: "What did you actually pass?"

Sovereignty resides in the people. The seat the senator is sitting in was loaned by the voter. The loan can be recalled at the next election. The condition of the loan, written or unwritten, is that the office produces work for the country, not a personal media platform funded by donors and audited by no one.

If the seat costs hundreds of millions and produces almost nothing, the seat is not the asset. The hiring process is the problem.

Eh ano ngayon? Bayad ng tao yan.
