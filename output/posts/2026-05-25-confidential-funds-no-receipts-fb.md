Every peso in the national budget comes with a receipt.

Except for the billions that don't.

This year, the 2026 national budget set aside around P11.8 billion in what the government calls "confidential" and "intelligence" funds. The Office of the President alone received about P4.5 billion of that, roughly 38 percent of the entire pot.

Here is what makes these funds different from the rest of the budget. Almost every other line item has to be disclosed, itemized, and audited in the open. Confidential and intelligence funds are the one category written so that the public is not allowed to see where the money actually went. The label itself is the loophole.

The defense is always the same: national security, sensitive operations, things citizens supposedly cannot be trusted to know. And there are genuinely sensitive activities that need discretion. Nobody serious disputes that.

But look at what the receipts that DO surface keep showing.

The Commission on Audit, the body whose entire job is to check the math, has spent the last few years issuing notice after notice of disallowance on confidential funds. In one running case, COA ordered the return of P375 million in confidential spending, three cash advances of P125 million each, because the documentation did not hold up. Stack that on an earlier P73-million disallowance and the disallowed total climbs to roughly P448 million.

COA's own signed guidelines say the quiet part plainly: confidential funds must be spent strictly on legitimate intelligence activities and must be backed by adequate documentation, even when some details stay classified. Classified is not the same as undocumented. Secret is not the same as unaccountable.

Here is what nobody in the budget briefings says out loud.

This is not a new invention. It is the pork barrel wearing a better suit. The country watched PDAF get struck down. It watched the DAP controversy. Every time one mechanism for discretionary, hard-to-trace public money gets exposed, another one quietly takes its place under a more respectable name. Priority development. Intelligence. Confidential. Different label, same architecture: a pile of public money that the public is structurally prevented from following.

The 1987 Constitution does not leave this ambiguous. Article II, Section 1:

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

Read it slowly. If all government authority emanates from the people, then it is the people's money being spent, by people the public hired, on the public's behalf. A citizen being told "you funded this, but you are not allowed to know what it bought" is not a national security posture. It is the relationship between a republic and its owners turned upside down.

And this is the part worth sitting with. Filipinos are not asking for state secrets. They are asking for the same thing any reasonable employer asks of anyone handling their money: show the receipts, or return the cash. COA already says exactly that. The disallowances prove the standard exists. The fight is over whether anyone is made to follow it.

So the question is not whether intelligence work needs some discretion. Of course it does.

The question is why "confidential" has quietly become the one word in the entire budget that means "no questions, no receipts, no consequences."

Public money with a classified stamp is still public money.

Saan napupunta ang pera ng taumbayan?

If your household had to account for every peso this year, and the most powerful offices in the country did not, what does that tell you about who the system was actually built to serve?
