# What "raising the standard" actually means

The tagline of this page is "raising the standard for Filipino governance."

Worth pausing on what that actually means. Because it does not mean perfection. It does not mean copying another country. It does not mean saying the Philippines is hopeless. It does not mean migrating. It does not mean a slogan on a tarpaulin.

It means raising the FLOOR.

The floor is the baseline a republic is supposed to deliver to its citizens. Not the ceiling. Not the showroom. The boring, ordinary, daily-life things a functional country quietly provides, that the Filipino has been quietly going without for decades and being asked to call it normal.

Raising the standard means: a Filipino family should be able to take a sick child to the hospital and not lose a month's salary at the cashier window.

Raising the standard means: a Filipino entrepreneur should be able to register a small business in days, not weeks, without queuing through eleven agencies and four unofficial fees.

Raising the standard means: a Filipino commuter should be able to get to work knowing the train will arrive within a known window, and a breakdown should not be a national news event.

Raising the standard means: a Filipino taxpayer should be able to file an annual return online in under an hour, without paying anyone "for facilitation."

Raising the standard means: a Filipino school child should sit in a classroom built for them, not borrowed from another batch of children on a different shift.

Raising the standard means: a Filipino voter should walk into a polling place where the ballot does not list five members of the same family.

Raising the standard means: a Filipino household should pay their electric bill knowing the company they are paying is accountable to them, not just to a private shareholder base.

Raising the standard means: a Filipino professional abroad should be able to come home and find a country that actually wants to retain them, not one that quietly assumes they will leave again.

None of these are utopian. Every single one is already happening in countries with smaller populations, smaller economies, and worse weather than the Philippines.

This is the part the system has worked very hard to make Filipinos forget.

The Philippines is not technologically incapable. The Philippines is not geographically cursed. The Philippines is not too big, too poor, or too "different" to deliver what other countries deliver. The pilots exist. The talent exists. The capacity exists. What has not consistently existed is the political will, at the very top, to make the floor real.

That is a fixable problem. Fixable problems require pressure.

Pressure does not start in Malacanang. Pressure starts in the kitchen conversation. Then the family chat. Then the neighborhood barangay meeting. Then the voter who refuses to vote for a name they have been told to vote for. Then the journalist who refuses to soften a story. Then the small business owner who refuses to pay an unofficial fee. Then the teacher who refuses to call 50 students per class "manageable." Then the OFW who refuses to call themselves a "modern hero" while quietly absorbing the cost of the country not building a life worth coming home to.

The Constitution opens with a sentence written about exactly this:

> "We, the sovereign Filipino people..."

Sovereign. Not subjects. Not customers. Not audience. Sovereign.

The sovereign is the one who sets the standard. The state delivers it. When the state under-delivers, the sovereign is supposed to demand better. That is not rude. That is not anti-Filipino. That is what citizenship is supposed to mean.

The Filipino Standard is not asking for miracles. The Filipino Standard is asking for the floor. The same floor a smaller, less populated, less talented country has already laid for its own citizens.

If Filipinos can build that floor anywhere else in the world, and they can, they have, they do every day in dozens of countries, then Filipinos can demand it at home.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.

That is the standard.
