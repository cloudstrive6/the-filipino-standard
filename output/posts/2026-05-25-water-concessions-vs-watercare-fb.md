A pipe burst in Ermita this week, and half of Manila lost water overnight.

The repair is routine. Pipes fail everywhere, in every country.

The part worth looking at is who owns the pipe, and where your water bill actually goes.

---

From the night of May 24 into the afternoon of May 25, a damaged 750mm Maynilad line near Antonio Villegas Street shut off service across Manila's Districts 1 to 6. Tankers rolled out. Parts of Las Pinas, Paranaque, Pasay, and several Cavite towns went dry in the same window.

For most households this was a long, sweaty inconvenience. Nothing more. Infrastructure ages. Lines break. That is normal.

What is not talked about is the structure sitting behind that tap.

Metro Manila's water has not been a public service since 1997. That year, under Republic Act 8041, the "Water Crisis Act," the government handed MWSS to two private concessionaires. Manila Water took the East Zone. Maynilad took the West. They also absorbed the agency's debt, and they were handed something close to a monopoly over a thing no household can refuse to buy.

Twenty-eight years later, the owners have changed but the model has not. Maynilad now sits under Metro Pacific Investments. Manila Water now sits under Enrique Razon's Trident Water. Two private groups, two captive zones, millions of households with no other tap to turn to.

And the bill keeps climbing on schedule.

On January 1, 2026, the fourth tranche of the 2023 to 2027 rate rebasing took effect. Manila Water went up P8.39 per cubic meter. Maynilad went up P2.15. A Manila Water household using 30 cubic meters now pays roughly P135 more every month. Built into that increase is a line item called a "foreign currency differential," which is a polite way of saying the public absorbs the risk of the company's foreign loans.

Now compare who owns the water somewhere it is run as a service instead of a franchise.

In Auckland, water is run by Watercare. It is 100% owned by the Auckland Council. By law it is required to be a minimum-cost, cost-efficient provider. It pays no dividend to anyone, because there is no private shareholder waiting at the end of the bill. Every dollar a household pays goes back into the pipes, the treatment plants, and the network. At least twice a year, the board has to meet in public.

No country is perfect, and Auckland is not a postcard. It went through a serious drought in 2020 with real water restrictions, it has aging pipes of its own, and it has taken on heavy debt to fund upgrades. Its water is not free and not flawless.

But notice the one structural difference that changes everything. When a pipe bursts in Auckland, the money to fix it and the money you paid both stay inside a not-for-profit that owes nothing to investors. When a pipe bursts in Manila, the same household pays a regulated rate engineered to guarantee a private group its return, plus the currency risk on that group's debt.

That is the whole game. Not the burst pipe. The cap table behind it.

A public utility is built to break even while serving people. A concession is built to profit while serving people. Both deliver water. Only one is designed to send the surplus back to the household instead of past it.

The Constitution is not shy about which one is supposed to be the default. Article II, Section 4:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Clean water is not a premium add-on to that duty. It is the floor of it. A government that contracts out its prime duty for thirty years and lets the price ride on a "foreign currency differential" has not solved a water crisis. It has rented out the one service nobody can live without.

So the next time the tap runs dry and the tanker shows up, the question is not only when the water comes back.

Saan ba talaga napupunta ang binabayad natin?

If you are in Metro Manila, pull up your last water bill and find the line that has nothing to do with water.
