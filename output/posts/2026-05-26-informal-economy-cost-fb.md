Most Filipino businesses never appear on any official list.

Not because the owners are hiding from something.

Because in the Philippines, being formal often costs more than staying invisible.

The micro-enterprises are the real economy here. The sari-sari store on the corner. The palengke vendor. The tricycle operator. The online seller running everything from a phone. The freelancer juggling three clients. By sheer headcount, MSMEs, and especially the micro tier, are how most Filipinos actually make a living.

And a huge share of them operate off the books. In May 2025, the Philippine Statistics Authority counted self-employed Filipinos with no paid employee at almost 28 percent of all workers, plus another 7.5 percent in unpaid family work. Put together, roughly a third of employed Filipinos are working outside a normal wage-and-salary arrangement. A large slice of that activity never touches a registration form.

The instinct is to call this a discipline problem. It is not.

Staying informal is a rational response to what formality costs. Registering means the gauntlet of permits and the BIR filing calendar. It means time that a one-person business does not have, and fees before the first peso of profit. So many owners do the math and quietly choose to stay small and invisible.

Here is the part that should bother everyone.

Invisible has a ceiling. A business with no registration cannot open a proper business bank account. It struggles to get formal credit. It gets blocked from the e-commerce platforms that require a registered seller. It is shut out of bidding for any government contract. The informal business is not just unregistered. It is capped.

So the system produces a strange outcome. The people working hardest, taking the most personal risk, are nudged into the exact corner where they can never scale. The friction that was supposed to organize the economy ends up freezing it.

That is not a Filipino failing. It is a design failure. A government that makes it cheaper to stay invisible than to grow is not protecting its smallest businesses. It is quietly holding them down.

The fix is not lectures about formalizing. The fix is making formal the easier, cheaper, faster choice. Other countries decided that years ago.

Hindi dapat parusa ang pagiging legal.

If you run something small, what was the one requirement that nearly made you give up?
