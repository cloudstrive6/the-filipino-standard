In New Zealand, most salaried workers never file a tax return at all.

The system already knows what they earned, and it squares the rest up for them.

In the Philippines, doing taxes can cost a small business owner entire working days, a folder full of forms, and a queue that starts before the office even opens.

Both governments tax their people. That part is the same everywhere. The difference is what each government assumes about the person standing on the other side of the counter.

New Zealand's Inland Revenue runs an online portal called myIR. A freelancer or a business owner logs in, files, and pays in minutes. For most wage earners, tax comes out automatically through PAYE and the year-end is settled without them lifting a finger. The built-in assumption is that a citizen's time is worth protecting.

The Bureau of Internal Revenue assumes something closer to the opposite. Books of account to register and stamp. Authority to print official receipts and sales invoices. Quarterly percentage or VAT filings. Withholding tax. Then the annual income tax return on top. Each step with its own form, its own window, and its own version of "come back tomorrow."

None of this is because Filipinos are worse with paperwork. It is because the system was built around the queue, not around the citizen.

A wage earner in Auckland can finish an entire tax year having spent zero hours on it. A sari-sari store owner or a small contractor in Metro Manila can lose full working days to compliance. Days they are not earning. Days the business sits closed.

Here is what nobody says out loud. A government tells you exactly what it thinks of you by how it designs the most boring interaction you will ever have with it.

When filing takes ten minutes, paying tax feels like a normal part of being an adult. When filing takes three trips and a day in line, tax avoidance stops looking like a crime and starts looking like self-defense. The friction does not only waste time. It quietly teaches a whole country that the state is something to be endured, not something that works for them.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has its own problems, from punishing housing costs to long waits in parts of its health system. But the floor for an ordinary taxpayer sits on a different planet from what Filipinos are raised to accept as normal.

A tax system can respect your time, or it can waste it. One is a choice. So is the other.
