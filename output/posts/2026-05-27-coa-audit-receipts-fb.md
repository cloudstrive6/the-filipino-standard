Every year, the Commission on Audit hands the country a stack of receipts.

Billions flagged. Findings detailed. Agencies named.

And almost every year, the stack lands on a shelf and nothing moves.

The Commission on Audit is the independent auditor written into the Constitution. Its job is to follow public money and report back on where it actually went. It does this. It issues notices of suspension and disallowance. It publishes Annual Audit Reports that name agencies and amounts. The Department of Education alone recently had roughly two billion pesos of spending tagged as disallowances, subject to appeal all the way up to the Supreme Court.

So the detection works. The country is not flying blind. The receipts exist.

Here is what nobody says out loud. Catching it is not the same as fixing it.

The Supreme Court has clarified that COA's audit powers do not include imposing administrative penalties. In plain terms, COA can find the problem and write it down, but it cannot, by itself, make anyone pay for it. The consequence depends entirely on other offices choosing to act. And too often, they simply don't. The disallowance gets appealed for years. The finding ages. The news cycle forgets. The next year's report arrives and joins the pile.

That is the real scandal, and it is quieter than any single number. It is not that the corruption is hidden. It is that it is documented, public, and tolerated anyway.

The 1987 Constitution is blunt about whose money this is. Article II, Section 1:

"Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

Every peso in those reports is the people's. The audit is, in effect, a letter addressed to the owner. The owner almost never gets told what it says.

Transparency was supposed to be the cure. But transparency without consequence is just paperwork. A system that sees everything, records everything, and changes nothing has not failed by accident. It has been allowed to work exactly this way.

The receipts are public. The consequences are not.

Saan napupunta ang pera natin?

When was the last time a COA finding actually led to someone being held accountable?
