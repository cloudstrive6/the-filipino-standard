Shopee and Lazada put more Filipino businesses online than any government program ever has.

Then, quietly, they started sending the bill.

And the bill only moves in one direction.

Credit where it is due first. For millions of Filipino MSMEs, the platforms were the on-ramp the state never built. An instant storefront. Payments that actually worked. Logistics handled. Real buyers, on day one. No Negosyo Center, no DTI brochure, ever came close to that. The platforms did in a few years what decades of "MSME support" only talked about.

But the terms of that deal keep changing, always in the platform's favor.

Through 2025, the take rate climbed. Shopee raised marketplace commissions on popular categories, set its platform shipping fee at 5.6 percent capped at 100 pesos per item, and scrapped the 1 percent fast-shipping discount. From September, both Shopee and Lazada added a flat 5-peso processing fee on every single completed order, no matter how small. Lazada's mall commissions climbed to around 11 percent.

Put it together and the platform's own sample said it plainly: on a 1,000-peso sale, roughly 128 pesos disappears in fees before the seller has paid for the product, the packaging, or their own time. About 13 percent, gone off the top.

Here is the part that matters more than any percentage.

On the platform, you do not own the customer. You rent them. The algorithm decides who sees your store. Staying visible increasingly means buying ads on top of the commissions. The buyer who loved your product is the platform's contact, not yours. You can pour years into building something real and still not hold the keys to the house it lives in.

This is the same pattern The Filipino Standard keeps running into. A handful of dominant players. Costs that only ratchet upward. And the smallest builder, the one taking all the actual risk, absorbing every increase.

The platforms are not villains for charging. They are businesses. The problem is dependency with no alternative. When there is no realistic way to reach customers outside two apps, "take it or leave it" is not really a choice.

Renting a storefront an algorithm can empty overnight is not the same as owning a business.

Hindi mo tunay na pag-aari ang negosyong nasa kamay ng iba.

If you sell online, how much of every sale actually reaches you after the platform takes its cut?
