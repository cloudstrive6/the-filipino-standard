A middle-class family in Metro Manila would need to save every single peso they earn for almost twenty years to afford a median condominium.

Not the down payment. The whole unit.

And that is the version that counts as the affordable option.

The math is brutal once you look at it directly. In 2025, a median condo in Metro Manila costs around 19.8 times a median household's entire annual income. The benchmark economists use for healthy, affordable housing is around five times income. Manila is nearly four times past that line. Townhouses are worse, sitting around 33 times annual income.

For context, even Los Angeles, one of the most expensive cities in the United States, runs at roughly 10 to 12 times income. Metro Manila is about double that. This is not a story about Filipinos buying beyond their means. It is a story about a market that has floated away from the people it is supposed to house.

And it is getting structurally worse, not better.

Since 2019, household incomes in the National Capital Region grew by about 20 percent. Over the same stretch, residential property prices rose by about 62 percent. Incomes walked. Prices sprinted. The gap between what a family earns and what a home costs widens a little more every year, no matter how disciplined that family is.

Here is what nobody likes to admit. No amount of personal budgeting closes a 20x gap. You cannot skip enough milk tea to out-save a market moving three times faster than your salary. When the shortfall is this large and this structural, "just save harder" stops being advice and starts being a way to blame people for a problem built above their heads.

A functioning country treats a gap like this as an emergency. It builds public and social housing at real scale. It tracks whether wages and shelter are drifting apart and intervenes when they do.

Even New Zealand, which has a genuine housing affordability crisis of its own, is having a loud, constant national argument about it. No country is perfect. The difference is whether a country treats unaffordable housing as a crisis to fix or a fact to accept.

A home should be a floor to stand on, not a finish line you never reach.

Hindi dapat panaginip na lang ang sariling tahanan.

If you are renting or saving right now, how far does owning a home actually feel from where you stand?
