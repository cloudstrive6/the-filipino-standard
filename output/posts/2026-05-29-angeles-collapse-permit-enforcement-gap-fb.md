# FB Long-Form Post - Angeles Collapse: The Permit Enforcement Gap

**Pillar:** 6 (Business & SME Advocacy) + 1 (Governance Comparison) + 5 (Filipino Empowerment)
**Type:** Hybrid
**Platform:** Facebook (primary)
**Scheduled publish:** May 29, 2026, 12:00 PM PHT

---

A sari-sari store owner in Quezon City needs three months of paperwork to legally sell five-peso candies.

A nine-story building in Angeles City got a 2025 work stoppage order, ignored it, added an unpermitted floor, and was still standing tall on May 23.

On May 24 at 3 AM, that building collapsed.

At least five people are dead. Fifteen are still missing. Most of them were construction workers.

Filipinos who have ever tried to open a small business in this country already know the regulatory machinery. SEC or DTI. Barangay clearance. Mayor's permit. Sanitary permit. Fire safety inspection. BIR registration. Books of account. OR and SI authority to print. For anyone building or renovating a space, the gauntlet gets longer: architectural plans signed by a licensed architect, structural plans signed by a registered civil or structural engineer, electrical, sanitary, mechanical, and plumbing plans each signed by their own licensed professional, all before the Office of the Building Official even begins its review.

It is months of running between offices. It is fees on top of fees. Every signature is a small kingdom. The compliance burden is real, and small business owners feel it in their bones.

So the question after Angeles City is not "where are the rules?"

The rules exist. They are everywhere.

The question is: how does a nine-story commercial building get a Department of Labor and Employment work stoppage order in September 2025, keep working through it, add a floor and rooftop modifications without permits, and not get shut down before it falls on the people building it?

Because the same state that grinds down a small business over a barangay clearance was nowhere to be found when a high-rise project blew through a formal stop order.

That is not a paperwork gap. That is a selective enforcement gap.

Here is what is now confirmed. The collapsed building on Teodoro Street in Barangay Balibago, Angeles City, was a nine-story mixed-use structure where construction began in 2020 and the latest permit on record was issued in 2023. Initial findings indicate that structural changes, including an added floor and rooftop modifications, were carried out without the necessary permits or approved revisions from the Office of the Building Official. The Department of Labor and Employment Central Luzon had already issued a work stoppage order against this exact building in September 2025 over safety violations. The local government of Angeles City is now summoning the owner and contractor over the unpermitted expansion. A Senate probe has been sought.

Five dead. Fifteen missing. A work stoppage order on file. Months of construction after that order. A building taller than what was approved.

Now look at the comparison. New Zealand is not a utopia. The country has its own construction-failure history, including a multi-decade leaky homes crisis that cost owners billions to remediate. No country is perfect. But here is the floor. Under the New Zealand Building Act, a council has 20 working days to process a complete consent application. Before a building can be lawfully occupied, the council must issue a Code Compliance Certificate certifying the work was built to the approved consent. If the work deviated, the certificate does not come, and the building is not legal to use.

Different country. Different design choice.

The Philippine Constitution, Article II, Section 4, is plain:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime. Not optional. Not "when the inspector is available." Not "when the contractor decides to cooperate."

So here is the part that nobody in power wants to discuss out loud.

The state is not under-equipped. The Office of the Building Official exists. The DPWH inspectorate exists. The DOLE has the authority to issue work stoppage orders, and one was already on file for this building. The fire bureau exists. The Office of Civil Defense exists. The barangay knows what is being built on every corner.

The state is under-enforced, on purpose, where enforcement would inconvenience whoever is well-connected, and over-enforced, gleefully, where enforcement would extract a fee from someone with no political cover.

A sari-sari store cannot open without a fire safety inspection certificate. A nine-story project under a labor department work stoppage order kept pouring concrete.

That is not a coincidence. That is a system that has decided who it is for.

Filipinos who have built businesses in countries where the same kind of regulatory architecture exists know the difference is not in the rule book. It is in whether the rules are applied to everyone or only to the politically inconvenient. In NZ, a contractor who builds beyond consent does not get a Code Compliance Certificate. The unit cannot be sold or rented legally. The market itself enforces what the regulator wrote down.

In the Philippines, the unpermitted tenth floor goes up. The unpermitted hotel opens. The unpermitted condo is sold to unit owners who only find out something is wrong when the building lists, or cracks, or, in the worst case, comes down.

The Filipino entrepreneur is not the problem. The Filipino construction worker who was inside that building at 3 AM is not the problem. The Filipino consumer who would have stayed at that hotel is not the problem.

The problem is a state that has chosen which laws to actually enforce, and which laws to leave on paper for the next victim to find.

Five dead. Fifteen missing. A work stoppage order that meant nothing.

Ganun kalala.

If a small business owner has ever waited three months for a permit to sell candies, ask one question. Where was that same energy when the nine-story building on Teodoro Street kept going up?
