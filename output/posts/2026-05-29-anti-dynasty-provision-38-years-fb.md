Article II, Section 26 of the 1987 Constitution bans political dynasties.

Congress has spent 38 years refusing to define what one is.

The clause itself is short. The state, it says, shall guarantee equal access to opportunities for public service, and prohibit political dynasties as may be defined by law.

Read that last clause one more time. "As may be defined by law." Four words that have done more to protect Philippine dynasties than any campaign machine ever has.

The Constitution was ratified in 1987. Since then, every single Congress has had at least one anti-dynasty bill filed. Sen. Miriam Defensor Santiago filed one. Sen. Panfilo Lacson filed another. Dozens more have followed across the decades. They keep dying in the same place: at the committee stage, in committees often chaired by members of the very families the bill would restrict.

The only anti-dynasty law that has ever made it through in 38 years is the Sangguniang Kabataan Reform Act of 2016. It applies to youth council elections. Nothing else.

This week alone is what the gap produces.

On Friday, May 29, the Sandiganbayan's 2nd Division issued an arrest warrant and a hold departure order against Sen. Jinggoy Estrada, son of a former President, in connection with a P573-million flood-control kickback case. The same afternoon, Estrada posted P90,000 bail for the graft component. It is his third plunder case in 25 years. The plunder count, which is non-bailable, remains pending.

On the other end of the news cycle, Vice President Sara Duterte, daughter of a former President, has until Monday, June 1, to file her answer to a second impeachment summons. Her trial begins July 6.

Read those two paragraphs side by side without naming any individual.

Two of the country's most prominent legal stories this week involve children of former Presidents. Both come from political families with multiple members holding office simultaneously. The pattern is not a coincidence. It is what an unimplemented constitutional clause has been quietly authorizing for almost four decades.

The receipts on how widely the pattern has spread are not contested.

The Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism has tracked it for years. As of 2025, political dynasties controlled 87 percent of provinces, 83 percent of legislative districts, and 75 percent of cities. More than 80 percent of district representatives in the House are members of dynasties. At least 18 so-called "obese" dynasties, families with five to nineteen seats each, won in the May 2025 elections. Roughly half of the 64 party-list seats in the 20th Congress are filled by members of political clans. Seventy-one of 82 governors come from political families.

That is not a country with a small dynasty problem and a strong constitution. That is a country whose constitution wrote the cure, and whose legislature has spent thirty-eight years refusing to fill the prescription.

Here is the part nobody wants to put in plain language.

The refusal IS the policy. Congress not defining "political dynasty" is not negligence. It is enforcement in reverse. Every Congress that has filed a bill and let it die has effectively voted to keep the door open. The committee chairs who shelve the bill are not bystanders. They are usually the people the bill would name.

The Constitution does not get an "as may be defined by law" loophole on any other clause people care about. Sovereignty does not wait on an enabling statute. Freedom of speech does not wait on an enabling statute. The prime duty of government to serve the people does not wait on an enabling statute.

Only the clause that would inconvenience the people writing the laws has been left undefined for 38 years.

So when a single week of news brings warrants, bails, summonses, and impeachment timelines starring last names the country has elected and re-elected across generations, the real question is not which family did what.

The real question is how a republic ends up in a place where multi-generation political families investigate other multi-generation political families on the front page, while the one constitutional sentence written to prevent the consolidation sits on the same shelf where it has been sitting since 1987.

The framers wrote the clause for exactly this moment.

Congress has spent four decades not finishing the sentence.

Kailan pa ba?

Name one province in the country where the same two or three surnames have NOT held the governor's seat for the last thirty years.
