The Philippines has a Freedom of Information order.

What it does not have, after almost thirty years of filed bills, is a Freedom of Information law.

That gap is not an accident. It is a decision.

In 2016, an executive order operationalized the people's right to information. It was a real step. Citizens could request records and, in principle, expect an answer. But read the fine print: the order covers only the executive branch. The legislature is not bound by it. The judiciary is not bound by it. Local government units are not bound by it.

And an executive order has another quiet weakness. What one president signs, the next president can narrow, ignore, or revoke with a signature. It is a favor granted from above, not a guarantee owed from below.

A real Freedom of Information law would be different. Passed by Congress, it would bind all branches of government, not just one. It would survive a change of administration. It would turn access to public information from a courtesy into a right with teeth.

So why, after roughly three decades of FOI bills, does that law still not exist?

Here is what nobody says out loud. The branches most capable of passing it are also the ones a real law would expose. A genuine FOI statute would open the records of the very offices that have to vote it into being. Asking them to pass it is asking them to install the floodlights in their own back rooms.

The right to information lives in the Constitution. But a right with no enabling law is a promise with no plumbing. The water is supposedly yours. The pipe was just never built.

Article II, Section 1 of the 1987 Constitution is the whole argument in one line:

"Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

You cannot be the sovereign over what you are not allowed to see. A people kept in the dark about how their own money is spent are not really in charge. They are just told they are.

The country was handed the favor and denied the guarantee, by the very people the guarantee would hold to account.

Bakit ayaw nilang makita natin?

Have you ever tried to get a public document from a government office? How did that actually go?
