It took 300 days.

It took the EDSA Shrine filling with protesters. It took an independent commission led by a former Supreme Court Justice. It took the AMLC freezing over 1,600 bank accounts. It took COA auditors with drones, satellite imagery, and geo-tagged photos.

And yesterday, the wheel of accountability finally moved one notch.

The Office of the Ombudsman filed plunder and two counts of graft before the Sandiganbayan against a sitting senator and a former Public Works Secretary, together with three DPWH National Capital Region engineers, over an alleged P573 million in kickbacks tied to flood control projects. No bail recommended. Hold departure orders requested.

That is the news. Read it once, then read what is around it.

The flood control scandal broke roughly 300 days ago. In that window: communities went under water, thousands of Filipinos marched at EDSA Shrine, a sitting DPWH Secretary resigned, his successor scrapped P252 billion in locally funded flood projects from the 2026 budget, and the executive branch had to invent an entirely new Independent Commission for Infrastructure to handle the workload because the existing system clearly was not going to.

For the wheel to move one notch.

This is what the system actually requires before anyone in power is even charged. Not convicted. Charged. Filings. A hearing. A trial that will run for months, possibly years. After 300 days of public outrage.

Here is the part that should not get lost.

The COA reports that triggered the fraud audits were not new. Year after year, the Commission on Audit has filed observations on flood control projects, on confidential funds, on procurement irregularities, on ghost deliveries. Year after year, those audits landed on coffee tables in Manila and gathered dust. Filipinos who have followed the receipts already know this rhythm: the report comes out, the headlines run for a week, nothing happens, and by the next audit cycle the same names show up in the same places doing the same things.

What changed this time was not the audit. The audit was always there. What changed was that the public refused to stop talking about it.

Article XI, Section 1 of the 1987 Constitution puts it plainly:

> "Public office is a public trust. Public officers and employees must, at all times, be accountable to the people, serve them with utmost responsibility, integrity, loyalty, and efficiency, act with patriotism and justice, and lead modest lives."

"At all times" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Not "when the AMLC catches up." Not "when satellite photos prove the seawall was already there before the contract was signed." At all times. The Sandiganbayan exists because this clause exists. Plunder is a capital-grade offense in Philippine law because the founders of the 1987 Constitution understood, viscerally, what happens to a republic when public office is treated as a private revenue stream.

So when a case like yesterday's gets filed, it is not generosity from the system. It is the floor. It is the bare minimum the Constitution already demanded 38 years ago.

This is also not a vindication. It is a filing, not a verdict. The Sandiganbayan still has to try the case. Past plunder cases against high-profile figures have a long history of stretching across decades, of being dropped on technicalities, of being reduced on plea, of being quietly absorbed back into the ordinary furniture of Manila politics. Filipinos who have watched a previous plunder case run its full course, through arrest, through arraignment, through bail hearings, through eventual acquittal or plea, already know the script.

What is different this time is the audience.

The audience kept the receipts. The audience kept showing up. The audience refused to let "300 days on, the scandal fades" become the headline that ended the story.

And the audience has to keep doing it.

Because the next 300 days will look like motions to dismiss, debates over jurisdiction, prejudicial questions filed at the Court of Appeals, allies in the Senate suddenly remembering parliamentary courtesy, and every other procedural lever a long career inside the building can pull. None of that is illegal. All of that is part of how the system runs out the clock.

Filipinos who have watched this play before know the move. They also know what stops it.

The only thing that has ever moved a Philippine accountability case forward is sustained public attention. Not anger that flares and dies. Sustained.

So save the filing. Bookmark the docket. Note the date. Watch what happens in three months, in six months, in a year. The wheel moved one notch yesterday. There are a hundred more notches to go before this scandal lands where the Constitution says it should land.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
