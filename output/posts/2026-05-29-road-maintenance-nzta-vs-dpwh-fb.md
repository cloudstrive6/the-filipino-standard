In New Zealand, a road is built to last a known number of years, and someone is accountable if it doesn't.

In the Philippines, the same stretch of road seems to get "repaired" every rainy season.

One of those is maintenance. The other is a business model.

Every Filipino knows the ritual. A road gets freshly paved. Within months it is dug up again. Then patched. Then it cracks. Then the rains come and it fails. Then, right on cue, here comes another crew, another barrier, another budget line to fix the thing that was just fixed. Ginagawa pa lang, sira na naman.

It is so familiar that it feels normal. It should not.

New Zealand's transport agency manages its state highways through structured maintenance programmes built around how long a road is actually supposed to last. Contracts are written to a standard. The work is audited. The basic expectation is simple: a road should reach its design life, and if it fails early, that is somebody's problem to answer for.

Compare that to a system where roads seem to be repaved on a schedule that tracks the budget calendar more closely than the actual wear on the asphalt.

Here is what nobody says out loud. A road that lasts is boring. It produces no new contracts, no ribbon to cut, no recurring line in next year's budget. A road that fails on schedule, on the other hand, is, for the wrong people, a renewable resource.

The pothole is not always a failure of the system. Sometimes, for someone, it is the system working perfectly.

The Constitution is not vague about whose job this is. Article II, Section 4:

"The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Durable roads are not a luxury upgrade. They are part of that prime duty. A government that lets infrastructure be designed to fail is not just wasting money. It is quietly deciding that the recurring contract matters more than the people who drive, commute, and ship goods over that road every day.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has rough rural roads and real weather damage too. But the floor is different: a road is expected to last, and someone owns it when it doesn't.

A road should outlast the politician who cut the ribbon on it.

Bakit paulit-ulit ang ginagawang kalsada, pero paulit-ulit pa ring sira?

What's a road near you that has been "under repair" more times than you can count?
