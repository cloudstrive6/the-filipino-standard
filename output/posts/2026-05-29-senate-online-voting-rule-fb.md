The Senate is trying to rewrite its own rules so that senators who cannot, or will not, show up in person can still vote.

That is not a software upgrade. That is a survival mechanism.

On Tuesday, May 26, the 11-member Senate minority bloc walked out of the plenary. They were protesting a motion, filed two weeks earlier by Sen. Rodante Marcoleta, that would amend the rules to allow senators to participate in sessions remotely under "justifiable" circumstances. Only twelve senators remained in the chamber after the walkout. The Senate has 24 members. A quorum is 13. The session was adjourned and will resume on Monday, June 1.

Stop and look at that sentence again. One walkout, one quorum collapse, and the chamber that legislates for 118 million Filipinos went dark for a week.

Here is what current Senate rules already say. Remote participation is allowed only as an exception, under force majeure or a national emergency, and the rules explicitly bar counting the vote of an absent senator. The system was designed around physical presence for a reason. Voting in person is the only thing that guarantees a senator was actually in the room when the country's laws were being made.

The proposed amendment would loosen that. "Justifiable" is doing a lot of work in the new wording. What counts as justifiable? Who decides? The Senate majority that wrote the motion.

Now line up who currently benefits from that flexibility.

Sen. Ronald "Bato" dela Rosa is under an International Criminal Court arrest warrant on charges of crimes against humanity related to the drug war. On May 28, the Office of the Ombudsman filed plunder and graft cases at the Sandiganbayan against Sen. Jinggoy Estrada over a P573-million flood-control kickback scheme. Plunder is non-bailable. The Department of Justice has recommended plunder filing against Sen. Joel Villanueva on the same flood-control mess. An impeachment trial of Vice President Sara Duterte is pending before the Senate.

That is the backdrop the rule change is being timed to. Not a typhoon. Not a pandemic. Not a senator in the ICU. A roster of pending arrests, fugitive warrants, and an impeachment trial that needs votes the majority cannot afford to lose.

This is the deeper truth nobody is saying out loud. The remote-voting rule is not about modernizing the Senate. It is about engineering a workaround so that a senator who is in detention, or on the run, or facing criminal charges, can still cast a vote that decides the country's future.

The 1987 Constitution is not vague on this. Article VI, Section 16(2):

"A majority of each House shall constitute a quorum to do business, but a smaller number may adjourn from day to day and may compel the attendance of absent Members in such manner, and under such penalties, as such House may provide."

Read it carefully. The Constitution does not say "or beam them in by Zoom." It says: compel attendance. The framers expected senators to show up. When they did not, the constitutional answer was to drag them in, not to redesign the building.

Other countries have looked at this question. New Zealand's Parliament adopted hybrid sittings on March 1, 2022. It was a Sessional Order, meaning a temporary rule. It was triggered specifically by COVID-19 public-health risk. It expired with the parliamentary term. It was not a permanent quorum workaround for MPs facing arrest. No country is perfect. New Zealand has its own institutional fights, its own backbench scandals, its own MPs the public has had enough of. But the floor for parliamentary integrity is on a different planet from what is being proposed in Manila this week.

The PH version flips the logic. The trigger is not a pandemic. The trigger is a calendar full of court appearances.

This is why the minority walkout matters. Eleven senators denying quorum is not obstruction. It is the only constitutional tool the minority has when the majority is trying to push through a rule that benefits the majority's own legal exposure. The framers anticipated this exact move. That is what "smaller number may adjourn from day to day" is for.

What sits underneath all of it is the oldest pattern in Philippine politics. The system was designed to be answerable to the people. Whenever the people start asking real questions, the system quietly redesigns itself to stay answerable only to itself.

A Senate that cannot get 13 of 24 members to physically sit in one room is already telling Filipinos something. A Senate that responds by changing the rules so the missing members can dial in is telling them something louder.

Public office is a public trust. Showing up is the floor. Voting on the country's laws from inside a detention facility or from outside the reach of a warrant is not a tech feature. It is a constitutional crisis dressed in IT clothing.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa ganito.
