Imagine choosing a surgeon because they were popular on TV.

Not because they trained for years. Just because everyone already knew their name.

That is, more or less, how the country fills some of its most powerful public jobs.

A Senate seat is not a small thing. Senators write national law. They confirm key officials. They shape the budget that funds every hospital, school, and road in the country. It is one of the highest-leverage jobs a Filipino can hold.

And yet the single strongest predictor of who wins one is often not legislative skill, policy record, or experience. It is name recognition. Whoever the public already knows starts the race halfway to the finish line, before a single idea has been debated.

To be fair, fame and competence are not opposites. Some well-known names have turned out to be serious, hard-working legislators. That is real, and worth saying.

But the problem was never any one person. The problem is the pipeline.

When fame is the cheapest, fastest route to a Senate seat, the system quietly starts selecting for visibility instead of capability. Weak parties with no real platforms make it worse, because there is no policy brand for a voter to choose, only a face to recognize. Add a short campaign memory and the ballot slowly turns into a popularity contest where the prize happens to be the power to write the laws everyone else has to live under.

Here is the reframe that makes it click. A vote for a senator is a hiring decision the whole country makes together.

Now think about how a serious employer hires for a complex, high-stakes role. They read the resume. They check references. They ask about the track record. They interview. Nobody hands over that kind of responsibility on fame alone.

For the people who write national law, the country routinely skips the interview entirely.

That is not a Filipino flaw. Voters respond rationally to the only information that is made easy and abundant: a familiar name. Fix the information, strengthen the parties, and the incentive changes. The pipeline is built. It can be rebuilt.

Fame is not a qualification. It is just a head start.

Bakit kasikatan ang hinahanap natin, at hindi kakayahan?

When you vote for a senator, what is the first thing you actually know about them?
