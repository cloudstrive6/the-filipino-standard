In a lot of countries, a politician switching parties is a scandal that trails them for years.

In the Philippines, it is practically a season.

The election ends, and the winners' party quietly swells with new arrivals.

It is one of the most predictable events in Filipino politics. The votes are counted, the new power center becomes clear, and a steady migration begins. Officials who campaigned under one banner drift toward whichever party now holds the keys. The ruling coalition balloons. The opposition thins out. And almost no one pays a price for it, because by the next election, almost no one remembers.

Here is the question that unlocks the whole thing. How is this even possible?

The answer is uncomfortable. It is possible because the parties stand for almost nothing in the first place.

In a system where parties have real, distinct platforms, jumping ship means betraying the actual ideas you were elected to carry. Voters see it for what it is, and they punish it. But when parties are mostly vehicles for personalities and access to power, not vessels for policy, switching sides costs nothing. You are not abandoning a principle. There was never a principle there to abandon.

And that is the real damage, far more than the spectacle of the switch itself.

If your representative can change sides the moment the wind changes, then your vote did not buy a direction. It bought a name. Platform-free parties turn every election into a personality contest and every term into a quiet negotiation for proximity to whoever is winning. Accountability needs a fixed position to measure a politician against. Turncoatism erases the position before you ever get to hold them to it.

Some countries treat this seriously enough to write anti-defection rules into their systems, precisely so that a vote for a party means something durable. No country is perfect, and those rules have their own trade-offs. But they exist because other places decided that a party should be more than a flag of convenience.

This connects to something the page keeps coming back to. We are taught to vote for who, almost never for what. A name is easy to recognize. A platform is something you can actually be held to. One of those is convenient for politicians. The other is useful to citizens.

Kapag walang prinsipyo, walang pinagtataksilan.

Do you actually know what your representative's party stands for, or just who they are?
