The Senate did not convene yesterday.

Not because of a typhoon. Not because of a quorum failure caused by traffic. The chamber failed to open because one of its members had been arrested earlier that day, and the majority bloc decided that the appropriate response was for the entire institution to fall silent.

Here is what the Senate did not do yesterday.

It did not move the Magna Carta of Barangay Health Workers. It did not move the Anti-Hospital Detention Bill. It did not confirm the military and police officials waiting in front of the Commission on Appointments. It did not act on the two pending citizenship-by-naturalization bills sitting on its calendar. The Solid Bloc 11, the chamber's own minority, called this what it is: "a boycott of duty."

That is the news in plain language.

A sitting senator was arrested under a Sandiganbayan warrant in connection with a P573-million flood-control plunder case. The warrant was marked "No Bail." He surrendered to the CIDG and was booked. The same evening, the leadership of the chamber he sits in chose institutional silence as the response.

The framing offered for the silence was "Senate independence." The chamber, the public was told, should be allowed to fall quiet so the country is forced to ask why a co-equal branch would rather go silent than be made to serve.

Filipinos who have read the 1987 Constitution already know the country has an answer to that question.

It is in Article VI, Section 11:

> "A Senator or Member of the House of Representatives shall, in all offenses punishable by not more than six years imprisonment, be privileged from arrest while the Congress is in session. No Member shall be questioned nor be held liable in any other place for any speech or debate in the Congress or in any committee thereof."

Read that first sentence twice.

"In all offenses punishable by not more than six years imprisonment."

The framers of the 1987 Constitution sat in a room and decided exactly how much immunity from arrest a Filipino senator should have. They settled on this: small-offense protection only, and only while Congress is sitting. Anything heavier than six years of possible jail time was deliberately left out of the shield.

Plunder, under Republic Act 7080, is punishable by reclusion perpetua. Twenty years and one day to forty years. The threshold for the crime is P50 million in ill-gotten wealth. The case in question alleges more than ten times that amount.

In other words, the framers wrote Article VI, Section 11 to cover exactly this scenario. A senator charged with a serious crime is not protected from arrest. That is not a loophole. It is the rule the Constitution carefully drew.

So when the chamber's leadership shuts down the building because a senator has been arrested for a non-bailable offense, the chamber is not defending the immunity the Constitution gave it.

The chamber is manufacturing the immunity the Constitution refused to give it.

Those are not the same thing.

A Senate that protects its right to debate freely is exercising the privilege the Constitution wrote.

A Senate that locks the doors because a colleague has been arrested for plunder is exercising a privilege the Constitution explicitly withheld.

Filipinos who have watched the country's accountability cases run their full length already know what comes next. The procedural levers will be pulled. Motions to quash. Petitions for review. Sudden, fervent invocations of senatorial courtesy. Calendars that mysteriously cannot accommodate plenary work for weeks at a time. Bills that wait. Confirmations that wait. Barangay health workers' Magna Carta that waits.

None of that is illegal. All of it is exactly how a chamber runs out the clock on its own member's case while still being able to call the delay a constitutional principle.

Here is the deeper point.

The 1987 Constitution wrote Article VI, Section 11 in narrow, precise language because the framers had just lived through a long stretch of Philippine history in which "the dignity of the office" was used to put officials beyond the reach of the people who paid for them. The narrow language was the lesson. The narrow language was the floor. The narrow language was meant to make it impossible, in 2026, for a chamber to claim that walking out is the same as principled silence.

The chamber walked out anyway.

That is not a small thing. That is the country's upper house, in the middle of a flood control scandal that has already cost the public hundreds of billions of pesos in cancelled projects and a national budget rewrite, choosing to halt its own work to make a point about the arrest of one of its members under a properly issued warrant.

The Constitution already made the point the chamber is now refusing to accept.

A senator can be arrested for plunder. Congress was never given the power to override that. The chamber's only legitimate response to a lawful arrest of a colleague is to let the process run, and to keep doing the work the public sends it to do.

Anything else is the same old script in a new costume.

The bills the chamber did not move yesterday have names. The barangay health workers waiting on the Magna Carta have names. The patients waiting on the Anti-Hospital Detention Bill have names. The military and police officers in front of the Commission on Appointments have names.

The country did not vote for a Senate that goes silent when one of its members is arrested. The country voted for a Senate that does its job, every session day, regardless of which colleague's name happens to be in that morning's headlines.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.

The Senate's job tomorrow is not to fall silent. The Senate's job tomorrow is to open the doors, move the calendar, and let Article VI, Section 11 speak for itself.
