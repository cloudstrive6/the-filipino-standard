Foreign direct investment into the Philippines fell about 35 percent in the first two months of 2026.

Foreign capital can leave when the system stops making sense.

Filipino entrepreneurs cannot.

That is the part of the story the headline number leaves out.

The BSP data is the loud part. Net FDI for January and February of 2026 came in at $1.03 billion, down from $1.58 billion in the same window of 2025. Net equity placements down 12.5 percent. Intercompany borrowings down almost 39 percent. The top equity sources were still Japan, the United States, and Singapore, but the cheques were smaller. On June 2, Malacanang publicly said what its own economists had been hinting at for weeks: the political turmoil in the Senate, the arrests, the walkouts, the chamber that goes silent for two days at a time, is starting to weigh on investor confidence and on the country's broader economy.

That part is true. The Senate has spent the better part of three weeks acting like a closed shop in a hurricane. Foreign capital noticed.

Here is the part nobody is saying out loud.

The dysfunction foreign capital is reacting to in 2026 is the same dysfunction Filipino entrepreneurs have been navigating since long before this Senate boycott. The chamber that walked out for two days is a fresh episode. The system underneath is older than the headline.

Talk to any Filipino who has tried to register a small business in the Philippines. The path is its own gauntlet. SEC or DTI for the business itself. BIR for the TIN and the authority to print receipts. Barangay clearance. Mayor's permit. Sanitary inspection. Fire safety check. Sometimes a locational clearance. Occasionally an electrical inspection on top. Thirteen or more separate steps depending on the LGU. Each step a fee. Each fee a small kingdom.

Talk to any Filipino SME owner who has tried to get a business loan. The bank prefers to lend against three months of an employee's payslips than against three years of healthy receipts. A 10-year track record of a profitable small business often counts for less than an upper-middle salary at a multinational. The credit scoring infrastructure technically exists. Most banks still don't really use it. The lending is relationship-based, which is a polite way of saying it locks out anyone without connections.

Talk to any Filipino business owner about BIR compliance. Books of account. OR and SI registration. Withholding tax slips. Quarterly filings. Annual ITR. For many SMEs, full compliance can eat 15 to 25 percent of margin. The system has accidentally taught entrepreneurs that informality is the rational choice. As of January 2025, roughly 20 million Filipinos, more than four out of every ten employed workers, are now in the informal economy. That is a 3-million-person jump from the year before.

That is not laziness. That is a market reading the same signal foreign investors are reading, and answering the only way it can.

Now look at where the foreign cheques went instead.

Vietnam disbursed about 25 billion dollars of FDI in 2024 and about 27.6 billion in 2025, its highest in five years. More than 80 percent of that went into manufacturing and processing. The Philippines, in the same 2024, attracted 8.93 billion dollars, barely a tenth of one percent higher than the year before. Same region. Same general developmental story. Vietnam was Filipino factories' main rival for the same global supply-chain mandates. Vietnam kept getting them.

Foreign capital is mobile. It reads the dysfunction signal, prices it in, and walks. A Tokyo or Singaporean investor who is uncomfortable with the Philippine Senate's behaviour this week opens a different Excel model and routes the next round to Hanoi or Jakarta. The exit is clean. It does not show up as a sob story. It shows up as a 35 percent drop on a BSP release.

The Filipino entrepreneur is not mobile in the same way.

The Filipino entrepreneur cannot reincorporate in Hanoi in 90 days. The Filipino entrepreneur cannot move the family, the suppliers, the warehouse, and the customer base to Jakarta. The Filipino entrepreneur reads the same dysfunction signal the Japanese investor reads, does the same math, and answers with a slower, quieter exit. Stay informal. Cap headcount at five. Never scale into the registered economy where the friction lives. Settle for the franchise model because the franchisor handles the regulatory layer. Send the most talented daughter to Singapore so at least one earner in the family is out of range of the system.

That is not a press release. That is not a 35 percent line item on a chart. It is a steady, quiet leaching of Filipino entrepreneurial energy that has been going on for decades, in plain sight, and has rarely been told as a story.

Article II, Section 4 of the 1987 Constitution:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Prime duty. Not optional. Not when convenient. The PRIME duty.

A Filipino who is trying to build something legitimate, who is willing to register, file, pay tax, and hire workers honestly, is exactly the kind of citizen the prime duty was written for. The state that taxes their formality at 15 to 25 percent of margin and then asks them to absorb a 13-deep permit gauntlet on top is not serving and protecting them. It is monetizing them.

A Senate that grinds to a halt for two days at a time over the arrest of one of its members is the same Senate that has not built a single-window business registration regime in 38 years. The dysfunction the foreign investor is fleeing this quarter is the dysfunction the Filipino SME has been paying for, every quarter, since 1987.

The headline number is foreign. The structural problem is not.

When the foreign cheque leaves, it makes a sound. The system listens, briefly. There are press conferences. There are reform-pipeline promises.

When the Filipino entrepreneur goes informal, or quietly shuts down, or sends a son to Auckland and a daughter to Doha, the system does not flinch. Because nothing showed up on a BSP release.

That is the cost no one is invoicing.

The 35 percent figure is real. So is the 20 million. One number is making news this week. The other has been making the country every week for 38 years.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
