# 2026-06-03 - Healthcare: NZ public system vs PhilHealth reality (Facebook)

In New Zealand, getting wheeled into a hospital does not come with a bill at the door.

In the Philippines, getting wheeled into a hospital comes with a deposit window.

That single sentence is the whole gap.

Filipinos who have lived under both systems describe the same shock the first time they get sick abroad. In NZ, the public hospital admits, treats, and discharges without invoicing the patient. Emergency care, planned surgery, inpatient stays, all of it sits inside the public system funded out of general taxation. The prescription co-payment is capped at five New Zealand dollars per item, and once the household annual ceiling is reached, prescriptions are free for the rest of the year.

Back home, PhilHealth is universal on paper. Under the Universal Health Care Law, every Filipino is auto-enrolled. The premium is now five percent of monthly salary, shared between employer and employee for the formally employed, paid in full by self-paying members and OFWs.

What does that premium actually buy when the moment arrives?

A case rate. A fixed peso amount per condition. Not a refund of the bill. The hospital still charges the full price. PhilHealth pays a slice. The patient or the family pays the rest. The Commission on Audit has flagged PhilHealth, year after year, for billions of pesos in questionable claims, ghost dialysis sessions, and benefit packages released without proper documentation.

Out-of-pocket health spending in the Philippines hovers around 40 to 45 percent of total health expenditure, among the highest shares in the entire ASEAN region. Translation: when a family member gets seriously sick in PH, the family pays. Savings drain. A GoFundMe gets started. A relative abroad is asked to send money. That is the system. That is what universal coverage looks like on the ground.

No country is perfect. New Zealand's healthcare has real problems. Elective surgery wait times are long and getting longer in some regions. Rural and remote communities still struggle for specialist access. Mental health services are stretched. The Kiwis are not pretending the system is perfect, and the system is not.

But here is the part Filipinos rarely get to feel.

In NZ, when a child breaks a bone, the parent worries about the bone. Not about the receipt. Not about which lola to call for the cash advance. The financial dimension of getting sick has been engineered out of the moment of care. That is what a state owes its citizens when it works.

Article II, Section 4 of the 1987 Constitution puts it plainly:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Reading a sick child's bill is not a private failing. It is a public failure. The prime duty is the floor, not the ceiling.

The Philippine state collects PhilHealth premiums, OFW remittances, VAT, income tax, excise tax, sin tax, and a long list of fees. The country is not poor in revenue. The country is poor in design choices about where that revenue goes.

A health system that requires the patient to keep paying at the cashier window is not a health system. It is a private market with a government logo printed on the receipt.

Filipinos who pay PhilHealth every payday already know this. The receipt at admission is the proof.
