On Monday, June 8, more than 27 million Filipino students go back to school.

They go back to a system still missing about 165,000 classrooms.

And to a new rule that quietly raises the passing grade so that, on paper, more of them will be marked as failing.

That last part is what almost nobody is talking about.

Starting this school year, the Department of Education has tightened the transmutation table. Under the old system, a raw score of 60 was converted up to a transmuted grade of 75, the passing mark. Under the new system, students need a raw score of 70 to 72.99 to be transmuted to that same 75. By SY 2027-2028, transmutation goes away entirely. Students will receive their exact raw scores.

EDCOM 2, the second Congressional Commission on Education, flagged the problem in plain terms. The old transmutation table contributed to mass promotion. It masked the depth of students' learning gaps in school records. In other words, the system was quietly inflating grades to hide how much was not being learned.

So far, so reasonable. Honest grading is better than dishonest grading. The system that was making the numbers look better than the learning deserved a closer look.

Here is the harder question.

What about everything else?

The shortage of about 165,000 classrooms is DepEd's own number. Twelve years ago the gap was 55,000. It has roughly tripled. At the current pace of construction and funding, DepEd itself estimates the gap takes 30 to 55 years and roughly 397 billion pesos to fully close. That is not a wild critic's figure. That is the agency in charge saying out loud how long it would take to give every Filipino child their own seat in a real classroom.

Many public schools still run double and triple shifts. Children sit through truncated days because there is no room for a single, full schedule. Typhoon season cancels weeks of instruction every year, which is exactly why the three-term calendar was approved in the first place: to give schools room to absorb suspensions.

Now imagine the same Filipino child in this system, on Monday morning.

Same classroom built for 35 students, now holding 60. Same teacher. Same 30-year-old ceiling fan. Same half-day shift. Same six-week disruption when the typhoon hits in October. Same patchy power and internet.

What changed?

The cutoff on the transmutation table moved up. The number the student has to hit, to be marked as passing, just got higher. The structural conditions they have to learn under did not move at all.

Other countries chose a different sequence. They built the system first, then measured against it honestly.

New Zealand spent more than twenty years running NCEA, the National Certificate of Educational Achievement, through the New Zealand Qualifications Authority. Students were not curved against each other and were not transmuted up. They were measured against fixed external standards. Not Achieved. Achieved. Merit. Excellence. Internal assessments inside schools were externally moderated by NZQA so a Year 12 result in Wellington meant the same thing as a Year 12 result in Whangarei.

NZ is not a postcard. Housing affordability is rough. Brain drain to Australia is a real and ongoing conversation. NCEA itself is now being reviewed for replacement by the current NZ government. No country is perfect.

But the FLOOR is different. The point is not that NCEA is sacred. The point is that NZ assessed students against external standards for two decades, audited the result honestly, and is now reforming the assessment on the basis of that audit. The system was real enough to be measured against. The system in PH has been so under-built for so long that, until recently, the grades themselves had to be quietly inflated so the records would not show how bad the gap had become.

That is the part that should sit heavy with every Filipino parent dropping a child off on June 8.

Article XIV, Section 1 of the 1987 Constitution:

> "The State shall protect and promote the right of all citizens to quality education at all levels and shall take appropriate steps to make such education accessible to all."

Read it again. Quality education. At all levels. Accessible to all.

Raising the passing cutoff while 165,000 classrooms are still missing is not quality education. It is honest paperwork on top of a dishonest system.

The student in the overcrowded shift class did not fail. The system failed the student first.

Raising the passing grade is not the same as raising the standard. One is paperwork. The other is rebuilding the system underneath the paperwork.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
