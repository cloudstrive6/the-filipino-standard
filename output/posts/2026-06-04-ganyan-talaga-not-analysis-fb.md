# 2026-06-04 - "Ganyan talaga" is not an analysis (Facebook)

"Ganyan talaga."

It is the two-word sentence that ends every Filipino conversation about reform.

The kuryente bill is insane. Ganyan talaga.
The traffic at EDSA is hopeless. Ganyan talaga.
The same family has held the seat for forty years. Ganyan talaga.

The phrase sounds like wisdom. It is not. It is a coping mechanism that has been doing very specific political work for a very long time.

Here is what the phrase actually does.

It ends the conversation before solutions become possible. It moves the question from "why is this broken" to "stop asking, this is just how it is." It transfers responsibility from the people who designed the system to the people enduring it. And it does all of this in a tone gentle enough that nobody feels lectured. That is what makes it so effective.

The phrase is not the speaker's fault. Filipinos who say it are not surrendering. They are surviving. After enough decades of being short-changed, the brain rewires the disappointment into a saying. The saying becomes furniture. The furniture becomes the whole room.

But "ganyan talaga" is not a fact about the Philippines. It is a coping mechanism the powerful have benefited from for forty years.

Look at where the phrase shows up.

It shows up exactly when an answer would cost someone influence. Nobody says "ganyan talaga" about the sunrise. Nobody says "ganyan talaga" about the tide. The phrase is reserved for the systems where a different choice could be made by someone in power, and that someone in power has decided not to make it.

Filipinos who have lived abroad describe the same delayed realization. The countries they moved to are not magic. They are not richer in some mystical way. They are running on slightly different rules, decided by slightly different people, who decided long ago that "ganyan talaga" was not a permitted answer in the room where decisions actually get made.

NZ, Singapore, Estonia, Japan. None of these are utopias. Each has its own version of broken. None of them, however, accept "that is just how it is" as a national circuit-breaker for civic conversation. When something works badly, somebody is asked why. When somebody is asked why, somebody answers. When somebody answers, something usually moves.

In the Philippines, the system has trained an entire generation to stop at the question.

Article II, Section 1 of the 1987 Constitution opens with a sentence that is the direct opposite of "ganyan talaga":

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

That is not a slogan. That is the structural reply. The people are not the audience of the state. The people are the source of its authority. Every kuryente bill, every BIR queue, every collapsed flyover, every senator's privilege speech, every "delayed" infrastructure project, every disappeared confidential fund, all of it operates downstream of a citizenry that has the legal authority to demand more.

That sentence does not need the citizen to be brave. It needs the citizen to stop accepting "ganyan talaga" as a complete explanation.

The phrase is not the Filipino's flaw. It is what decades of being short-changed has taught the Filipino to say to spare their own nervous system.

And it is doing real damage anyway.

Because every time "ganyan talaga" closes a conversation, somebody in power keeps something they should not have kept. A budget. A franchise. A seat. A standard of service so low that nobody is embarrassed to deliver it.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.

That line is not a slogan. It is the rebuttal.

A conversation that ends in "ganyan talaga" is a conversation the system already won.

The next time it shows up, treat it the way the Constitution treats it. As something the speaker did not write, and the speaker is allowed to revise.
