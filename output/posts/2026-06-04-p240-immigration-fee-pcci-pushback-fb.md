## Caption (ready to publish)

The Philippines spent a year telling its citizens it had no money to fix things.

Now it has found a way to fix one thing: charge every traveler P480 round-trip to walk through immigration.

That is the proposal under review at the Bureau of Immigration. Four US dollars, about P240, every time anyone enters or leaves the country. About P480 for the round trip. On top of the P1,620 travel tax most outbound travelers already pay.

The country's biggest business chamber, the Philippine Chamber of Commerce and Industry, came out this week and said the obvious. PCCI president Ferdinand "Perry" Ferrer pointed out that if the fee pushes through, the Philippines would be the only ASEAN member country to charge a modernization fee on global travelers.

Not Singapore. Not Malaysia. Not Vietnam. Not Indonesia. The Philippines alone.

PCCI's other line was even sharper. The Bureau of Immigration, the chamber said, should leverage its existing revenue streams to fund the modernization of border control instead of putting it on travelers.

Look at the receipts.

The Bureau of Immigration's 2026 budget is approximately P4.64 billion. In the first quarter of 2026 alone, the bureau generated about P40 million in revenue from immigration tax. The bureau already has a budget. The bureau already has revenue. The bureau is choosing to layer a new fee on top.

And the project itself, the so-called Civil Aviation and Immigration Security Services or CAISS program, did not even originate inside the Philippine government. It came in as an unsolicited proposal from a foreign firm, Securiport LLC, submitted in May 2023 through the PPP Center.

So the structure is this. A foreign company pitches a system. The system is for modernizing 11 international airports, a major seaport, and six mobile border crossings. The bill is then handed to every Filipino who flies home for Christmas, every OFW who comes back to bury a parent, every domestic worker returning from the Gulf with a balikbayan box, and every tourist the country is supposedly desperate to attract.

Here is the part nobody in officialdom is saying out loud.

In 2025, overseas Filipinos sent home a record USD 35.63 billion in cash remittances. That was equal to 7.3% of the country's entire GDP. The diaspora is not a footnote in the economy. The diaspora is the floor under the peso, the lifeline under the malls, the reason a lot of provinces still function.

The proposal in front of the Bureau of Immigration would tax the act of coming home. It would tax the act of going abroad to work. It would tax the act of returning to the family on leave. The country's largest export earner would be charged a toll, by its own government, to physically be present in its own country.

This is the pattern Filipinos have started to recognise.

When PhilHealth runs short, premiums go up. When transport is broken, fares rise. When power costs spike, the ERC explains the bill shock is just the law working as written. When the immigration system needs an upgrade, the user fee goes on the traveler. Every time the state finds a problem, the bill is quietly slid across the table to the citizen.

The Constitution opens with a single sentence on this exact question. Article II, Section 1:

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

That line is not decorative. It is the design rule for the entire republic. Authority flows up from the people. The state exists to serve them. Not the other way around.

A government that defaults to billing its citizens for its own modernization has inverted that line.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has expensive housing, healthcare wait times in some regions, brain drain to Australia. The point is not that other countries are utopias. The point is that other countries, when they need to upgrade infrastructure, look first at existing budget, existing revenue, and existing public-private structures that do not put the cost on the end user. The Philippines defaults to putting the cost on the end user, then asks why nobody trusts the system.

After enough backlash, the Bureau of Immigration this week committed to review the proposed charge. That is a pause, not a withdrawal. The proposal is still on the table. It can still come back. The question is whether Filipinos stay loud long enough to make it stay off.

Eight million OFWs and tens of millions of domestic travelers cannot be the country's permanent ATM. PCCI saw it. The traveling public saw it. The Constitution already wrote it down 38 years ago.

If the people are sovereign, the people are not a revenue line.

Kailan pa ba?
