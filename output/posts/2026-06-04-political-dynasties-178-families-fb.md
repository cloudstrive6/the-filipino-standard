# 2026-06-04 - Political dynasties: how 178 families effectively control PH politics (Facebook)

Roughly 178 families effectively control Philippine politics.

That number is not a typo. It is not partisan. It is what comes out of the academic literature when researchers count who is actually sitting in the elected seats.

Across the Senate, the House, the governorships, the mayoralties, and a long list of vice-positions and barangay captaincies, the same surnames keep appearing. A father retires, a son runs. A wife runs while the husband is term-limited. A grandfather steps aside for a grandson. A brother becomes a senator while a sister becomes a governor. Multiple academic studies, the Asian Institute of Management Policy Center, the Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism, and peer-reviewed research over more than a decade, all converge on the same finding. Philippine elected office is concentrated in a small number of families, and the concentration has only deepened since 1987.

The most embarrassing part is not the number.

The most embarrassing part is that the 1987 Constitution itself already says this is illegal.

Article II, Section 26 of the 1987 Constitution reads:

> "The State shall guarantee equal access to opportunities for public service and prohibit political dynasties as may be defined by law."

Read that line twice.

The State SHALL prohibit. Not "may." Not "should consider." Not "is encouraged to evaluate." SHALL. The Constitution explicitly orders the state to ban political dynasties.

And then comes the trapdoor: "as may be defined by law."

Thirty-eight years after the Constitution was ratified, the law defining "political dynasty" still does not exist.

Bills have been filed. Versions exist. Some have been read into committee records. None has ever been enacted. Not under any administration. Not under any composition of Congress. The legislators who would have to pass the enabling law are, by definition, the people the law would constrain. They have been asked to define their own surnames as a public hazard. They have, predictably, declined.

This is not incompetence. This is the system working as designed.

A dynasty is not a single family. It is a structural arrangement that uses the apparatus of democracy to preserve a narrow, hereditary share of public power. Elections still happen. Ballots are still cast. The system looks like a republic. The seats keep going to the same surnames.

Compare to how other countries handle this. New Zealand does not have an anti-dynasty law in the same explicit form, but the party system, the proportional representation rules, and the relative weakness of family-based political brands make multi-generation seat inheritance rare. Germany, the Nordics, Japan, Singapore, all of these countries see family-based concentration in the single digits as a share of national elected office.

In the Philippines, the share is the dominant one.

That is not a Filipino flaw. That is what happens when the Constitution writes the rule and then leaves the definition to the only group of people who would have to disqualify themselves to enforce it.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own oligarchic features (corporate concentration, Auckland-Wellington political weight). Germany has its old-guard CDU-SPD networks. None of those equate to 178 families running the seats.

The Philippines is not running a democracy designed for the people. It is running a democracy designed for the people who already hold the seats. Every election, every cycle, the seats are passed within a small, predictable circle, and the rest of the country is invited to ratify the arrangement.

Article II, Section 26 has been waiting 38 years for Congress to do its job.

The wait is not a delay. The wait is the policy.
