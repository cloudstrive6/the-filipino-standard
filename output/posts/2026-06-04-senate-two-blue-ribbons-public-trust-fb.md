## Caption (ready to publish)

On Thursday, the Philippine Senate ran two parallel Blue Ribbon Committees, issued two contradictory orders to its own staff, and produced one missing thing.

The flood-control hearing the country actually needed.

In the morning, the Cayetano-bloc Blue Ribbon Committee, chaired by Sen. Pia Cayetano, pushed through with the scheduled public hearing on the flood-control scandal. Sens. Loren Legarda, Rodante Marcoleta, Imee Marcos and Robinhood Padilla attended. The hearing proceeded on the strength of Sen. Alan Peter Cayetano's claim that he remains the legitimate Senate president, a position he restated on June 3 by publicly calling the leadership reorganization an "illegal coup d'etat."

At the same time, the Tulfo-led Blue Ribbon Committee, installed by the new majority that on Wednesday declared all Senate leadership positions vacant, formally suspended Thursday's hearing "until further notice." Sen. Erwin Tulfo said the next sitting of his version of the panel would be on June 8.

Then came the administrative half of the split.

Acting Senate Pro Tempore Sherwin Gatchalian ordered Senate staff to work from home on June 4 to, in his own words, "preserve the neutrality of the Senate bureaucracy." Cayetano, in a competing directive, ordered Senate officials and personnel to keep the premises open. Staff who reported on-site were later authorized by the new leadership to leave starting at 10:00 AM "for safety reasons."

So on a single Thursday, the chamber that is supposed to investigate where the country's flood-control money went had:

Two competing chairs.
Two competing committees.
Two competing orders to its own workers.
And one corruption probe that did not move forward, except in the version chaired by the bloc the new majority no longer recognises.

This is what the procedural rules look like when the procedural rules become the prize.

Look at what is sitting in the queue underneath this.

The flood-control scandal is not a small file. It is a years-long pattern of allegedly anomalous public-works contracts, billions of pesos in disputed disbursements, ghost projects in real barangays, drainage that was paid for and never built, embankments invoiced and never poured. Every typhoon season, Filipinos who live in flood-prone communities pay the bill for whatever was not constructed. The receipts for whatever was constructed live in a committee report that today exists in two competing drafts.

The Financial Executives Institute of the Philippines this week called on the Senate to demonstrate credibility "by strictly adhering to its existing rules and established procedures." Malacañang has publicly conceded that the Senate turmoil is now affecting investor confidence and the broader economy. Economist Diwa Guinigundo, writing in the Manila Times the same Thursday morning, warned that the economy could "end up as collateral damage."

That is the visible cost. There is a deeper one.

Article XI, Section 1 of the 1987 Constitution opens with the line every Filipino has heard quoted at school and rarely seen practised:

> "Public office is a public trust. Public officers and employees must, at all times, be accountable to the people, serve them with utmost responsibility, integrity, loyalty, and efficiency, act with patriotism and justice, and lead modest lives."

Public trust is not a slogan. It is a legal standard. A trustee owes a duty of loyalty to the beneficiary. In this case, the beneficiary is the Filipino public. The trustee is the entire Senate, as an institution, regardless of which bloc holds the gavel that morning.

A trustee who is busy fighting another trustee over the keys to the office, in front of a stack of unread audit files, is not serving the beneficiary.

The deeper pattern is older than this week.

Filipinos who have watched Philippine politics for any length of time know how this episode rhymes. A chamber that party-jumps without consequence. A majority that gets re-engineered every time the arithmetic shifts. Senate rules that bend to whichever bloc has the numbers on a Wednesday afternoon. Committee chairmanships that move not by jurisdictional logic but by factional balance. None of this is invented for the 2026 Senate. The 2026 Senate is just the latest, loudest version of it.

No country is perfect. New Zealand's parliament has its own dysfunctions: opposition theatrics, urgency motions used to push through unloved bills, real-estate scandals, a brain drain to Australia that puts long-term pressure on talent. Westminster systems have their own tantrums. The point is not that other parliaments are utopias. The point is that other parliaments tend to fight loudly about policy. The Philippine Senate this Thursday is not fighting loudly about policy. It is fighting loudly about who gets to chair the room where the policy fight is supposed to happen.

The flood-control corruption probe, the one that would put names and numbers on receipts that the public is already paying for, is the part that is supposed to be louder than the leadership fight.

It is not. That is the design failure.

A republic where the trustees fight over the chair while the beneficiary's money sits unaudited is not a procedural mess. It is a constitutional one.

Filipinos who pay the taxes that fund these salaries, sit through the typhoons that the missing drainage was supposed to manage, and watch the hearings end without anyone going to jail, have every right to ask the question Article XI, Section 1 already answered 38 years ago:

If public office is a public trust, who exactly is the trustee on Thursday?

Two competing answers in one building is the same as no answer at all.

Ganun kalala.
