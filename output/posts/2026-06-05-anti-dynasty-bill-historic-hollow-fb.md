After 38 years, the House finally passed an anti-dynasty bill.

The version they passed is the bill the dynasties wrote about themselves.

On Wednesday, June 3, 2026, House Bill 8389 cleared third and final reading by an overwhelming majority. It is the farthest such a bill has reached, in either chamber, since the 1987 Constitution ordered Congress to pass one.

That is the headline. Read it again. Then read what is actually in the bill.

The prohibition only reaches the second degree of consanguinity or affinity. Parents, children, grandparents, grandchildren, siblings, the spouse. That is it.

The original committee version reached the fourth degree. Cousins, nephews, nieces, aunts, uncles, in-laws. Somewhere between the first version and the final vote, those relatives were quietly excused from the rule.

The bill also leaves succession untouched. A covered relative can step into the seat the moment the politician leaves it. Father to son. Mother to daughter. No pause. No reset.

The bill also applies only to the same locality. A governor in one province, a mayor in another, a senator in the national chamber, a congressman in a third district. All the same family. All perfectly legal under HB 8389.

In other words, the dominant model of Philippine politics, the family that holds office across regions, the household that hands the seat down generation after generation, the surname that splits its members across the national, provincial, and city levels, walks through this bill without breaking stride.

This is the part the news cycle is not pausing on.

The 1987 Constitution, Article II, Section 26, says it plainly:

> "The State shall guarantee equal access to opportunities for public service, and prohibit political dynasties as may be defined by law."

Prohibit. Defined by law. The Constitution wrote the command. It left the definition to Congress. For 38 years, Congress did nothing.

Then, this week, Congress did something. And what Congress did is the strongest possible evidence of why it took 38 years.

The body that was supposed to write the law against political dynasties is itself, by any honest count, dominated by political dynasties. The very people who needed to be defined out of public office were handed the pen and asked to draw their own line. They drew it exactly where their own seats are safe.

Second degree only. Same locality only. Succession allowed. Cross-district allowed. Cousins exempted. National-and-local pairing exempted.

It is not that the dynasties failed to stop the bill. They wrote the bill. The bill passed because the version that passed leaves them intact.

This is what political scientists in calmer countries would call "legislating against yourself." It is hard. It is rare. And when it is done sincerely, it produces a law that hurts. This law does not hurt. That is how to read the vote.

No country is perfect. New Zealand has its own concentrations of political power, business interests, and old family names too. The difference is structural. A first-cousin pairing in a New Zealand electorate, two siblings running a council together, a parent immediately succeeded by their child in the same seat, those situations are visible and discussed and constrained, not normalized as the operating system of the entire republic.

Filipinos who have watched politics in NZ, Australia, Canada, Singapore, and beyond describe the same thing. The presence of family in politics is not the problem. The presence of family AS politics is. When governance becomes household logistics across three generations, the ballot stops being a hiring decision and starts being a confirmation hearing for the same surnames.

That is the part this bill leaves perfectly intact.

The historic part is real. Congress moved a piece of paper further than it has been moved in 38 years. That deserves to be noted.

The hollow part is also real. What moved across the floor was a definition of "political dynasty" so narrow that it would not have stopped most of the families currently holding power from getting to where they are.

The Senate now receives the bill. Whatever happens next will be measured against the same test.

A real anti-dynasty law does three things. It covers more than the second degree. It bans simultaneous holding of office across localities by the same family. It prohibits immediate succession. The day a Philippine anti-dynasty law does all three, the country will know the moment has actually arrived.

Until then, the bill that just passed is the answer to a question Filipinos have been asking for 38 years.

The question was: will the dynasties ever write the law against themselves?

The answer is: yes. And when they do, this is what it looks like.
