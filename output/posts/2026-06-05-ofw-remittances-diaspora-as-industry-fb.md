# 2026-06-05 - OFW remittances: the country's largest "industry" is its diaspora (Facebook)

The single largest industry in the Philippines is not BPO, agriculture, manufacturing, or tourism.

It is the people who had to leave.

OFW cash remittances run at roughly USD 35 to 40 billion every year. Add personal remittances and the figure climbs higher. By either measure, the Philippines is consistently among the world's top four remittance-receiving countries. The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas publishes the numbers monthly. The World Bank tracks them in its Migration and Development Briefs. The story does not change.

Remittances are roughly 8 to 10 percent of Philippine GDP.

Roughly two million Filipinos are deployed abroad at any given time, working in nursing, construction, seafaring, domestic work, IT, engineering, hospitality, and a hundred other categories. Their wages, sent home month after month, prop up the peso, fund family consumption, pay for tuition, buy the small lots, build the small houses, and absorb the inflation the domestic economy keeps generating.

The country has effectively turned its own people into its most reliable export.

This is usually told as a story of pride.

Filipinos are hard-working. Filipinos adapt. Filipinos thrive abroad. Every word of that is true. Filipino nurses run wards in the UK, Canada, the Gulf, and New Zealand. Filipino seafarers move global trade. Filipino caregivers hold the aged-care system together in Hong Kong, Singapore, and Taiwan. Filipino engineers build infrastructure in places that pay them ten times what the Philippines pays for the same skills.

But here is the part that does not get said out loud.

The diaspora's success is the country's diagnosis.

A country that has to send 10 percent of its labour force abroad to keep itself solvent is not a country that has succeeded. It is a country whose elites have outsourced the cost of their own failure to their own citizens. The story of OFW remittances is not heroism. It is what happens when domestic wages, public services, and economic mobility cannot keep up with a citizen's ability to imagine a better life.

Filipinos who leave do not stop loving the Philippines. They send home the proof every month.

The proof gets deposited into a domestic account. It pays the bills the local labour market cannot. It funds the school tuition the local school system is supposed to fund. It pays the medical bill PhilHealth was supposed to cover. It fills the gap between what a Filipino household needs to live and what the Philippine economy delivers.

What happens when a country runs on that?

The country develops a structural dependency on its own absence. Policymakers stop fixing the domestic labour market because the remittance line keeps the dollars flowing. Politicians celebrate OFWs as "modern heroes" in every speech, because the heroism narrative is cheaper than wage reform, healthcare reform, education reform, or labour rights reform. The system has every incentive to keep the conveyor belt moving.

No country is perfect. NZ, Australia, Canada, all of them benefit from the export of Filipino labour. They have their own problems. They are not the heroes of this story. They are the recipients of an arrangement Manila has been more than happy to maintain.

The honest verdict is the one the country never says.

The Philippines is not too poor to fix its labour market. It is too comfortable with the alternative. As long as USD 35 to 40 billion a year keeps coming back from abroad, the political class can keep delivering the same governance, the same services, the same broken floor, and the public is asked to be grateful.

OFWs are not modern heroes because they wanted to be heroes. They are modern heroes because the country did not give them the option to just be employed Filipinos at home.

The day the Philippines stops needing to call its overseas citizens "modern heroes" is the day the system has started to fix itself.

Until then, the title is a confession.

Bayaning isinusugal sa eroplano.
