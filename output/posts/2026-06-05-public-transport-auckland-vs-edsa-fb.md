# 2026-06-05 - Public transport: Auckland/Wellington vs MRT/LRT/EDSA (Facebook)

In Auckland, a delayed train sends an apology straight to your phone.

In Metro Manila, a delayed train becomes a national event.

That single contrast is the whole story.

Filipinos who have lived in cities with functional public transport describe the same disorientation when they come back. The thing that takes the longest to readjust to is not the heat, the noise, or the smell of EDSA exhaust. It is the assumption that buses arrive when the schedule says, that trains run at posted intervals, and that any service disruption gets logged, communicated, and refunded.

In Auckland, a single contactless card pays for buses, trains, and ferries. The same card. The same tap. Real-time service data feeds open apps. If the train is late, riders get a notification before they reach the platform. If the line is cancelled, the alternative is suggested. The infrastructure is not perfect. Aucklanders complain about it daily. They are not pretending it works flawlessly.

But the floor is on a different planet from EDSA.

In Metro Manila, the MRT-3 runs at a fraction of its design capacity at peak hours. Breakdowns are routine. Train shortages have been documented for years. Riders queue for an hour before reaching the platform, then crush into a train that is missing half its planned cars. The LRT-1 and LRT-2 have integrated ticketing within themselves through Beep, but the broader bus and jeepney network is fragmented, cash-based, and largely uncoordinated.

The EDSA bus carousel runs in patches. The jeepney modernization program has been moving for years without converging. The published schedules of the bus lines exist in different formats, different apps, different LGUs.

JICA's Mega Manila transport studies estimate that the metro region loses tens of millions of US dollars in productivity every single day to traffic congestion. The exact number moves depending on methodology, but the order of magnitude does not. Manila is bleeding GDP into the asphalt every working morning.

This is not a Filipino driver's failure. It is not a Filipino commuter's failure. The commuter is doing extraordinary things to survive a system that nobody designed for the commuter.

It is the result of a long series of policy choices.

Choose to underinvest in mass transit, year after year. Choose to award concessions on legal terms that protect the concessionaire, not the rider. Choose to skip the integrated ticketing layer that every other functional city installed decades ago. Choose to let the jeepney sector age out without a coherent transition plan. Choose to leave the EDSA carousel half-implemented while the population that depends on it doubles.

Each of those choices was made by people in offices. Not by the commuter standing in the rain at Cubao at 6:42 AM.

No country is perfect. Auckland has real complaints. Ferries cancel. Bus frequencies in some suburbs are too thin. The fare hikes have been steady. Wellington's commuter rail has had its own bad years. Kiwis grumble about their transport daily and they have every right to.

But here is the structural difference.

When something breaks in Auckland or Wellington, somebody gets asked why. When somebody gets asked why, somebody answers. Service alerts go out. Investigations get conducted. The next contract gets written with the failure in mind.

In Metro Manila, the breakdown becomes the news for a day, the politicians blame the previous administration, the operator releases a statement, and the next day the commuter is back on the same broken platform.

Article II, Section 4 of the 1987 Constitution says the prime duty of the government is to serve and protect the people. A government that cannot reliably move its citizens from home to work and back is failing the most foundational version of that duty.

Public transport is not a luxury. It is the bloodstream of a working economy.

The country that runs on jeepneys and broken trains has been running on the people who endure them.

Saan napupunta ang oras ng commuter?
