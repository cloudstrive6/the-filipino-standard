A bill that promises to punish only "false information" is not a bill about truth. It is a bill about who gets to decide.

The Philippine House of Representatives passed HB 9465, the Digital Media Anti-False Information Act, on third and final reading on June 3, 2026. The vote was 286 in favor, 3 against, 7 abstaining. The chamber then adjourned sine die. The bill now sits with the Senate.

The headline numbers are clean. Six to twelve years in prison. Fines of P500,000 to P2 million. Targeting anyone found guilty of "knowingly and willfully" creating, financing, directing, or "materially assisting" in disseminating false information intended to cause "verifiable public harm" or threaten national security.

The bill writes itself a list of protected speech. Criticism of government. Criticism of public officials. Satire. Journalism. Academic discourse. Whistleblowing. That list is the part the sponsors point to when asked if this is a censorship bill.

Read the rest carefully. The elastic parts are not on that list.

"Verifiable public harm" is undefined. "Materially assists" is undefined. Those two phrases are doing the work. In a country with a long, documented record of vague speech laws being used against the people they were supposedly written to protect, that is not a detail. It is the whole story.

Human Rights Watch publicly called on lawmakers to withdraw the bill on June 2. The National Union of Journalists of the Philippines has flagged a long-running concern that disinformation hearings have become platforms for censorship rather than reform. Local think tanks have warned the law is "vulnerable to state abuse." None of those groups are denying that disinformation is a real and damaging problem. They are saying this is the wrong shape of cure.

Look at the floor this is about to land on.

The 2026 World Press Freedom Index ranks the Philippines 114th of 180 countries. The ranking technically "improved" from 116 in 2025. The actual score dropped, from 49.57 to 46.79. The country rose in the table because the rest of the world fell faster. The Philippines is now 6.79 points away from the "very serious" red zone reserved for the world's most repressive media environments.

That is the platform on which a brand-new criminal offense for speech is about to be built.

The 1987 Constitution has something to say about this. Article III, Section 4:

> "No law shall be passed abridging the freedom of speech, of expression, or of the press, or the right of the people peaceably to assemble and petition the government for redress of grievances."

Notice the word "abridging." Not "outlawing." Not "banning." Abridging means shortening, narrowing, chilling. A law that makes an ordinary Filipino pause before sharing, commenting on, or signal-boosting a story they cannot personally fact-check to a courtroom standard is, by definition, an abridgement.

Here is the part that does not survive the official statements.

Vague speech laws do not get used the way their sponsors promise. They get used the way the prosecutor finds convenient. Libel and cyberlibel were sold to the public as protections for ordinary people. The case file from the last decade shows they have most often been used against journalists, bloggers, and citizens who annoyed the powerful. The Maria Ressa cyberlibel conviction is the headline case. The hundreds of smaller cases filed against local reporters and citizen commentators sit underneath it, mostly unreported, mostly unknown outside the families paying the legal fees.

A new offense built on phrases as elastic as "materially assists in verifiable public harm" hands the next administration, and the one after that, a tool. The tool will outlast the sponsor. The tool will outlast the Senate hearing that promised it would only be used against troll farms. Tools do not stay on the shelf.

Two things can be true at the same time. Disinformation in the Philippines is real. Coordinated inauthentic behavior, paid troll networks, foreign-backed influence operations, and AI-generated content used to defame are real. A serious response is needed. And: a vague criminal statute with a 12-year ceiling is not that response. It is a leash long enough to reach anyone the next administration would like to reach.

The bill now sits with the Senate. That chamber spent the previous two weeks visibly paralyzed on other matters. Whether it acts on this one, and how, will tell Filipinos how much of Article III, Section 4 is still load-bearing in 2026.

Sino ang magpapasya kung ano ang totoo?

That is the question the bill is actually asking. The constitutional answer was settled in 1987. The Senate now decides whether that answer still holds.
