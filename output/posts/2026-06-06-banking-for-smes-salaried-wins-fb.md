# 2026-06-06 - Banking for SMEs: why a salaried employee gets a loan and your business doesn't (Facebook)

A Filipino with a stable monthly salary can walk into a bank and walk out with a car loan in days.

A Filipino with a five-year-old profitable small business can walk into the same bank and walk out with nothing.

That gap is not bad luck. It is the system working exactly as designed.

PH banking lends against payslips. It lends against real estate collateral. It lends against the creditworthiness of large corporations. It does not, as a rule, lend against the cash flow of a small business. That is true even when the small business produces more reliable receipts than the salaried employee next to it in the queue.

The Magna Carta for MSMEs has, for years, required banks to allocate at least 8 percent of their loan portfolio to micro and small enterprises and another 2 percent to medium enterprises. In practice, the largest banks pay the regulatory penalty rather than meet the quota. The penalty is cheaper than the lending. That is not a complaint. That is a settled fact in years of BSP reports.

World Bank Enterprise Surveys consistently rank "access to finance" among the top constraints reported by Philippine SMEs. Filipino entrepreneurs who have run businesses in both countries describe the same shock the first time they approach a bank abroad. In New Zealand, small business overdrafts can be approved on cash flow. EFTPOS terminal financing arrives in days. The Inland Revenue's tax record on the business is a usable credit signal because the system trusts its own data.

In the Philippines, the system does not trust its own data, because the system has not been built to use it.

The Credit Information Corporation exists. It was created to enable real credit scoring across PH financial institutions. Coverage is partial. Utilisation is patchier. Banks still lend, by default, on the basis of relationships. The result is what every Filipino SME owner already knows. The cousin of the bank officer gets the line of credit. The hardworking carinderia owner of seven years does not.

This is not a story about banks being evil. Banks are profit-seeking institutions doing the math the regulatory environment lets them do.

It is a story about a regulatory environment that has, for decades, allowed payslip-secured lending and large-corporate financing to be the rational thing for banks to do, and SME cash-flow lending to be a hassle the banks would rather pay a fine to skip.

Compare the consequences.

A country that lends against payslips makes employment the only durable path to capital. The entrepreneur, the freelancer, the side hustler, the family business owner, all of them are pushed back toward formal employment as the only legible identity to a bank. The economy gets dragged toward the categories the banks understand. Innovation, by definition, is what does not fit those categories yet.

A country that lends against business cash flow, by contrast, makes building something legitimate a path to capital. New ventures can grow without their founder needing to be a salaryman first. Risk gets priced. Failures happen. Survivors compound. That is how middle-class economies are actually built.

The Philippines did not get the second model.

The Philippines got the first model, plus a Magna Carta that the banks pay to ignore, plus a CIC that most institutions are still warming up to, plus an unspoken rule that the people who get capital are the people who know the people who hand out the capital.

The Filipino entrepreneur is not the problem.

The Filipino entrepreneur is producing receipts that the banking system has never been required to read.

No country is perfect. NZ banking has its own scandals: mortgage gouging, fee opacity, slow innovation in some retail lines. The system is not a paradise. But the floor for an honest small business is on a different planet. A working SME in NZ does not have to know somebody at the branch to be taken seriously. Their last 12 months of receipts do the talking.

The Philippines could have that floor too. The Magna Carta is already on the books. The CIC already exists. The payment rails are already in place. The bottleneck is enforcement and the political will to make enforcement matter more than the penalty fee.

A bank that pays the fine instead of doing the lending is a bank that has done the math the regulator allowed it to do.

The math is changeable. The regulator answers to a state. The state answers to the people.

Sino ba talaga ang pinapaboran ng sistema?
