## POST TEXT (publish this verbatim)

Classes open on Monday.

The country is short more than 165,000 classrooms.

That is not an opinion. That is DepEd's own number, cited by GMA News and confirmed in the same agency's planning documents. It is the figure behind every double-shift, every triple-shift, every classroom built for 40 children that now holds 60.

For School Year 2025 to 2026, DepEd projected 27.6 million enrollees in basic education. The same agency has openly said that at the current pace of construction, closing the classroom gap would take roughly 55 years to solve. Fifty-five. Years.

Most of the children walking into class on Monday will be grandparents before the country builds them a seat.

And the picture gets worse, not better. EDCOM II, the joint congressional commission on education, has projected that 51,000 public school buildings will age out and be condemnation candidates by 2028. Layer that on top of today's gap and the projected total shortage by 2028 climbs to roughly 219,000 classrooms.

That is not a country building schools faster than the population grows. That is a country falling behind on the most basic civic infrastructure there is.

Meanwhile, this same week, a Bulacan court ordered the arrest of a contractor at the centre of the flood control kickback case, the latest in a months-long parade of malversation filings tied to billions of pesos in ghost and substandard projects.

So the country has the money for projects that do not exist. The country does not have the money for classrooms that 27 million children walk into every June.

Two ledgers. Same state.

Compare that to a functional system. NZ runs single-shift schools. Class sizes typically 25 to 30 students. An Equity Index channels extra operational funding to schools serving lower-income communities, so a child's postcode does not decide the quality of the chalkboard. NZ has its own real problems, regional teacher shortages, equity gaps that activists still fight about, debates over assessment reform. No country is perfect. But the FLOOR for a Kiwi child is one classroom, one teacher, one shift, one full school day. Not half a school day in a room shared with another grade because the building was supposed to be replaced a decade ago.

NZ has 5.3 million people. The Philippines has 118 million. Different scale, of course. But the gap between the two systems is not 22 times wider. It is structurally different. Different priority. Different choice.

The Constitution is not vague on whose job this is. Article II, Section 4 of the 1987 Constitution:

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people."

Read that line, then look at a Filipino public school on the first day of class. Fifty-something children in a room built for 40. A teacher trying to hold attention in a room next to another teacher doing the exact same thing through a thin wall. A roof that has not been replaced since the building was first opened.

That is the prime duty. Not an aspiration. Not "if budget allows." Not "marami pa kasing problema." The prime duty.

A country that misses 165,000 classrooms today and is on track to miss 219,000 by 2028 has not been short on resources. It has been short on the political decision to spend them on its children before its contractors.

Brigada Eskwela cleaned the classrooms last week. That is good. That is community. But cleanup is not construction. A repainted wall is not a new room. A scrubbed floor is not a teacher.

The children are showing up on Monday whether the country is ready or not. They always do. That is the Filipino reflex the system has been quietly counting on for decades.

Filipinos who have raised children abroad will say the same thing. The shock is not the wealth of the other country. It is the assumption that a child showing up for school is the floor, not a victory.

Filipino children deserve that floor too. Not as a bonus. As a baseline.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
