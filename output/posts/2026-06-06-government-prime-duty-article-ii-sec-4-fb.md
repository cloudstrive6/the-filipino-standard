# 2026-06-06 - The government's "prime duty" and what it implies (Facebook)

Most Filipinos have never been walked through what their own Constitution actually says about the government.

That is not their fault. Civics classes were a long time ago. Politicians prefer the document stay quoted only on Independence Day.

Article II, Section 4 of the 1987 Constitution is one sentence that, if every Filipino held it as a daily working standard, would change every conversation about flooding, hospitals, schools, transport, and corruption.

> "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people. The Government may call upon the people to defend the State and, in the fulfillment thereof, all citizens may be required, under conditions provided by law, to render personal military or civil service."

Read that line again.

The PRIME duty. Not "a" duty. Not "an important" duty. The PRIME duty. The first, the foundational, the load-bearing obligation of the Philippine state. To serve and protect the people.

Constitutional commentaries from Justice Bernas and others read this clause as the floor of legitimate government action. A government can do many things. A government must do this. Anything below this is a government failing a constitutional standard, not a soft expectation.

What does that imply on an ordinary Tuesday?

When a child cannot get to school because the EDSA bus carousel collapsed at 6 AM, the prime duty is not being honoured. When a typhoon flattens a coastal town and the rebuild is still tents two years later, the prime duty is not being honoured. When a public hospital cannot admit a patient without a deposit, the prime duty is not being honoured. When a small business owner spends three weeks running between SEC, BIR, barangay, mayor's permit, sanitary, and fire safety just to register, the prime duty has been displaced by the duty to extract fees.

None of these are private failings of the people enduring them.

They are constitutional failures, in the literal sense the word "failure" has when measured against the actual text of the 1987 Constitution.

This matters because Filipinos are often asked, gently or otherwise, to lower their expectations. "Ganyan talaga." "Wala nang magagawa." "Bahala na ang Diyos." Every one of those phrases asks the citizen to absorb the gap between the prime duty and the actual delivery.

The Constitution does not ask the citizen to absorb the gap.

The Constitution asks the state to close it.

Compare. New Zealand does not have a "prime duty" clause written exactly this way, but its parliamentary doctrine and statutes embed the same idea: that the state owes its citizens functioning core services as a baseline of legitimacy. Public hospital admissions do not require a deposit. Public schools are funded per child. Emergency response is funded, drilled, and audited. None of this is because Kiwis are better people. It is because the floor was written down, defended, and treated as non-negotiable.

The Philippines wrote the floor down. In 1987. In one sentence. Then it spent four decades quoting the sentence at parades and ignoring the sentence on Monday mornings.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own services that fall short of its own floor. So does every functioning country. The point is not that NZ has flawlessly executed its own version. The point is that Filipinos already have a "prime duty" sentence in their own founding document and have, for thirty-eight years, watched the political class read it aloud on cue and ignore it operationally.

The prime duty is the floor, not the ceiling.

Every Filipino reading this is the constitutional standard's audience and its enforcer. The state is not the sovereign. The people are. The state is the contractor.

When the contractor fails to deliver, the contract is the answer. Not a slogan. Not a campaign jingle. The text. Article II, Section 4. Verbatim.

Read it once a year. Memorise the second clause too.

Then notice every time it is being broken.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
