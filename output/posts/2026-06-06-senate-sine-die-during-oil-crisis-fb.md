# 2026-06-06 - Senate adjourns sine die for 51 days while oil crisis bites (Facebook)

The Senate just went on a 51-day vacation.

The country is in the middle of an oil crisis. The President asked them, on the record, to stay and pass a supplemental budget. They left anyway.

That is not a procedural footnote. That is a snapshot of who the institution actually serves.

The first regular session of the 20th Congress formally adjourned sine die on June 6, 2026. The second regular session does not open until July 27, when the chamber reconvenes for the State of the Nation Address. Between now and then, roughly seven and a half weeks pass with no plenary sessions.

In those same seven and a half weeks, the country continues to import close to 98 percent of its crude oil from the Middle East. The Iran war is still pushing prices up. The national energy emergency, signed by the President in March, is still in effect.

President Marcos went on the record only days earlier asking senators to "get back to work." He floated a supplemental budget. He floated legislative amendments. The reason was specific: Filipinos hit hardest by the fuel shock, the jeepney and tricycle drivers, the fisherfolk, the farmers, the stranded OFWs in the Middle East, need help that requires Congress to act.

The Senate's response, as a chamber, was to elect new officers, declare a quorum with twelve members present, and adjourn.

Look at what was left undone.

The P2.5 billion Pantawid Pasada fuel subsidy already on the books is not enough for the surge in pump prices. Expansion proposals range from roughly P7.6 billion at the low end to P61 billion at the higher coverage levels, depending on how many drivers, operators, fisherfolk and stranded OFWs are included. The expansion needs Congress. Congress is on break.

A Bayanihan-style framework bill for broader crisis response has been floated by senators publicly. It also needs Congress. Congress is on break.

The excise-tax suspension on fuel that the President signed back in March, P10 per litre on gasoline and P6 per litre on diesel, is set to expire on a calendar that does not pause for the Senate's holiday. The next moves around it also need Congress. Congress is on break.

This is the part that deserves a moment of quiet anger.

A regular Filipino working a six-day week, hit by the same fuel prices, does not get to declare a 51-day adjournment of their own life. The jeepney driver does not get a sine die. The fisherman does not get a sine die. The OFW family wiring money home does not get a sine die. The market vendor whose deliveries are now more expensive does not get a sine die.

Only the institution that exists to make the rules gets to step away from them.

Compare what the rest of the constitutional setup is doing in the same window. The executive branch is still operating. The judiciary is still hearing cases. Local government units are still collecting taxes and paying salaries. The military is still on duty. Only the Senate, the body whose primary product is legislation, has decided that this particular crisis is something the next session can pick up.

The 1987 Constitution opens with a line every Filipino is taught to memorise and then trained to forget. Article II, Section 1:

> "Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them."

Read that line one more time, then put it next to the calendar.

The sovereign is the Filipino people. The Senate's authority is delegated. The senators on a 51-day break are not the bosses of the people who pay for jeepney fare and rice. They are the contractors. The sovereign Filipino public is the client.

When a contractor walks off the job in the middle of a crisis, the question is not whether they have the right to do it. The question is whether they should ever be rehired.

That is not a small question. The 2028 national elections are closer than they look. Every senator who walked off the floor of this chamber today is going to come back and ask Filipinos for another term.

The receipts of this week deserve to be remembered then.

No country is perfect. New Zealand's parliament also recesses, and recesses on a published calendar that voters can actually plan around. The difference Filipinos who have watched both systems describe is not whether legislatures take breaks. It is whether the legislature treats a national crisis as a reason to stay.

The Senate that adjourned today did not treat this as a reason to stay.

Filipinos paying for that decision at the pump and the wet market will be the ones asked, in 2028, whether the contractor deserves a new contract.

Hindi tayo dapat sanay sa mediocre.
