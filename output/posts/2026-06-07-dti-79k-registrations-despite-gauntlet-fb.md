79,165 Filipinos started a new business in May.

That number is not a sign the system is working. That number is a sign the system has not yet broken the people inside it.

The DTI announced on June 6 that business name registrations climbed 19.69% in May 2026. 79,165 names filed in a single month, up from 66,144 the year before. Year-to-date, 622,706 Filipinos have walked into a registration portal so far in 2026, 11.63% more than the same stretch last year. Wholesale and retail dominated. Food and accommodation came next. Manufacturing, real estate, and a long tail of small services made up the rest.

Read past the headline. That is hundreds of thousands of Filipinos, in a single five-month stretch, deciding to build something.

Now read what they actually had to do to register.

A DTI certificate is only step 1. After that comes BIR. Then barangay clearance. Then the mayor's permit. Then sanitary inspection. Then fire safety inspection. Sometimes locational clearance. Sometimes electrical inspection. Different offices. Different forms. Different windows. Different excuses for why the form that was enough yesterday is suddenly not enough today.

This is the gauntlet Filipino business owners have been describing for decades. Thirteen-plus separate steps depending on the LGU. Weeks of running. Fees at every counter, and sometimes a second informal fee on top of the official one.

Compare that to what a Filipino entrepreneur abroad is asked to do.

In New Zealand, the equivalent process is one online portal, about 30 minutes, around NZD $130. The certificate of incorporation lands in the inbox before the morning coffee is cold. One director needs to reside in NZ or Australia. That is the entire structural floor.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own small-business headaches: a tiny domestic market, an aggressive IRD compliance burden once a company crosses the GST threshold, real difficulty raising capital outside Auckland. Building a real business there is not a holiday.

But the FLOOR is on a completely different planet. A Kiwi entrepreneur and a Filipino entrepreneur are not starting on the same line. They are not even running the same race.

Here is what nobody likes to say out loud about that 19.69% growth figure.

That growth is not evidence the Philippine registration system is good. It is evidence of how relentless Filipinos are at trying to build despite a system that taxes their time before it taxes their revenue. The 79,165 in May are not a success story for the regulators. They are a success story for the regulators' opponents: the small business owners who decided the gauntlet was worth running anyway.

Every step in the registration process is a fee. Every fee is a small kingdom. Take the friction away, and a lot of people lose their cut. Friction is not an accident. Friction is the business model.

This is why a huge slice of Filipino employment quietly sits inside the informal economy. Informal is not laziness. Informal is a rational response to the cost of being formal. The math has been quietly teaching entrepreneurs for years that compliance can eat more margin than the penalty for skipping it.

What the DTI's growth print actually proves is the same thing Filipino workers abroad have been proving for fifty years.

Filipino nurses run wards in the UK, in Canada, in the Gulf, in NZ. Filipino engineers design infrastructure in Singapore. Filipino seafarers move global trade. Filipino entrepreneurs build successful businesses in dozens of countries the moment they are dropped into a functioning environment to build in.

The Filipino is not broken. The Filipino is the engine.

The system the Filipino is forced to build inside is the brake.

A country whose people are this willing to start something deserves a state that is willing to make starting something easier. That is not asking for utopia. That is asking for one online form. One reasonable fee. One certificate. One country that finally treats a Filipino entrepreneur the way every functional country in the world already does.

79,165 in one month, and the system still made them earn it twice.

Hindi dapat ganito kahirap magsimula.
