## Caption (ready to publish)

The headline this week was that inflation "eased" to 6.8% in May.

The government's own target band is 2 to 4 percent.

So the country is being asked to celebrate a number that is still almost twice the ceiling the government set for itself.

Look at how the print actually breaks down, because the front page and the receipt are telling very different stories.

The Philippine Statistics Authority reported on June 5 that headline inflation slowed from 7.2% in April to 6.8% in May. Year-to-date, the average sits at 4.5%, above the official 2-4% target.

About 70% of that slowdown came from a single sector: transport. Transport inflation decelerated to 16.2% in May from 21.4% in April. Diesel year-on-year fell to 58.5% from 122.7%. Gasoline year-on-year slowed to 51.6% from 59.6%.

Those numbers are not a story about policy. They are a story about base effects on global fuel prices. Last year's spike is rolling out of the comparison window. The "easing" is the calendar doing the work, not the cabinet.

Now look at the part the headline does not lead with.

Rice inflation accelerated. It went from 13.7% in April to 15.6% in May. Corn inflation climbed from 21.0% to 25.5%. The two cheapest staples on the Filipino plate, the ones the bottom half of the country actually eats every day, got more expensive faster.

Food inflation overall eased slightly to around 5.7%, but the easing was carried by vegetables, fish, and meat. Rice and corn, the staples, went the other way.

Talk to any Filipino household running a weekly grocery budget right now and the story is the same. The fuel pump is slightly less brutal because the global cycle has turned. The kilo of bigas is heavier than ever.

This is what economists call a base effect easing on top of a structural problem. The print looks better. The bowl does not.

Now place the Philippine number next to a country with similar volatility exposure and a real target band.

New Zealand's annual inflation was 3.1% in the 12 months to March 2026. The Reserve Bank of New Zealand's official target band is 1 to 3 percent. NZ inflation is at the upper end of its band. The Reserve Bank treats that as a problem worth a policy response, not a victory.

No country is perfect. NZ households are also being squeezed: electricity went up 12.5%, local authority rates rose 8.8%, meat climbed 4.0%, and rents have been a multi-year political issue. The point is not that Auckland is a postcard. The point is that the country at the top of its target band is treating "3.1" as a problem to fix, and the country almost twice over its target band is treating "6.8" as a win.

That is two different definitions of what citizens are owed.

Filipino households are not being told the truth about what an inflation target is for.

A target band is not a goal to wave at when the print comes down. It is a promise about purchasing power. It says, in plain language, that the government will defend the floor under the value of a peso so that wages, savings, and pensions are not silently taxed by price drift. When the band is set at 2-4% and the print is 6.8%, that promise is being broken. When the band is set at 2-4% and the YTD average is 4.5%, the promise is being broken on average, not on a one-off bad month.

Calling that "easing" without calling it a target miss is not journalism. It is a tone choice.

The deeper issue is the one almost nobody names in the press release.

Filipinos are being trained to treat the bare minimum as a milestone. Filipinos who travel, work, or run small businesses overseas notice this fast. Other countries calibrate their inflation narratives around the floor citizens are owed. Filipinos at home are calibrated around how bad it almost was.

A Filipino sari-sari store owner buying a sack of bigas this week does not feel a 6.8% headline. The sari-sari store owner feels a 15.6% rice number. The Filipino jeepney driver does not feel a 16.2% transport headline. The driver feels gasoline at 51.6% above last year.

The receipt is the actual economy. The press release is its narration.

The headline number is a poll-test, not a policy. The print eased. The target missed. The staples climbed. The bowl shrank.

A country that celebrates "less bad" is a country that has been short-changed long enough to forget what "good" is supposed to look like.

Hindi pag-unti ang pagbaba ng problema.
