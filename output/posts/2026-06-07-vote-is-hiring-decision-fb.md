# 2026-06-07 - A vote is a hiring decision (Facebook)

Imagine hiring an employee the way Filipinos elect officials.

The position is general manager. Salary, benefits, security detail, six-year term, decision-making authority over public budgets. The candidate walks in. The interview panel asks for a CV. The candidate hands over a campaign tarpaulin.

The panel asks about prior experience. The candidate says they have been on television.

The panel asks for references. The candidate names a cousin who already works at the company.

The panel asks for the candidate's plan for the first year. The candidate says the plan is that things will be better.

In any honest business, that interview ends in five minutes.

Now flip it. That is the Philippine electoral process for one of the most consequential roles in the country.

Filipinos are asked to choose senators, congresspeople, governors, mayors, and a president, and the criteria the system actually rewards are name recognition, surface-level charisma, perceived ability to deliver patronage, and a faint personal familiarity with the candidate's family. Survey data from SWS and Pulse Asia confirms this cycle after cycle. The voter is not voting on competencies. The voter is voting on what the system has trained them to evaluate.

This is not because Filipinos are bad hirers. Filipinos are excellent hirers in their own lives. Filipino HR teams sit on resumes for hours. Filipino small business owners ask sharp questions before taking on a new employee. The diaspora's reputation abroad is for diligence, attention to detail, and high standards in the workplace.

The same people, in the voting booth, are asked to evaluate candidates without the tools every other hiring decision uses.

There is no published job description. The Constitution gives high-level duties, but no working spec for the role. There is no required CV or work history. Candidates rarely submit a substantive plan. Debates, when they happen, are talk-show formats. Track records exist but are rarely surfaced; the candidates' previous performance in office, if any, is usually evaluated through campaign storytelling, not data. Independent assessments of competence are rare.

The candidate is, in effect, hiring themselves on the strength of a tarpaulin.

Compare to countries where voter education has been structured around competency assessment. New Zealand, the Nordics, Germany. Candidates publish detailed manifestos. Party platforms are codified. Independent broadcasters host long-form policy debates. Civic education starts in middle school and treats the act of voting as a serious deliberation, not a celebration. None of these systems are perfect. NZ has its own scandals; the Nordics have populist swings; Germany has coalition messiness. The structural difference is that voters in these systems are given the tools to evaluate candidates as if they were hiring them. And the candidates, knowing this, prepare for the interview.

In the Philippines, the candidates prepare for the camera.

Article XI, Section 1 of the 1987 Constitution says:

> "Public office is a public trust. Public officers and employees must, at all times, be accountable to the people, serve them with utmost responsibility, integrity, loyalty, and efficiency, act with patriotism and justice, and lead modest lives."

Read that as a job description. Utmost responsibility. Integrity. Loyalty. Efficiency. Modest lives.

Now consider how few of the candidates Filipinos have been asked to evaluate over the past four decades would have made it past round one in a serious interview against that standard.

That is not the voter's fault. That is the absence of an interview, full stop.

No country is perfect. Every electorate elects mediocre candidates sometimes. But systems with structured deliberation produce mediocrity that is at least competent. The Philippines is producing mediocrity that is openly hereditary, and is asking the voter to absorb the consequences.

A vote is a hiring decision. The Constitution already wrote the job description. The country has, for thirty-eight years, refused to make the candidates show up to the interview.

Dapat idemand natin ang gobyernong gumagana.

That demand starts with treating every ballot the way Filipinos already treat every other consequential hiring decision in their own lives.

The voter is ready. The interview is what is missing.
