# 2026-06-07 - Why voters re-elect the same families (Facebook)

The lazy answer is that Filipinos are stupid voters.

The lazy answer is wrong.

Filipino voters are some of the most strategic decision-makers in any electorate on earth. The system has just trained them to be strategic about the wrong things.

Every election cycle, the same families return to office. Senate, House, governorships, mayoralties, vice-positions, barangay captaincies. The surnames change slowly, if at all. Academic studies from the AIM Policy Center and the Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism have documented the pattern for more than a decade. The Philippines has one of the highest documented incumbency advantages of any electoral democracy.

Pundits look at the result and ask, with a faintly exasperated tone, why "Filipinos keep electing the same people." The framing is wrong before the sentence is finished. Filipinos are not electing the same people randomly. Filipinos are electing the same people because the system has made every other choice expensive.

Here is the structural picture.

The PH party system is weak. Political parties switch members after every election. The party logos are stickers, not platforms. A senator can run under one party and serve under another within a single term. Voters cannot pick a party with confidence because there is no party with a stable platform to pick. So voters pick a name. A name they recognise. A name they have been hearing since they were children. A name that has done something for somebody they know.

The candidate's family has been on the ballot since the voter's grandparents were on the ballot. The candidate has the campaign budget to drown the precinct in tarpaulins, sound trucks, sponsored Facebook ads, and Christmas envelopes. The candidate's network can deliver on small, immediate favours, a job referral, a hospital bill, an emergency transport, a coffin, a scholarship, a tricycle license. The challenger does not have any of that.

The voter who chooses the dynasty candidate is not voting for a dynasty. The voter is voting for the only candidate who has been proven, in the voter's own lived experience, to actually deliver something tangible. The state failed to provide functioning public services. The dynasty filled the gap with personal patronage. The voter, doing the math, picks the patronage.

This is not stupidity. This is rational behaviour inside a system that has been engineered to make rational behaviour look stupid from outside.

Compare to countries that have walked away from this pattern. Germany, the Nordics, New Zealand to a meaningful degree. None of these systems are perfect. All of them, though, have done some combination of three things: strong public financing of elections, robust public services that reduce the value of personal patronage, and proportional representation rules that make party platforms matter more than family names. The result is that incumbency advantage is lower, dynastic seats are rarer, and voters cast ballots on policies rather than on Christmas envelopes.

The Philippines has done none of those three things at scale.

Public financing of elections is minimal. Public services are partial. Proportional representation exists only in the party-list system, which has its own well-documented capture problems.

What the country has done is hold elections. On schedule. Every cycle. With ink, ballots, watchers, and the language of democracy. Then the seats go back to the same surnames, and a national conversation begins about how the voters keep failing the system.

The voters are not failing the system. The system was built to produce this result, and it is producing it on time.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own oligarchic features. Corporate concentration, Auckland centrism, the awkward dominance of two parties in older cycles. The point is not that NZ has done the perfect thing. The point is that the Philippines already knows what the structural fixes look like, has known for decades, and has chosen not to install them.

The dynasties are not powerful because Filipinos love them.

The dynasties are powerful because they have built a parallel social safety net that the public state was supposed to provide, and they have made themselves the only candidate who can convincingly promise to continue providing it.

The way to weaken that grip is not to lecture voters about better voting. The way to weaken that grip is to build the public services that make personal patronage redundant.

Functioning hospitals. Functioning schools. Functioning transport. Functioning permit offices. Functioning courts. A working CIC for credit. A working PhilHealth for health.

A republic that delivers those does not need to ask its voters to be braver. The voter, given a real choice, picks the better one. That has been true everywhere a government has finally bothered to deliver.

The Filipino voter is ready. The state has not been.
