# 2026-06-08 - PhilHealth: where the contributions actually go (Facebook)

Every working Filipino pays PhilHealth.

The premium is now five percent of monthly salary, split between employer and employee for the formally employed, paid in full by the self-employed and OFWs.

Year after year, an enormous pool of money flows into the agency.

Year after year, the Commission on Audit publishes the same uncomfortable findings.

Year after year, families still get to the hospital admissions counter and are told the case rate covers only a slice.

That is the gap this post is about.

PhilHealth's contribution rate has climbed steadily under the Universal Health Care Law. Under the current schedule, formally employed Filipinos see five percent of their monthly salary go into the system. Self-paying members and OFWs pay the full rate themselves. Over millions of contributors over decades, the agency has accumulated some of the largest insurance reserves in the country.

What comes out is uneven.

PhilHealth pays benefits on a case rate. That means a fixed peso amount per condition, regardless of what the hospital actually charges. The patient or the family pays the rest. The "rest" is often the larger share, especially for serious illness, ICU stays, and dialysis. Filipinos who have endured a serious medical event in the family already know this math. The PhilHealth slip covers part. The receipt at the cashier covers the rest.

That gap is not a private failing. It is a coverage design choice.

Then come the COA reports.

The Commission on Audit, year after year, has flagged PhilHealth for billions of pesos in questionable disbursements. Ghost dialysis sessions. Benefit packages released without complete documentation. Unliquidated cash advances. Procurement irregularities. The COA findings are not hot takes from a tabloid. They are the constitutional audit body of the Philippines doing its job. The findings are repeatedly the same year over year, which is itself the indictment.

Add to that the recurring controversies over PhilHealth fund balances, accumulated reserves that periodically become the subject of national debate. Transfer to the treasury. Reversion. Reallocation. The numbers move on slide decks and press releases without ever quite landing as additional benefit at the cashier window.

The Filipino contributor is doing the right thing.

The system has not been doing the right thing back.

Compare to functioning public health systems abroad. NZ funds public hospital care directly out of general taxation. No separate "health premium" deduction shows up on a Kiwi payslip. The cost is socialised at the tax base, and the benefit shows up at the point of care in the form of admissions that do not bill the patient. The system is not perfect. NZ has its own wait times, its own staffing shortages, its own underfunded regions. The structural design is different. The taxpayer is paying once; the citizen is receiving care once.

In PH, the contributor is paying twice. Once through the PhilHealth premium. Again at the cashier window when the case rate does not cover the bill.

No country is perfect. NZ healthcare has problems. But the architecture is honest. There is no premium-collecting public insurer that captures contributions and then quietly refuses to fully cover the events the contributions were ostensibly for.

Article II, Section 4 of the Constitution says the prime duty of the government is to serve and protect the people. A health system that requires every working Filipino to pay in monthly, then asks the same Filipino to pay again at the moment of getting sick, is failing the prime duty in the most concrete possible way.

The fix is not to stop paying PhilHealth contributions. The fix is to demand that the institution Filipinos already fund actually deliver what it was designed to deliver. Honest case rates that reflect real medical costs. COA findings that are answered, not absorbed. Fund balances that translate into expanded benefits, not into accounting maneuvers.

The contributor has held up their end. The contract has not been honoured.

Saan napupunta ang PhilHealth ng bawat Pilipino?

The honest answer is the one Filipinos have been waiting for an institution to give them for thirty years.
