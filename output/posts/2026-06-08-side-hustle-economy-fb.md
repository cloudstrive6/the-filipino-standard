# 2026-06-08 - The side hustle economy (Facebook)

The side hustle is the most honest unemployment number in the country.

When a Filipino office worker also runs a meal-prep account, drives Grab on weekends, sells imported skincare on Shopee, manages a private tutoring schedule, and books a small printing service from a phone at lunch, that is not entrepreneurial spirit. That is the labour market admitting it cannot pay a living wage.

Filipinos working two and three jobs at once are not chasing passion projects. They are doing the only thing the math allows. The salary, by itself, does not cover the rent, the kuryente, the school baon, the meralco, the meralco again, the unexpected hospital trip, and the small monthly amount sent to the parents in the province.

The PSA Labor Force Survey routinely captures large shares of multi-jobholders. The Bangko Sentral's Consumer Expectations Survey has tracked household financial positions through real-wage erosion. Real wages in PH have lagged inflation in multiple recent quarters. In several regions, the legislated minimum wage has been below the family living wage threshold for years.

The side hustle did not happen because Filipinos suddenly became more entrepreneurial. The side hustle happened because the primary wage stopped working.

That said, here is the second story the country does not tell loudly enough.

A meaningful share of registered Filipino MSMEs began life as a side hustle. The carinderia that now feeds two staff started in a kitchen at home on weekends. The online seller that now imports its own stock started by reposting from Shopee at lunch breaks. The Grab rider with three motorcycles started with one motorcycle and a part-time night shift. The tutoring agency started with a single Saturday morning student.

The side hustle is not just a survival mechanism. It is the soil from which future Filipino SMEs are emerging.

The state has not really noticed.

There is no coherent national program for graduating side hustlers into formal small businesses. DTI Negosyo Centers exist, but they are not where the side hustle owner goes when she finally has enough income to consider registering. There is no clean, fast, online business-registration path that takes a single Filipino with a side income from informal to legitimate in an afternoon. The friction described in earlier posts (SEC, BIR, mayor's permit, barangay, sanitary, fire safety) is exactly the friction that keeps the side hustler informal.

The country, in effect, is asking its workers to be two people at once, then taxing one of them and ignoring the other.

A working state would treat the side hustle ecosystem as the most important economic development pipeline in the country. Streamline registration. Provide tax-friendly graduation paths from informal to formal. Build proper cash-flow-based credit access. Standardise platform-worker protections. Let the side hustler become the SME owner without losing six months to forms.

A working state would, in short, treat its overworked workers as the founders of the next generation of Filipino businesses, rather than as a labour surplus that the formal sector failed to absorb.

No country is perfect. The gig economy in NZ, Australia, the US, all of them have their own version of this problem. Platform workers without benefits. Side hustlers stuck below the formalisation threshold. Real wages eroded by housing costs. None of those countries have figured this out perfectly. They have all, however, at least admitted that the side hustle is a structural part of their labour market and need policy attention.

The Philippines is still treating the side hustle as anecdote.

Two and three jobs is not a Filipino value. It is what an underbuilt economy looks like in real time.

A working economy makes one job enough for a working day. A failed economy makes two jobs the rent.

The Filipino worker is not the problem.

The Filipino worker is paying twice for a state that has been delivering once.

Tama na ang dalawang trabaho para mabuhay.
