In New Zealand, an independent body can investigate the police commissioner himself.

In the Philippines, the police mostly investigate the police.

That gap is not an accident. It is a structural choice.

New Zealand's Independent Police Conduct Authority, the IPCA, has existed since 1989. It is a statutory body. It reports directly to Parliament. It is not a unit inside the New Zealand Police. It is not supervised by a police commander. It is not staffed by people whose next promotion depends on the goodwill of the officers they are investigating.

When a Kiwi citizen lodges a complaint against an officer, the IPCA can take that complaint, investigate it, and publish its findings. When something serious happens, the IPCA leads the review. The police force can disagree with the findings. It cannot bury them.

Now look at the Philippine architecture.

The Internal Affairs Service exists inside the PNP, created under the 1998 PNP Reform Act. NAPOLCOM sits above the PNP as the national police commission. At the city and municipal level, the People's Law Enforcement Board, the PLEB, handles citizen complaints. On paper, three layers of oversight.

In practice, the three layers blur into each other and into the PNP itself. The Internal Affairs Service is staffed by police personnel inside a police chain of command. NAPOLCOM's supervisory teeth depend on political will from the executive branch. PLEBs are unevenly resourced from one city to another, and most Filipinos have never been told the PLEB exists, let alone how to file a complaint there.

When a high-profile case happens, the public watches a press conference. A commander is "relieved." A unit is "restructured." An investigation is "ongoing." The news cycle moves. Most cases never produce a verifiable, public finding. The structural reason is not that Filipino investigators are weaker than Kiwi investigators. It is that the system was designed so the police would mostly answer to the police.

No country is perfect. The IPCA has its own critics in New Zealand. Some Kiwi advocates argue it should have stronger prosecutorial power. Maori communities have raised concerns about how complaints are processed. The IPCA is not above review.

But the floor is on a different planet. The floor is: an independent statutory body exists, it has access, it can name the police commissioner if the facts require it, and its findings are made public.

The 1987 Philippine Constitution, in Article XVI, Section 6, defines the PNP as "civilian in character." A civilian force is supposed to be accountable to civilians. Not symbolically. Operationally. With an independent body that can take a complaint, investigate it, and publish what it found, regardless of who is wearing the uniform.

That body does not exist in the Philippines. Three different agencies share fragments of the job. None of them is independent in the structural sense the word implies in countries where police accountability actually works.

This is why the same pattern repeats. Why the same kind of incident produces the same kind of press conference and the same kind of silence six months later. Why most Filipinos have stopped expecting names, evidence, and consequences from the system that was supposed to deliver them.

Accountability is not a personality trait of an institution. It is a design.

Walang independent body. Walang public na findings. Walang nanagot.

That sentence should bother the country until it changes.
