A Filipino SME today often runs payments through four channels at once. Personal GCash. Business GCash. Bank transfer screenshots. InstaPay QR.

At the end of the month, the owner reconciles all of them by hand in a notebook.

That is not because the country has no digital rails. It does. InstaPay is real-time. PESONet handles batch. GCash and Maya have moved tens of millions of Filipinos into digital payments faster than any government program in the country's history. That part of the story is real, and it is good.

The gap that is rarely talked about is what happens once the business gets bigger than a sari-sari counter.

A real business needs three things from its payment infrastructure. Clean payout reconciliation, so the books match the bank. Recurring billing, so the B2B invoice on net-30 terms is collected without chasing. And a proper business account, separate from the owner's personal money, with statements that an accountant can actually use.

The Philippine consumer payments stack is excellent. The Philippine business payments stack still asks the owner to do most of the work.

Now look at how a Filipino entrepreneur in New Zealand experiences the same problem.

New business registered online in about an hour. Business bank account opened with that certificate of incorporation. An EFTPOS terminal arranged with the bank, often delivered within days. Direct debit arrangements set up under the Bank Direct Debit Authority for recurring B2B billing, with the customer's bank, not with a screenshot.

By the end of the first week, the business is taking payments through the same rails as every other business in the country. The reconciliation report is generated by the bank. The terminal hardware is supported by the bank. The integration with accounting software is standard. The owner is back to running the business instead of running the back office.

The difference is not that Kiwi entrepreneurs are smarter. The difference is that the rails were designed with the small operator in mind, and the bank's commercial team showed up.

No country is perfect. NZ banks charge fees that frustrate small operators too. Card surcharging has its own debates. Wholesale banking competition there is also concentrated. None of this is utopia.

But the floor is different. The Kiwi SME starts at "the rails are ready, plug in." The Filipino SME starts at "build your own workflow on top of three apps and hope nothing breaks."

Talk to any Filipino business owner who has scaled past a one-person operation. The same complaints come up. Statements that don't reconcile cleanly. Receipts that don't carry the right VAT formatting. Customers who pay through a personal account because the business onboarding was too much friction. Bookkeepers who do half their work in Excel because the bank exports are messy.

Filipino developers have built brilliant fintech products. Filipino SMEs have adopted them in volume. What is still missing is the institutional plumbing that turns those products into something a small business can run on without heroics.

The country built the consumer rails fast. The B2B rails are the next layer. They are not optional. An SME economy that cannot reconcile its books cannot grow into the formal economy that the country keeps saying it wants.

Pwede naman pala, kung may gusto lang.

The capacity is there. The political and commercial decision to finish the job is the missing piece.
