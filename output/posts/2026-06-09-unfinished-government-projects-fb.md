# 2026-06-09 - Unfinished government projects (Facebook)

The Philippines has built a national landscape of half-finished government projects.

A flyover that ends mid-air over a busy intersection. A waiting shed with three walls and no roof. A school building whose typhoon damage was photographed, allocated, and then quietly left as is. A drainage system whose first kilometre got built and whose remaining nine kilometres became a footnote in a budget no one revisits.

This is not folklore. The Commission on Audit publishes it. Year after year, the COA documents large shares of allocated infrastructure budgets being underspent, mis-executed, or unaccounted for. The Department of Budget and Management's own data on underspending tracks the same pattern. Multiple administrations have promised audits. Few have produced enforceable consequences.

A flyover that ends mid-air is not a bug. It is the system working exactly as designed.

Here is the structural anatomy of how it happens.

A budget is allocated. A line item appears in the General Appropriations Act. A contract is bid out, often within a narrow set of pre-qualified contractors. Construction begins. Initial progress is photographed and posted online. The project enters the news cycle with a "groundbreaking" event, complete with hard hats and shovels and a tarpaulin showing the smiling face of whoever holds the relevant seat.

Then the work slows. The contractor cites a delay. The agency cites a delay. The right-of-way is contested. A geotechnical survey reveals an issue. The funds get reallocated. The next administration arrives. The project is "under review."

Years later, the rebar is rusting. Vegetation has grown along the unfinished edges. A new tarpaulin has been pasted over the old one. The smiling face is different. The verdict is the same.

The Commission on Audit has documented this pattern, year after year, decade after decade. The numbers are not partisan. The agencies are not partisan. The findings sit in the annual reports for anyone willing to read them. Reputable broadsheets, BusinessWorld, Inquirer, Rappler, periodically run features on "ghost projects" and stalled flyovers. The reporting is consistent. The follow-through is not.

This is not all corruption. Some projects stall for legitimate reasons. Right-of-way disputes are real. Contractor disputes happen. Geotechnical surveys do produce surprises. The point is not that every unfinished project is criminal. The point is that the pattern, in aggregate, points to a structural failure: a state that consistently allocates more than it executes, and consistently allows execution failures to disappear into the next budget cycle without consequence.

Compare to systems that publish project dashboards. NZ Transport Agency publishes a project map with status, funding, and contractor information. UK Infrastructure and Projects Authority publishes annual reports with progress on every major capital project, including the troubled ones. Singapore's project execution is closely audited in real time. None of these countries have eliminated delay. All of them have, however, made delay legible. The state is held to its own published schedule.

In the Philippines, the published schedule is the launch event. After that, the schedule belongs to whoever is managing the silence.

Article XI, Section 1 of the 1987 Constitution says:

> "Public office is a public trust. Public officers and employees must, at all times, be accountable to the people, serve them with utmost responsibility, integrity, loyalty, and efficiency, act with patriotism and justice, and lead modest lives."

Read "efficiency" as the operative word here.

A state that allocates budgets it does not execute is failing the efficiency standard the Constitution imposes. A state that turns "groundbreaking" into a media event and "completion" into a footnote is failing public trust.

The fix is not new programs. The fix is publishing what already exists.

A national project dashboard with status, funding, contractor, and timeline. COA findings linked to specific projects, surfaced to the public in plain language. Consequences attached to chronic non-execution, not just to ribbon cuttings. A media culture that asks where the second kilometre of drainage went rather than where the next groundbreaking will be.

None of this is technologically impossible. The Philippines has the digital infrastructure. The Philippines has the public servants who could maintain such a dashboard. The Philippines does not have the political will, because the half-built flyover is profitable to the people whose budgets are not being executed, and embarrassing only to the people standing under it in the rain.

No country is perfect. NZ has its own delayed road projects. The UK has its own Crossrail-style cost overruns. Singapore has its own quiet recalibrations. The structural difference is that those delays get reported in the next quarter's dashboard. The Philippines' delays get hidden in the next administration's review.

A republic that finishes what it starts is a republic that respects its own citizens' time.

Tama na ang lumang patron na may bagong tarpaulin.

The flyover that ends mid-air is not a metaphor.

It is the receipt.
