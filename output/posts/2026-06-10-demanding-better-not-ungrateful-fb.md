"Ungrateful ka lang kasi nasa abroad."

Every Filipino who has criticized the country from overseas has heard that line.

It is not a serious argument. It is a tactic to end the conversation.

Every year, OFWs send home around USD 38 billion. That is the country's largest single source of foreign currency. It is roughly 8 to 10 percent of the entire Philippine GDP. The Philippine peso, the Philippine retail economy, the Philippine real estate market in many regions, all of these are propped up by the Filipinos who had to leave to keep their families fed back home.

Almost two million OFWs are deployed every year. Many of them work conditions that few back home would tolerate. They miss birthdays, school recognitions, their kids growing up. They send the money. They build the house. They put a sibling through college. They keep the country liquid.

And then when one of them looks at a news headline from Manila and says "this is not okay," somebody in the comments tells them they are ungrateful.

That move needs to be named for what it is.

Telling a Filipino that paying attention to their country from abroad is a betrayal of the country is one of the most efficient ways to keep accountability silent. Because the diaspora is uniquely positioned to make the comparison. Filipinos who have lived in countries with functioning hospitals, functioning roads, functioning courts, functioning utilities, can see what is missing back home with a clarity that is much harder to access when you grew up inside the system and were taught from childhood that "ganyan talaga."

That clarity is a national asset, not a national problem.

Filipinos who criticize the country from abroad are not turning their backs. They are showing receipts. They are saying: it is possible to have a public hospital that does not bankrupt you, it is possible to register a business in an hour, it is possible to have an electric utility that pays a dividend back to the household. They have seen it. They have lived it. They send the remittance home anyway.

That is not ingratitude. That is love with information.

The 1987 Constitution, Article III, Section 4, is clear about it:

> "No law shall be passed abridging the freedom of speech, of expression, or of the press, or the right of the people peaceably to assemble and petition the government for redress of grievances."

The right to petition the government for redress of grievances is not a foreign concept. It is a Filipino constitutional right. Filipinos abroad do not lose it the moment their plane takes off from NAIA. They take it with them.

So the next time the "ungrateful" reflex shows up under a post, name it. It is a control mechanism. It exists to protect the people who benefit from the country staying exactly the way it is. It is not an argument. It is a leash.

OFWs paid for the house. The house is the country. They have every right to walk through it and ask why the wiring is broken.

Demanding better is not betrayal. It is the most patriotic posture available.

Hindi kayo walang-utang-na-loob. Kayo ang nagbabayad. Tama lang na may say din kayo.
