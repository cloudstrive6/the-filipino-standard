A senator's wristwatch is worth more than a Filipino public school teacher earns in three years.

That is not a metaphor.

It is the math.

Around 17 million Filipinos live below the poverty line, per the latest PSA poverty incidence figures. The median Filipino family takes home what most government officials spend on a single weekend abroad. That gap is not new. What is worth saying out loud is that the gap is the story, not a footnote to it.

The 1987 Philippine Constitution, Article XI, Section 1, defines the standard:

> "Public office is a public trust. Public officers and employees must, at all times, be accountable to the people, serve them with utmost responsibility, integrity, loyalty, and efficiency; act with patriotism and justice, and lead modest lives."

Read the last three words again. "Lead modest lives."

That phrase is not decorative. The framers of the Constitution chose it on purpose. They had just come out of a regime defined by displays of wealth that the country could not afford. They wrote the floor in. Public office is supposed to look modest. Not because modesty is a virtue in itself, but because a public servant in a country with 17 million poor citizens has no business living visibly above them on a public salary.

Now compare the constitutional standard to what the country actually sees on its feeds.

A convoy that includes vehicles each worth more than a barangay health center's annual budget. A watch that is the price of three rural classrooms. A foreign trip schedule that exceeds the official travel of leaders from much wealthier countries. Branded events. Tarpaulins with the official's face larger than the project he is opening. A jewelry collection that quietly outpaces every honest source of declared income.

None of this is hidden. Most of it is on social media, posted by the officials themselves.

And every time it surfaces, the same defense appears in the comments. "Pwede naman, sahod naman 'yan." "Bakit, may inutang sila sa'yo?" "Inggit lang." "Hindi naman lahat corrupt."

That defense has a structural problem.

If a public servant's lifestyle visibly exceeds the SALN they filed, the Constitution has a word for that. It is not "lifestyle." It is a discrepancy. And it is one of the few questions the Filipino public is constitutionally entitled to ask, out loud, in writing, in court.

The argument that "they earned it elsewhere" assumes the elsewhere is clean. The argument that "everyone in business has nice things" assumes the public office is, in fact, a business. It is not. The Constitution says so.

What makes this story worse is the public side of the same frame.

Outside the Senate, the parking lot fills with luxury vehicles. Outside a public hospital in the same city, the parking lot fills with old jeeps, tricycles, and people who took two rides to get there because of a relative they cannot afford to lose. Same country. Same week. Same constitutional framework, supposedly applied equally.

A country that cannot align its politicians' visible lifestyles with its constitutional standard is not failing optics. It is failing the document its government swore an oath to.

This is why "lead modest lives" is the line that should be read aloud at every senate hearing, every committee meeting, every campaign launch.

Filipinos are not asking their officials to be poor. They are asking them to be aligned. The country's poverty rate and the country's politicians' luxury are not two different facts. They are the same fact, told from opposite sides of the same gate.

Pampublikong tungkulin, hindi pampribadong negosyo.

That is the Constitution speaking. It is past time the country held it to its own words.
