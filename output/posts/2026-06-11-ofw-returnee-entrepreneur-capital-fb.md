A Filipino comes home after fifteen years abroad with a lump sum from a lifetime of overtime.

The country offers her three default options. A sari-sari store. A tricycle. A jeepney.

Two years later, two of the three are gone, and so is much of the savings.

This is not a story about effort. It is a story about a country that has not built itself to receive its own returning capital well.

Around 1.8 to 2 million OFWs are deployed every year. A meaningful slice of them eventually come home for good. Each one carries years of savings, real skills picked up from foreign workplaces, hard-won discipline, and a network. In any economy designed to put returning capital to work, that flow would be a national asset rivaled by few others.

In the Philippines, the flow lands in a system that mostly hands the returnee a permit form and wishes them luck.

The OWWA reintegration program exists. The Department of Migrant Workers exists. On paper, there are loans, training, business advisory services, and reintegration grants. In practice, the support is thinly resourced, geographically uneven, and frequently invisible to the very people it is supposed to serve. A returnee in a coastal town in Eastern Samar has a different OWWA experience from a returnee in Quezon City, and neither typically resembles the brochure.

So the returnee builds the only thing the surrounding environment seems to support.

The sari-sari is set up because everyone in the neighbourhood is already running one and it does not require permits beyond barangay clearance. The tricycle is bought because the franchise market in town allows it. The jeepney is acquired because a relative knows a relative. None of these are bad decisions. They are rational decisions inside a system that did not present better ones.

Compare that to how returning capital is received in countries the OFW has just spent years working in.

In New Zealand, when a worker accumulates KiwiSaver and savings and wants to start something, business.govt.nz, Callaghan Innovation, regional growth councils, and bank SME desks are all reachable and used. The conversation begins at "what do you actually want to build, and what does the market need," not "fill out these forms and try not to lose money in the first year."

In Singapore, returning entrepreneurs find SkillsFuture credits, Enterprise Singapore grants, and a formal SME ecosystem ready to take their capital and skills.

In Canada, immigrant and returnee entrepreneur programs route capital into structured incubators, with mentorship and follow-on funding built in.

These are not perfect systems. KiwiSaver and Callaghan have their gaps. Singapore's grant programs are competitive. Canada's incubators do not save every venture. No country is perfect.

But the floor is on a different planet. The floor is: the returning capital meets infrastructure that has been put there to receive it. The decision tree for the returnee includes more than three options.

The Philippine returnee deserves the same floor.

A sari-sari is an honourable business. A jeepney is an honourable business. A tricycle franchise is an honourable business. The objection is not to any of them. The objection is to a country where, for hundreds of thousands of returning OFWs every year, those three options are the entire menu.

This is the cost of treating the OFW economy as a cash flow rather than as a strategic national asset. The dollar comes in, the home is built, the schooling is paid, the savings are saved. And then when the worker comes home with capital, skills, and ambition, the country has not bothered to set the table.

The diaspora has been carrying this economy for forty years. The least the country owes them is a real plan for what happens when they come home with the money they earned.

Tama na ang sari-sari, tricycle, jeep bilang default. Marami pang pwede, kung tutulungan lang sila ng sariling bayan.
