Filipino households pay among the highest electricity rates in Southeast Asia.

Not "expensive for our income." Expensive in absolute peso-per-kilowatt-hour, on a chart with every ASEAN neighbour.

That is not a price tag. That is a verdict on a decades-long policy choice.

Look at a Meralco bill the way an analyst would. The generation charge, the cost of buying power from generation companies, makes up roughly 55 to 60 percent of the total. Transmission, paid to NGCP for moving power across the grid, takes another slice. Distribution is what Meralco itself collects to run the wires that reach the house. System loss, the electricity that disappears between the plant and the meter, is charged back to the customer. Then taxes and government charges, including a 12 percent VAT on most components.

Compare those line items to neighbours.

Indonesia and Malaysia keep household power cheaper through subsidies and a different fuel mix. Vietnam's state-owned utility runs at lower margins. Thailand's grid is structured around long-term planning that smooths volatility. Even Singapore, with no domestic fuel reserves, often lands at a household rate not far above Manila's, because the wholesale market is tightly regulated.

The Philippine grid is different by design. It is private at almost every layer. Generation is dominated by a handful of private players. Transmission is a private concession. Distribution in Metro Manila is Meralco, which has never been community-owned in its 122-year history. The regulator, the ERC, signs off on rates that have rarely produced material reductions when input costs fall, but consistently produce increases when input costs rise.

That asymmetry is the structural story.

The generation mix matters too. PH still leans heavily on coal, much of it imported. Imported coal means every shock in the global commodity market lands inside a Filipino household within a billing cycle. Renewables have grown, but their share is still small enough that the country is exposed to the price tail of fossil inputs while paying premium retail rates.

The 1987 Philippine Constitution, Article XII, Section 6, names a principle that should be inconvenient for this arrangement:

> "The use of property bears a social function, and all economic agents shall contribute to the common good."

In plain English: a private business that controls something essential like electricity has a constitutional obligation to operate in the public interest, not only in shareholder interest. That obligation is supposed to be enforced by regulators. The ERC was built precisely to play that role. The track record speaks for itself.

No country is perfect. New Zealand's power market has its own controversies, including pricing during dry hydro years. Europe wrestles with green transition costs. Singapore's tightly managed market trades flexibility for stability. There is no clean system anywhere.

But the question is not whether the perfect system exists. The question is why a country with 118 million people, an Asian middle-income economy with real industrial potential, pays among the highest household rates in the region while the public utility that distributes power in its capital has never paid a single peso back to the households it bills.

The answer is not "it is hard." Other developing economies have done it. The answer is that the structure was chosen this way, and every administration since the 1990s has chosen not to rebuild it.

Cost is not destiny. Cost is design.
