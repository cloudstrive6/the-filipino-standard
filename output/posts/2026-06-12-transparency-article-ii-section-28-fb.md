The 1987 Constitution has a clause most Filipinos have never been walked through.

It is one of the most powerful sentences in the document. And almost nobody in power wants the country to read it carefully.

Article II, Section 28:

> "Subject to reasonable conditions prescribed by law, the State adopts and implements a policy of full public disclosure of all its transactions involving public interest."

Read that twice. Full public disclosure. All its transactions. Involving public interest.

That is not a vibe. That is a constitutional mandate. The framers of the 1987 Constitution had just come out of fourteen years of a regime built on secrecy. They wrote transparency into the foundational document on purpose. Disclosure was supposed to be the default. Secrecy was supposed to be the exception that required legal justification.

The Constitution backs this up in Article III, Section 7:

> "The right of the people to information on matters of public concern shall be recognized."

Two clauses. One says the State must disclose. The other says the people have the right to know. Together they form the constitutional architecture for transparency in the Philippines.

Now ask, honestly: when did the country last see either clause honored in spirit?

The Freedom of Information policy in the Philippines is an executive order, EO 2 series of 2016. It covers the executive branch only. Congress and the judiciary are outside it. There is still no Freedom of Information Act covering all branches of government, almost four decades after the Constitution was ratified.

Confidential funds and intelligence funds have grown across multiple agencies, including agencies whose work is not classified by nature. The audit trail for these funds is routinely shielded from public release, with "national security" invoked even when no security interest is at stake.

SALN access, the Statement of Assets, Liabilities and Net Worth that public officials are constitutionally required to file, has been progressively walled off. The document is supposed to be public. The practice has drifted into "request through proper channels, expect denial."

Government project disclosures, contractor names, contract values, completion timelines, all of these are nominally public under procurement law. The lived experience for an ordinary Filipino trying to access them is queues, denials, and "the system is down today."

Each of these patterns, taken individually, has a defensible administrative excuse. Taken together, they describe a system that has steadily eroded the constitutional default.

What the Constitution wrote in is one line. What the bureaucracy has built around it is a maze.

This is why "FOIA pa rin tayo, tatlong dekada na" is not just a slogan. It is the literal status of constitutional transparency in 2026. The principle exists. The enabling law does not, fully. The lived experience reflects the gap.

Other countries have walked the same path differently. The United States has had FOIA since 1966. India's Right to Information Act has been in force since 2005. Estonia treats government data as open by default. None of those systems is perfect. All of them are at least operating from a presumption of disclosure.

The Philippines wrote the principle into the foundational text. The country is still waiting for the law that would make the principle operational across all three branches.

A constitutional clause is not self-enforcing. It is a contract between the state and the citizen. If one side does not honor it, the other side gets to ask, loudly and in public, why.

Sino ba talaga ang takot sa katotohanan?

The clause is there. Read it. Memorize the article and section. Article II, Section 28. Article III, Section 7. These are not academic citations. They are the receipts the country gets to wave back at any official who claims that transparency is somehow optional.
