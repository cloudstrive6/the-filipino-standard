In New Zealand, a family with two kids and a parent working full-time at the minimum wage gets help from the state.

In the Philippines, the same family is usually too "well off" to qualify for 4Ps, and far too short of comfortable to actually breathe.

That gap is not because one country loves its poor more than the other. It is a structural design choice.

Both countries run conditional or income-tested cash transfer programs. Look at how the architecture differs.

New Zealand's Working for Families program is paid through the Inland Revenue Department. It covers families with dependent children whose household income falls below a tiered threshold. The threshold is wide. It includes the Family Tax Credit, the In-Work Tax Credit for families with earned income, Best Start for newborns, and the Minimum Family Tax Credit. The point of the design is that supporting children should not stop at the poverty line. Working parents who are above the poverty line but below comfort still get supported, because the country has decided that raising children well is a public good.

The Philippine 4Ps, the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program, is codified by Republic Act 11310 in 2019. It covers around 4 million households below the poverty line. Eligibility is determined through Listahanan, the national household targeting system. The conditions are sensible: kids in school, regular health check-ups. The cash transfer is modest but real for the household that receives it.

Where the two diverge is the income band the program serves.

4Ps is poverty-line policy. The moment a Filipino family edges above that line, often through a single new income stream, the program tapers out. The household, still very far from comfortable in absolute terms, loses the support and re-enters the open market for everything: tuition, school uniforms, transport, medicine, food shocks.

Working for Families is family-of-working-class policy. A Kiwi family above the absolute poverty line is still eligible, in tiered amounts, well into the income band that the New Zealand state has decided is normal working life. The support tapers slowly. The cliff does not exist.

That structural difference matters enormously for the working poor.

In the Philippines, the working poor is the country's largest economic class. They are not eligible for 4Ps. They are not earning enough to absorb shocks. They are the family that is one hospital admission, one missed school year, one election-cycle ayuda gap away from sliding back below the poverty line they just climbed past. The program that exists does not reach them. The program that would reach them has not been built.

The 1987 Philippine Constitution, Article XIII, Section 1, instructs Congress to:

> "give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity, reduce social, economic, and political inequalities, and remove cultural inequities by equitably diffusing wealth and political power for the common good."

"Reduce social, economic, and political inequalities" does not stop at the poverty line. It is supposed to keep going up the income distribution. The 1987 framers did not write welfare for the very poor only. They wrote a constitutional aspiration to broad-based equity.

No country is perfect. Working for Families has its own debates in New Zealand: tapering rates, abatement thresholds, whether the In-Work Tax Credit penalizes single-parent households. Kiwi policy critics have plenty to say about it. The program is not above review.

But the floor is different. The floor in Wellington is that a working family with kids is not invisible to the state. The floor in Manila is that a working family with kids is mostly on their own the moment they earn a peso above the threshold.

4Ps is not the problem. 4Ps is too narrow to be the whole answer. The country needs a second, broader tier that catches the working poor and the lower middle. The Constitution already names that aspiration. Congress has had decades to build the policy.

Dapat may tulong na rin para sa mga nagtatrabaho, hindi lang para sa mga walang kita.

That sentence is not a slogan. It is a policy direction the Constitution actually asks for.
