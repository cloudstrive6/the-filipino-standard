Filipinos do not leave because they stopped loving the country.

They leave because the country has not been built to keep them.

That distinction matters. The country keeps having the wrong argument about brain drain.

Around 1.8 to 2 million Filipinos are deployed overseas every year. Filipino nurses staff wards in the United Kingdom, Canada, the United States, the Gulf, New Zealand. Filipino doctors run rural medical practices in Australia. Filipino engineers design infrastructure across Southeast Asia. Filipino seafarers move global trade. Filipino IT workers staff the back offices of multinational companies. Filipino hospitality workers run hotels on six continents.

Every single one of those workers was educated, trained, raised, and quietly subsidized by the Philippines. Most of them tried to make it work at home first. The vast majority of them would have preferred not to leave.

The question that almost never gets asked in the brain drain conversation is the structural one.

What does a country have to offer a top-tier nurse so that she will stay?

A salary that reflects her training. A career path that does not cap at a level she has already cleared. A workplace that does not require her to subsidize the hospital with unpaid overtime. A retirement plan she can actually retire on. A health system she does not have to fix with her own paycheck when her parents get sick. A safe neighborhood. A working public school for her children.

If the country cannot offer those baseline conditions, the nurse leaves. That is not a failure of patriotism. It is a market signal.

The 1987 Constitution, Article XIII, Section 3, names the obligation in plain language:

> "The State shall afford full protection to labor, local and overseas, organized and unorganized, and promote full employment and equality of employment opportunities for all."

Full protection of labor includes the protection of working conditions that make staying viable. Promotion of full employment includes the conditions under which a trained Filipino does not have to apply for a visa to use her training. The constitutional language is clear. The structural follow-through has been thin.

Other countries that hire Filipinos are not stealing them. They are simply offering what the Filipino state has not. Better pay. Functional public services. Predictable career growth. Clean institutions to work inside.

In a competitive global labor market, that arrangement is exactly how it works. If a country wants to keep its talent, it has to compete for it. Pricing in the labor market does not care about flag colors.

This means the standard narrative needs to be rewritten.

Brain drain is not a moral indictment of the people leaving. It is a diagnostic indicator of the country they are leaving. It is the country's own report card, written by the people who used to work inside it.

The country can argue with the report card. Or it can read it carefully.

The reading goes like this. If a generation of trained Filipinos is willing to live thousands of kilometres away from their families to earn a wage and conditions that make the move worthwhile, the local labor market has been broken for long enough that talent now has a default exit route. That is not a worker problem. It is an employer-of-last-resort problem, where the largest employer in the equation is the country itself.

Filipino workers are not the problem. Filipino workers in the right conditions are demonstrably world-class. The problem is the local conditions that produce no other rational option except leaving.

No country is perfect at retaining its talent. New Zealand also loses people to Australia. Vietnam loses skilled workers to Korea and Japan. Even Singapore quietly worries about its top professionals being recruited offshore. Retention is a permanent struggle for every modern state.

But there is a floor. The floor is: the country gets to make the case to its own people. Pay. Conditions. Career path. Health. Schools. Safety. Functional institutions. When the case is good, more people stay. When the case is bad, more people leave.

The Philippines has been losing the case for decades.

The day the country starts winning the case back, the diaspora will be the first to notice.

Hindi naman lalayo ang Pilipino kung may dahilan lang siyang manatili.
