A Senate hearing in the Philippines starts with the same three signals every time.

Bright lights. A pre-positioned camera. A senator already framed for the clip that will go viral by lunchtime.

What it almost never starts with is a draft bill.

The 1987 Constitution, Article VI, Section 21, is unambiguous about why hearings exist:

> "The Senate or the House of Representatives or any of its respective committees may conduct inquiries in aid of legislation in accordance with its duly published rules of procedure."

In aid of legislation.

Not in aid of ratings. Not in aid of personal brand-building. Not in aid of next year's senatorial campaign. The constitutional authority to summon citizens, demand documents, place witnesses under oath, and televise the entire process exists because legislators are supposed to be writing or amending laws.

What the country actually watches, week after week, is something else.

A witness is summoned. The hearing opens with prepared statements that read like opening monologues. A senator delivers a passionate speech that lands the clip on TikTok before the witness has answered the second question. A second senator counters with a different prepared monologue. Cross-examination becomes cross-performance. The witness cries, or the senator weeps, or a third party is dragged in for color.

The hearing ends. The chamber clears. Cameras pack up.

Then nothing happens.

The "follow-up hearing" never quite arrives. The bill that was supposed to result from the inquiry never quite materializes. Or it materializes, sits at committee level, and dies on the next adjournment. The witness goes home. The country's attention has already moved to the next televised episode.

Across many sessions, the volume of televised inquiries in the Philippine Senate has consistently outpaced the legislative output traceable to them. That is not a complaint about the camera. It is a complaint about purpose.

Compare this to how legislative inquiries function in countries where the institution still acts like an institution.

In New Zealand, parliamentary select committees take submissions, hold hearings, and the hearings are usually a structured input into a specific bill at a specific stage. Submitters know which bill they are addressing. Members know which clause they are scrutinizing. The press attends, but the press is covering legislation, not theater.

In the United Kingdom, select committee inquiries produce written reports that the government must respond to. The output is documentary, not viral.

In Singapore, parliamentary questions and select committee hearings are tightly tied to specific legislation or estimates of expenditure. The cameras matter. The cameras are not the point.

None of those systems is perfect. UK committees can be partisan. NZ submissions can be procedural. Singapore's parliament is small enough that scrutiny depends heavily on a thin opposition. No country runs the cleanest legislative scrutiny.

But the floor is different. The floor is: the hearing produces something on paper that moves the law.

In the Philippines, the hearing is too often the product itself. The clip is the deliverable. The trending hashtag is the receipt. The bill, the law, the regulation, the institutional reform, all of these are afterthoughts at best.

This is what "in aid of legislation" has been quietly drained of meaning to allow.

The country deserves to know what bill any given hearing is supposedly assisting. The country deserves to see the draft language six weeks after the hearing closed. The country deserves a measurable conversion rate between hours of televised hearing and pages of enacted law. Right now, that rate is unflattering in a way that would embarrass any working parliament in the region.

A Senate is not a studio. A hearing is not a season finale. The Constitution wrote the difference into Article VI, Section 21. It is past time the institution remembered.

Hindi telenovela ang pamamahala. Trabaho 'yan.
