In the Philippines, hiring a fifth employee is harder than hiring the fifteenth.

That is not a typo.

It is the structural result of running a labor code written for big employers and applying it word-for-word to a five-person team.

Look at what a small Filipino business owner has to absorb the moment a hire goes from "freelancer" to "employee."

SSS contributions. PhilHealth contributions. Pag-IBIG contributions. Mandatory 13th month pay. Service incentive leave. Holiday pay and premium pay rules. Statutory regularization at six months of continuous service. Separation pay obligations. Documentation requirements. DOLE reporting. Withholding tax administration. The cost is not only money. It is the time, the bookkeeper, the lawyer on retainer, the careful tracking of every working hour.

For a multinational with a payroll department, this is friction the system already absorbs. For a sari-sari that just grew into a small carinderia chain, it is a wall.

The PH MSME share of total employment is approximately 63 percent. Around 99.5 percent of registered enterprises in the country are micro, small, or medium. Most Filipino jobs are inside small businesses. The labor framework was not built for them. It was built for the formal large employer of a different era.

That mismatch produces a predictable outcome. SMEs avoid hiring formally. They hire "freelancers," "project-based contractors," or relatives. They keep teams artificially small. They underreport. They under-protect the very workers the Labor Code was designed to protect. Not because the SME owner is hostile to workers. Because the system did not present a path to formal hiring that a five-person team could actually afford.

This is the unintended effect that does not show up in committee hearings: a labor regime so heavy at the small end that it pushes the small end into informality, which then pushes the worker out of the protection the regime was meant to extend in the first place.

The 1987 Constitution, Article XIII, Section 3, names the obligation:

> "The State shall afford full protection to labor, local and overseas, organized and unorganized, and promote full employment and equality of employment opportunities for all."

Full protection of labor includes the labor that is not on a payroll because no one could afford the payroll. Full employment includes the hires that would happen if the framework let them. The constitutional language is broad. The implementation has missed entire categories of workers.

Look at how other countries tiered their labor regulation.

New Zealand has a minimum employment standards baseline that applies universally, but the compliance burden scales differently for small employers, with simpler ACC arrangements, KiwiSaver auto-enrollment, and tax handled cleanly through IRD. Hiring an employee in NZ does not require a separate compliance team.

Singapore runs a tiered employment law structure where the Employment Act distinguishes between different categories of workers, with smaller employers operating under simplified obligations for non-managerial staff.

Australia's Fair Work system has carved out small-business specific provisions, including a Small Business Fair Dismissal Code that gives small employers a workable framework without stripping worker protection.

None of those systems is perfect. Critics in NZ argue ACC has gaps. Singapore's Employment Act has been called restrictive by both sides. Australia's small-business code has been litigated more than once. No country has cleanly solved this.

But the floor is different. The floor is: the country has recognized that a 4-person team and a 4,000-person company should not face an identical compliance shape, and has written law that reflects the recognition.

In the Philippines, that recognition has been talked about for decades and built into legislation in a meaningfully complete form not yet. SMEs keep being asked to be small versions of large employers. Workers in SMEs keep being asked to accept informal arrangements because the formal ones are unreachable.

A tiered labor regime is not deregulation. It is calibration. Worker protection at full strength for the categories that have always had it. A genuinely workable on-ramp for the 5-person team that could hire formally if the wall came down.

The country has 1.2 million MSMEs and around two-thirds of its workers inside them. The next labor reform that actually fits that distribution is overdue.

Hindi lahat ng SME ay kayang gumalaw na parang malaking korporasyon. Hindi rin dapat sila gawin niyan.
