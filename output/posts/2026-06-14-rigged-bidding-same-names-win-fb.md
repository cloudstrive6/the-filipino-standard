Pull the public award histories for Philippine government contracts over the last ten years.

A handful of contractor names appear again and again.

That is not always a scandal. Sometimes it is competence. Often, it is something the procurement system was supposed to prevent.

Republic Act 9184, the Government Procurement Reform Act, was passed in 2003 to put discipline into how the country buys things with public money. The headline rule of the law is "lowest calculated responsive bid." Strip away the jargon and it means: the contract should go to the bidder who is cheapest, qualified, and responsive to the project's actual requirements.

In principle, that is a clean rule. In practice, the screen that decides who even gets to bid often does most of the work before the bid envelopes are opened.

Pre-qualification under RA 9184 requires bidders to demonstrate financial capacity, technical track record, completed similar projects, manpower, equipment, sometimes performance bonds. These requirements exist for good reasons. The country does not want a fly-by-night firm building a bridge. But the moment those requirements are calibrated tightly to projects that have just been completed by a specific small set of firms, the field for the next round of bidding has been narrowed before anyone has typed a peso amount.

This is how "open bidding" can produce closed outcomes.

The Commission on Audit has flagged, across multiple agencies and multiple administrations, patterns where the same companies win disproportionate shares of contracts in the same agency, with similar bid variances, similar timelines, and similar end-of-project issues. The technical term used in audit reports varies. The pattern is the same.

PhilGEPS, the public procurement portal, posts award histories publicly. Anyone with a few hours and a working internet connection can pull the data, sort by awarding entity, sort by winning supplier, and see the concentration. The receipts are not hidden. The country has just stopped looking at them carefully.

The 1987 Constitution, Article XII, Section 19, is explicit:

> "The State shall regulate or prohibit monopolies when the public interest so requires. No combinations in restraint of trade or unfair competition shall be allowed."

A bidding environment where the same small set of names wins repeatedly, by margins that mathematically suggest coordination rather than competition, is precisely what the constitutional clause was written to address. It does not have to be a literal monopoly. It only has to be a combination in restraint of trade. The clause is structural.

The country has the Philippine Competition Commission. It has COA. It has the Office of the Ombudsman. The institutional infrastructure to detect and act on rigged bidding exists on paper. What it has lacked, across decades, is the political will to follow concentration patterns from the data into the legal consequences the law actually permits.

Compare to how procurement is policed in countries where the rules have teeth.

In the United States, the False Claims Act allows whistleblowers to file suit on behalf of the government against contractors that defrauded public procurement. Settlements run into the billions of dollars annually.

In the European Union, public procurement directives mandate transparency requirements and remedies that competing bidders can pursue when they believe the process was unfair.

In Singapore, GeBIZ procurement records and aggressive corruption enforcement combine to produce both transparency and prosecution.

None of those systems is perfect. American False Claims cases drag on for years. EU procurement remedies are uneven across member states. Singapore's enforcement is partly enabled by political conditions other countries do not share. No country has solved this cleanly.

But the floor is different. The floor is: when concentration patterns become visible in the public data, somebody investigates and somebody publishes the result. The receipts move from observation into consequence.

In the Philippines, the receipts have mostly stayed on the receipt side.

The country deserves a procurement system where "lowest calculated responsive bid" is not a quiet euphemism for "the same names again." The data is public. The constitutional standard is named. The institutional capacity exists. What is missing is the political decision to use them.

Sino ba talaga ang patuloy na nananalo sa mga bidding na ito, at bakit hindi natin sila pinapatunog?
