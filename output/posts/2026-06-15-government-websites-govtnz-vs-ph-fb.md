A government website is the cheapest, fastest statement a state makes about how much it respects its citizens' time.

Open govt.nz, then open any random Philippine national agency website. The two tabs are speaking two different languages about citizenship.

govt.nz is a single entry point. The structure is task-based. "Pay your rates." "Apply for a passport." "Get help with childcare." A citizen lands on the page, clicks the task, and is routed to the agency that handles it. The agency pages themselves follow a similar visual grammar, similar typography, similar accessibility standards. Forms work on mobile. Forms work in browsers from five years ago. Forms tell you what they need before you start filling them.

Behind that is myIR, the Inland Revenue self-service portal. A tax filing that, for most workers, takes minutes from a phone. Payslip data already known to the system. Refund processed automatically. The citizen does not chase paperwork. The system shows the citizen what it already knows.

Now look at the Philippine experience.

The country has DICT. It has an E-Governance Master Plan. It has eGov.ph as the aspirational unified citizen portal. It has BIR eFPS, the LTO LTMS, GSIS online, SSS online, PhilHealth online, PhilSys for the national ID. Some of these are real improvements. The story is not that nothing has happened.

The story is that the average citizen experience across agencies is still wildly inconsistent.

One agency's website looks like it was redesigned last year. The next agency's looks like it was last updated when the country still had MRT line 7 as a fresh announcement. One form processes online cleanly. Another form requires the citizen to download a PDF, print it, sign it in three places, scan it back, then email it to an address that may or may not be monitored. A national ID system that the country has been promised for decades still produces a printed card with delays measured in years.

Type a common citizen task into a search bar and watch the results. Three agency pages with different navigation, two of them with broken sub-pages, one of them with a banner from a former secretary still featured on the homepage. Mobile responsiveness is partial. Accessibility for low-bandwidth connections is rarely a design priority. Search inside the agency site frequently fails.

That is the texture of citizenship online in 2026.

The 1987 Constitution, Article II, Section 24, names the obligation:

> "The State recognizes the vital role of communication and information in nation-building."

A government website is communication and information. The clause is broad. It is also fair to read it as an obligation: a state that recognizes the role of communication and information should put in the work to make its own digital surface usable. That has not consistently happened.

The cost of the gap is not abstract. A small business owner pays in time. A senior citizen pays in confusion. A diaspora Filipino trying to renew a passport from abroad pays in delays. A barangay-level worker trying to access her own records pays in commute hours. Every one of those costs is a tax of bad design that the country charges its own citizens silently.

No country is perfect. NZ's myIR has had outages. govt.nz redesigns have drawn user criticism. Singapore's gov.sg has had its own UX debates. Estonia's much-praised e-government has fielded security incidents. No system is clean.

But the floor is different. The floor in countries where digital citizenship works is that the average citizen experience across agencies is consistent, recent, mobile-first, and built around the citizen's task rather than the agency's org chart. The exception is the broken page. The default is the working one.

In the Philippines, the relationship is reversed. The exception is the working page. The default is the friction.

A country that wants to be taken seriously as a modern economy cannot keep treating its public digital infrastructure as somebody's discretionary side project. It has to be treated as core public works, with funding, talent, accountability, and a single coherent design system across agencies.

The country has the talent. Filipino designers and engineers build digital products that work for users in Singapore, San Francisco, and Sydney every day. What is missing is the political and institutional commitment to put that talent on the country's own front door and let it do the job.

Hindi naman tinitipid ng ibang bansa ang kanilang digital na pintuan. Tayo lang.
