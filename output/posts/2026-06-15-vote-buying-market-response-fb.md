Vote-buying in the Philippines is the wrong argument every single election cycle.

The conversation starts with morality. Filipinos selling their vote for 500 pesos. Filipinos selling their vote for 1,000 pesos. "Wala bang dignidad?" "Bakit ganun?" The frame collapses, every time, into "the Filipino voter is the problem."

That is not the right read. That is the read that protects the people who designed the market.

Look at vote-buying as the economic phenomenon it actually is.

In any functioning democracy, a voter exchanges a vote for an implicit contract. Vote for me, and I will deliver something measurable for your community: a working hospital, a road that does not flood, a school that does not run two shifts, a regulator that does its job. The vote has value because the contract is enforceable, even if loosely. The party has a platform. The platform is auditable. The next election is the renewal of contract.

That is not the market the Filipino voter walks into.

Philippine parties have been platform-light and personality-heavy for decades. Party-switching after elections is routine; a politician can win on one banner and govern under another by the next budget season. Patronage flows are personal, not programmatic. The connection between a campaign promise and a delivered service is, statistically, weak. The connection between a politician's name recognition and her vote share is, statistically, very strong.

In that market, what is the rational behavior of a voter who is poor, busy, and tired of being lied to?

The voter takes the 500 pesos. Or the 1,000 pesos. Or the 2,000 pesos. Because the cash is the only contract the politician has ever actually delivered on. It is the only contract the voter has any reasonable expectation will be honored. The check clears, today, in the hand, in actual pesos. Everything else the politician says is non-binding.

That is the structural answer to "bakit ganun." The country has produced an electoral market in which the cash transaction is more reliable than the policy transaction. Of course voters respond to the more reliable signal. They are not failing the democracy. The democracy has failed them, repeatedly, for long enough that this is what equilibrium now looks like.

The 1987 Philippine Constitution, Article V, Section 1, names what the system was supposed to be:

> "Suffrage may be exercised by all citizens of the Philippines not otherwise disqualified by law... The Congress shall provide a system for securing the secrecy and sanctity of the ballot as well as a system for absentee voting by qualified Filipinos abroad."

"Sanctity of the ballot." That phrase is doing a lot of work. Sanctity is not only about the privacy of the booth. It is also about the integrity of the process upstream of the booth. A ballot whose only honored contract is the cash before election day has not been kept sacred. It has been retailed.

Vote-buying is illegal under the Omnibus Election Code. The provisions are clear. Enforcement is rare. Prosecutions are rarer. That gap is not the voter's design. It is the institutional design that allows the market to clear at 500 pesos every cycle without consequence.

Other countries that have moved past widespread vote-buying did not do it through scolding voters. They did it through three structural moves at once. Strong, platform-based parties. Independent electoral commissions that prosecute. And, most importantly, governments that started delivering enough services for the policy contract to feel more reliable than the cash transaction.

When the policy contract becomes more reliable, the cash contract loses its edge.

No country is perfect at this. Vote-buying has surfaced in Malaysia, Indonesia, even in advanced democracies during specific cycles. The point is not that any system is clean. The point is that the structural levers are known. The Philippines has chosen not to pull most of them, consistently, across decades.

If the country is angry about vote-buying, the anger is misdirected when it lands only on the voter.

Direct the anger upstream. The parties that have never built a platform. The Congress that has never funded enforcement properly. The political families that benefit from a market priced in cash because it is cheaper than building actual delivery. The institutional design that has produced this equilibrium and refuses to touch it.

The Filipino voter is not selling democracy for 500 pesos. The Filipino voter is being offered 500 pesos by a system that has never offered her anything else worth voting for.

Hindi mura ang boto. Mura lang ang nag-aalok.
